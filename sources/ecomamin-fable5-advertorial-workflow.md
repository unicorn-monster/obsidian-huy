## why 90% of AI-generated advertorials fail

here's the mistake.

someone opens chatgpt or claude. types "write an advertorial for my magnesium supplement." gets back a page that leads with the product benefits, lists features, mentions clinical studies, and ends with a "buy now" CTA.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HNICFPtbEAAIDxz?format=png&name=large)

the copy is fine. grammatically correct. sounds professional. mentions all the right things.

and it bombs. 0.5% conversion rate. people bounce after 3 seconds.

why?

because the advertorial was written for someone who is product-aware and ready to buy. but the traffic hitting it is from a non-branded search query like "why do i keep waking up at 3am." that person is problem-aware at best. they don't know your product exists. they don't trust you. they're not shopping. they're researching.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HNIB9DgaEAEdf7S?format=png&name=large)

**the advertorial needs to meet them where they are.** and where they are is in the middle of a problem they're trying to understand.

the AI doesn't know this unless you tell it. and the way you tell it is the entire game.

## the framing instruction that changes everything

before i write a single word of the advertorial prompt, i give fable 5 one instruction that reframes the entire output:

"the person reading this page arrived from a google search query for \[exact keyword\]. they are problem-aware, not product-aware. they have never heard of this brand. they do not trust this page. your job is to validate their problem, agitate the unknown causes they haven't considered, introduce a mechanism (not a product) that addresses the root cause, then position this specific product as the best implementation of that mechanism. the tone is editorial, not commercial. the reader should feel like they're reading a health article, not a sales page."

this single instruction changes the entire architecture of the output. the advertorial stops leading with the product. starts leading with the reader's pain. and the conversion rate difference is massive.

without the framing instruction, AI produces a sales page disguised as an advertorial. with it, AI produces an education piece that sells by informing.

## the full fable 5 advertorial workflow

here's the exact process, step by step.

**step 1: keyword-to-awareness mapping**

before i touch fable 5, i look at the google keyword i'm targeting and determine the traffic's awareness level.

- "why do i wake up at 3am" = problem-aware. doesn't know the solution category exists
- "best magnesium for sleep" = solution-aware. knows the category, doesn't know the brand
- "brand X vs brand Y magnesium" = most-aware. comparing specific options

each awareness level needs a different advertorial structure. you don't use the same copy for someone who doesn't know magnesium helps sleep as you do for someone who's already comparing magnesium brands.

**step 2: the fable 5 prompt architecture**

i open fable 5 (claude(.ai), pro or max subscription gives you access) and structure my prompt in six blocks:

**block 1: traffic context** — "the reader arrived from a google search for \[keyword\]. their awareness level is \[problem-aware / solution-aware / most-aware\]. they have never visited this website before."

**block 2: brand and product context** — "the brand is \[name\]. the product is \[description\]. the key USP is \[mechanism\]. the price point is \[X\]. the primary differentiator vs competitors is \[Y\]. the target demographic is \[age, gender, lifestyle\]."

**block 3: advertorial structure** — follow this six-section structure:

- section 1: validate their struggle. acknowledge the specific pain behind the search query. make them feel seen
- section 2: explain the real cause. introduce the mechanism that most people don't know about. cite studies or expert logic
- section 3: introduce the solution category, not the product. show why previous solutions failed and introduce a new category that addresses the root cause
- section 4: introduce this specific product as the best implementation of that mechanism. explain how it works, the scientific backing, and the USPs
- section 5: social proof. testimonials, reviews, before/afters, data points
- section 6: soft CTA with a risk reversal. not aggressive. educational tone maintained

**block 4: tone and voice rules** — "the tone is editorial, not commercial. write as if this is a health/lifestyle article being published in a magazine, not a landing page. no hype words. no 'revolutionary' or 'game-changing.' the reader should feel educated, not sold. use simple language. short paragraphs. conversational rhythm."

**block 5: banned patterns** — "do not lead with the product. do not mention the brand name in the first 3 paragraphs. do not use exclamation marks. do not use the word 'imagine.' do not use generic transitions like 'but here's the thing.' do not use any language that makes this read like a facebook ad."

## the prompting mistakes that produce slop

**mistake 1: no awareness level in the prompt.** without telling fable 5 that the traffic is problem-aware, it defaults to writing for a solution-aware audience. the output leads with the product, not the problem. and cold traffic bounces.

**mistake 2: telling it to "write an advertorial" without structural constraints.** fable 5 is an insanely capable model. the most advanced available right now. but "write an advertorial" gives it too much freedom. it'll produce something competent but generic. the six-section structure forces it into the specific architecture that converts on google.

**mistake 3: not banning commercial language.** by default, AI models lean toward marketing language. "discover," "transform," "unlock." these words trigger ad-blindness. the reader's brain recognises commercial intent and shuts down. banning these words forces fable 5 to write in a more editorial, journalistic tone that bypasses the reader's sales filter.

**mistake 4: not specifying the search query.** the query is the context. it tells fable 5 what the reader was thinking 3 seconds before they landed on this page. "why do i keep waking up at 3am" is a completely different mental state than "best sleep supplement." the prompt needs to reflect that.

## the revision loop (this is where fable 5 earns its keep)

the first draft from fable 5 is 80% there. but the revision loop is what separates a good advertorial from one that prints money.

**revision 1: hook audit** — i paste the first draft back and ask: "read the first 3 sentences. if someone arrived from the google search query \[keyword\], would they keep reading or bounce? if bounce, rewrite the opening to match their exact mental state when they typed that query."

fable 5 is better at self-correction than any previous model. it catches weak hooks, generic openings, and mismatched tone. this revision alone usually doubles the quality of the opening.

**revision 2: mechanism clarity** — "is the mechanism explained clearly enough that a 35-year-old with no scientific background would understand it AND be convinced that it's different from what they've tried before? if not, simplify and add one concrete example or analogy."

**revision 3: CTA audit** — "does the CTA feel earned by the education above it, or does it feel bolted on? rewrite if it feels like a pitch rather than a natural next step."

three revisions. 10-15 minutes total. and the output is stronger than what i used to produce in 4-6 hours manually.

## the angle multiplication system

here's where this workflow becomes a scaling weapon.

each angle gets its own advertorial. same product. same mechanism. different entry point. different keyword. different hook.
