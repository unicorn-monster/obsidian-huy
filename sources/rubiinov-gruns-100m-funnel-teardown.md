## 900 ads running simultaneously: why creative volume is the variable

grüns has over 900 active meta ads at any given time.

the typical $30k to $80k/month supplement brand has between 5 and 20.

i've talked about creative velocity in the context of AI UGC production. grüns is doing the same thing with a traditional UGC-plus-static-plus-AI mix. the output is the same: more assets in market means more data, faster, which means faster identification of winning angles.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HMfe9X_a0AA8TWi?format=png&name=large)

their ad architecture mixes three categories:

- UGC: the highest volume. real-looking creators listing benefits in a conversational, almost stream-of-consciousness way. the tone is casual to the point of feeling unrehearsed.
- static ads: simple, fast to produce. easy to iterate. used for testing angles at low cost before committing production budget to video.
- AI-generated creative: used for scale and variation. not hiding that it's AI. just using it as one more production mechanism in the mix.

the principle is the same whether you're running 900 ads or 40: more creative in market means faster signal. the brands that find winning angles in 30 days instead of 90 days have a compounding advantage, and it comes from production velocity, not budget

if you're running fewer than 20 new creative assets per month on a supplement brand doing [$30k+](https://x.com/search?q=%2430k%2B&src=cashtag_click) in monthly revenue, you're moving slower than the market requires.

## the landing page architecture: message match at the angle level

grüns doesn't send all traffic to their homepage.

they run a primary landing page for general traffic, and a series of listicles for specific angle-matched traffic.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HMfe_D9boAAmhiD?format=png&name=large)

their listicles:

- nutrition-based angle: "seven benefits users love about grüns"
- weight loss angle: landing page built around weight loss language
- kids angle: parents buying for their kids
- protein angle: for fitness-focused buyers
- fiber angle: for gut health buyers

each ad focused on a specific angle drives traffic to the landing page built for that exact audience and that exact language.

this is the same principle AG1 uses. the ad buys the click. the landing page is supposed to close it. if the landing page speaks a different language than the ad, the emotional momentum breaks and the buyer leaves without converting.

the brands doing [$200k+](https://x.com/search?q=%24200k%2B&src=cashtag_click) per month on meta have this figured out. the brands doing $30k/month usually don't. they're sending all their traffic to one page and wondering why their conversion rate is flat regardless of how good their creative gets.

## the pop-up: the highest-leverage piece of their entire funnel

this is the part of grüns' funnel that gets overlooked, because it's not as visible as the ads or the landing pages.

grüns runs a full-page quiz-style pop-up form that converts at 15 to 20%.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HMffBBFa8AEkghg?format=jpg&name=large)

the industry average pop-up converts at 3 to 4%.

that gap is not an exaggeration. it's the difference between collecting 15 email subscribers per 100 visitors and collecting 60. at scale, that difference compounds into an email list that's four times larger from the same traffic volume, which means four times the LTV potential from every dollar of paid acquisition.

how they get to 15 to 20% opt-in:

the offer is genuinely aggressive. 52% off is not a normal supplement discount. it reads as a pattern interrupt. the specificity (52%, not 50%) makes it feel more real than a round number discount would.

the first step is a button click, not a form fill. you're not asked for your email immediately. you're asked to select your primary health concern with a single button press. this is the commitment mechanic. by engaging with the first step, you've committed to wanting the outcome. you're now more likely to complete the subsequent steps than someone who was taken directly to an email input field.

the framing is "you've got 52% off," not "get 52% off." the distinction is loss aversion psychology. "you've got" means you already possess something. not completing the pop-up means losing it. "get" means you have nothing and can gain something. humans experience the pain of loss approximately twice as intensely as the pleasure of equivalent gain. the framing doubles the psychological urgency of completing the form without changing the offer at all.

each step asks for one thing only. email only, then phone number separately. not combined. the cognitive load of giving two pieces of information simultaneously is higher than giving each one in sequence. lower friction at each step means higher completion rates across the full flow.

the result: for every 100 people who land on their site, grüns captures 15 to 20 email addresses and phone numbers. a standard pop-up captures 3 to 4. the email list is where 30 to 50% of supplement brand revenue lives long-term.

the pop-up is how you build it.

## the email strategy: entertain first, sell second

grüns sends three to four email campaigns per week.

plenty of brands at the $100M/year level do this. what sets grüns apart is the format, not the frequency. it's the format.

their emails are built to entertain, not promote. and the formats are designed around one insight: people skim marketing emails instead of reading them. so the email has to communicate its message to someone who's spending 8 seconds, not 2 minutes.

how they do it:

infographics over copy blocks. a single infographic showing five key ingredients with icons and one-line descriptions communicates the same information as a 300-word paragraph. it communicates it faster, more memorably, and with higher perceived production value. the viewer can extract value in a skim pass.

big buttons, above the fold. the CTA is visible without scrolling. if the button is at the bottom of the email, only the percentage of readers who scroll to the bottom can click it. if it's at the top, everyone who opens the email can click it.

bold text and color changes within copy. specific phrases are bolded or colored differently. this creates a skim path: the reader's eye moves to the high-contrast elements and constructs the gist of the email from those alone.

consistent offer anchor. the 52% offer appears in virtually every email. not as the primary message, but as the persistent context. the familiarity of the offer reduces the cognitive effort of deciding whether to buy.

## the SMS strategy: built purely for conversion

grüns runs an aggressive SMS welcome flow alongside their email welcome flow.

the key distinction from their email strategy is the purpose of the channel.

email can carry education. SMS cannot. 160 characters per credit. grüns uses SMS for one thing only: driving immediate conversion

the SMS flow is high-frequency in the first 72 hours after opt-in and simple in its messaging. the offer. the code. the CTA. nothing else.

this works because SMS has a 98% open rate and no spam folder. it reaches people at the moment they see it, which is usually within minutes of receiving it. the window for action is immediate in a way that email isn't.

for supplement brands right now: if you don't have an SMS welcome flow, you're not capturing the conversion potential of your email opt-ins. the first 24 hours after someone joins your list is the highest-intent window you'll ever have with that contact. SMS is the channel that reaches them in that window.
