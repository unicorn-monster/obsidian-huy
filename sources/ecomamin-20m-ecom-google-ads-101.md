Overview "101" doc — $20M+ spend across 40+ brands. Mostly restates ground already deep in [[google-ads]]/[[google-shopping]] (feed-first priority, PMax rules, branded-cannibalization, campaign phases). Notes below capture the genuinely new specific mechanics/numbers.

## 5 foundational principles (framing, mostly not new)
Buying intent not creating it (Google=capture vs Meta=generate). Campaign structure IS the strategy (where budget goes = who you acquire). 95/5 rule (95% revenue from 5% of keywords/products/optimizations — find winners fast, concentrate). Feed quality > campaign optimization (feed determines eligibility/display/quality-score/trust; campaigns only control bidding). Signal quality > budget size (more spend into bad signals = waste; conversion data + feed + landing-page relevance + negatives + right bid-strategy = what actually compounds).

## Quality Score mechanics (concrete formula + example — genuinely useful reference)
`Ad Rank = Bid × Quality Score`. Worked example: competitor bids $3 at QS 4 → rank 12; you bid $2 at QS 7 → rank 14 — you outrank them AND pay ~33% less. Levers to raise QS: exact landing-page/keyword match, keyword present in ad headline, fast page load, strong CTR (ad copy quality), strong conversion rate.

## Geographic arbitrage BY CONVERSION RATE (not just CPC)
Export the location report, calculate conversion rate by city/state (not just cost), bid up specifically where CVR is provably higher. Example cited: supplements brand — CA 2.1% CVR, TX 3.4%, FL 2.8% → bid TX +40%, FL +20% → 15% ROAS improvement on the SAME total budget, purely from reallocating existing spend toward where it already converts better.

## Dayparting bid schedule (concrete reference bands)
Midnight-6am: lowest conversion → bid -50%. 6am-10am: medium → 0%. 10am-6pm: high → +20%. 6pm-11pm: highest → +40%. Claimed 15-20% ROAS improvement from eliminating off-hour waste — treat the exact bands as a starting hypothesis to verify against your own account's hourly data, not a universal rule.

## Device bid adjustment
Mobile converts 30-50% worse than desktop for most ecom → desktop baseline, mobile -30%, tablet -20% as a starting adjustment; flagged exception for impulse-purchase/mobile-first categories where this could reverse.

## Margin-based campaign segmentation via supplemental feed
Tag products by margin tier via supplemental feed (doesn't touch source feed) — high margin (70%+): aggressive bidding, target 3x ROAS. Medium (50-70%): moderate, target 4x. Low (<50%): conservative, target 6x. Point: optimize for PROFIT not blended revenue — a low-margin product needs a much higher ROAS bar to be worth the same ad dollar as a high-margin one.

## Niche-specific what-matters/doesn't-matter table
Supplements: benefit-positioning + subscription LTV economics + 3rd-party-testing badges + specific-ingredient differentiation matter; brand story and packaging aesthetics mostly don't (invisible in Shopping). → problem-aware search + quiz funnels. Apparel: lifestyle imagery + style descriptors in title + size-inclusivity mentions + fabric detail matter; technical specs mostly don't (unless technical apparel). → Shopping + display retargeting. Home goods: exact dimensions in title + room-specific targeting + material-quality signals + assembly clarity matter; brand story mostly doesn't (unless established). → Shopping + comparison pages. Beauty: specific ingredients in title + dermatologist-testing mentions + skin-type targeting + before/after proof matter; luxury positioning mostly doesn't (unless $100+ tier). → problem-aware search + YouTube video. High-ticket ($500+): financing options (Afterpay/Klarna) + warranty length + expert endorsement + detailed vs-alternatives comparison matter; urgency tactics mostly don't (people don't impulse-buy $1000 items). → retargeting-heavy + long conversion windows.

## Scaling-ceiling concept (vertical scaling discipline)
Every campaign has a spend ceiling where ROAS starts breaking — the framing is explicit: find where efficiency starts declining (e.g. $500/day=6x → $1,000/day=4.5x → $1,500/day=3x) and treat the LAST profitable step ($1,000/day in that example) as the max efficient spend for that specific campaign, not a floor to push past. Scale to the threshold, not through it — when scaling stalls, the fix is usually adding new campaigns/products/markets (horizontal), not forcing more budget into an already-saturated one (vertical).

## New-product-injection as an unstall lever
When an account plateaus, the fix cited is adding new PRODUCTS (new keyword surface = new inventory to sell within the same account/campaigns) rather than pushing more budget into saturated keywords — cited example: account stuck ~$200k/mo for months, added 3 products, $350k/mo within 60 days on the same campaign structure.

## Realistic timeline benchmark (directional)
Month 1-2 ($3-8k/mo spend): 2-4x ROAS, foundation/data-collection. Month 3-4 ($8-15k/mo): 3-6x, kill losers/scale winners. Month 5-6 ($15-30k/mo): 4-7x, horizontal expansion. Month 7-12 ($30-100k+/mo): 4-8x blended, new markets/products. Explicit prerequisite framing: this assumes ≥40% gross margin, real search demand, <8% refund rate, and a 90+ day commitment at ≥$5-10k/mo — thin-margin dropshipping, novelty-no-search-demand products, poor-quality/high-refund products, and "$500 test budgets" are called out as situations where the channel structurally doesn't work regardless of execution.

## Attribution-gap framing (same thesis as other docs, worth the number)
Cited case: platform-reported 2.4x ROAS in Google Ads alone; third-party attribution (Hyros) showed true ROAS 3.8x once assisted conversions (YouTube-view → later branded search → purchase) were counted — used to argue for scaling more aggressively once the real number is known, not just the platform-native one.
