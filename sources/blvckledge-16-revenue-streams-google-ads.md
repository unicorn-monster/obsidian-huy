**Revenue Stream 1: Branded Search**

this is people searching for your brand name or specific product names you sell.

example:

"\[your brand name\]" “\[your brand name\] + \[product category\]” "\[your brand\] reviews" "\[your product name\]"

this is the easiest money you'll make on google

it also helps to protect your brand.

without it, someone can search for your brand, see an ad from a competitor, and buy from them instead.

usually 5-10+ ROAS with cpcs sitting under $0.3-0.4 (although it'll vary depending on the brand)

the goal is to hold the top position on those searches at the lowest cpc you can get away with

**Revenue Stream 2: Branded Shopping**

same buyer intent as branded search, just through shopping placements instead.

this is a bit trickier because shopping doesn’t target keywords directly

and brands tend to run these very inefficiently

i regularly see prospecting campaigns pulling in branded traffic without people realizing it.

as a result, they end up overpaying a lot for this traffic, sometimes 5-10x more than necessary.

the goal here is to push branded searches into a dedicated standard shopping campaign.

to do that, we use a tiered structure.

prospecting campaigns get higher priority with branded terms added as negatives.

branded campaigns run at low priority with a feed optimized for branded traffic.

the way it works is:

let's say someone searches "\[your brand\] shoes"

google starts by checking your higher priority campaigns (prospecting)

but branded searches are excluded there, so they don't enter the auction.

the search then gets passed to your low priority campaigns (branded)

and that's where the ad gets served.

i'd also run a prospecting keyword script against your branded campaign.

that way, any non-brand search terms that sneak in get identified and excluded.

**Revenue Stream 3: Product category keywords on Search**

these are shoppers that know what product type they want but haven't decided on a brand yet.

examples:

"buy running shoes" "resistance bands" "purchase protein powder".

these are active buyers with high purchase intent. for ecom, it should be the bread and butter of your prospecting efforts

the only challenge is that this is often competitive

pretty much every brand in your category will bid for these keywords, which drive cpcs up

a simple way we use to improve cpa is by testing different landing page formats.

at the same time, we reinforce the target keywords across the account. from the ads to the lps, etc.

the more relevant everything is, the better your ad position and the less you tend to pay for it.

standard product pages are the safest starting point. but you can often get better performance with:

\- sales pages - keyword theme pages - 3rd party comparison pages

[for a full guide on how to run these pages, check out our landing page playbook.](https://x.com/blvckledge/status/2040000095928910266)

**Revenue Stream 4: Product category keywords on Shopping**

this shows up in the same searches as the previous one, but on shopping

there are a few things i’ve seen brands mess up here:

the first is failing to exclude branded traffic correctly.

when that happens, the campaign ends up relying on branded searches and struggles to scale prospecting.

the second is that the product titles aren’t optimized for prospecting

many brands defaulted to putting the brand name first like this:

“\[Brand\] Premium Ergonomic Office Chair with Lumbar Support, Black, Model X3”

this is terrible for prospecting because

\> google treats \[Brand\] as the strongest signal and leans harder into branded traffic

\> the shopper sees \[Brand\] first, which does nothing for prospecting since they don’t know or care about your brand

as a result, you’ll have few non-branded search terms come through (or at least, nowhere near what you could have had)

instead, lead with the product attributes people are searching for.

the order we follow is

\- primary product attribute (what people search for) - secondary differentiator like material, use case, color, gender, size, or other key features - size or dimensions if that’s what people look for (this depends on the product. for some products, size matters more. for others, color or other attributes are more important) - brand name

the third issue is budget allocation.

in shopping, particularly through pmax campaigns, weaker products can absorb a big amount of budget while stronger products stay capped.

this is very common when you have a big catalog and everything is in the same campaign.

split products into high-value and low-value groups.

then, once enough data is available, move the low-value products into dedicated campaigns.

that gives you better control over:

\- targets - testing - budgets - scaling decisions - category-level performance - pushing certain products harder than others

**Revenue Stream 5: Long-tail searches on Shopping**

normally, you get one feed per product to match what someone's searching for

and that creates a big problem.

**you can't optimize for multiple search intents**

the thing is, the majority of products serve more than one type of buyer.

take magnesium supplements:

"magnesium for sleep" → person dealing with insomnia "magnesium for anxiety" → person managing stress "magnesium for muscle recovery" → athlete preventing cramps "magnesium for kids" → parent looking for children's supplement

so what do brands typically do?

they optimize for the highest-volume keyword: "magnesium supplement"

and by doing that, you essentially opted out of other search intents.

your ads can still show up, but since the relevance is low…

you're often paying more than necessary to win clicks and conversions from those audiences.

google's algorithm puts a lot of weight on relevance.

it wants to show people the products and ads most likely to match what they're looking for.

click-through rate is important as well. if your creative gets more clicks than other options in the auction, that's usually a positive signal.

when someone searches "magnesium for anxiety" and your title says "premium magnesium supplement for sleep support,"

google sees:

\- partial keyword match - moderate relevance signal - lower likelihood of click

your listing gets deprioritized. or you'll end up paying more for the same position.

I’m not saying that you have zero chance of showing up for "magnesium for anxiety" or "magnesium for muscle recovery"

but your odds drop significantly.

and any brand that built their feed specifically around those terms will consistently outrank you.

in other words, you’ll be missing out on a big chunk of traffic from keywords with different use cases or demographics.

the solution is product duplication.

the main idea is:

you create multiple versions of the same product, each focused on a specific search intent or use case.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HMNjhMla4AAjfky?format=jpg&name=large)

google treats them as separate listings and ranks each one for its specific quer

and often shows multiple variations from your brand in the same search result.

if you want the complete breakdown with everything you need to execute it, [check it out here.](https://x.com/blvckledge/status/2032087432301310209)

one important note here:

product duplication works best when it's done selectively.

you shouldn’t create a duplicate for every keyword you find.

we'll often start by creating a few versions built around the top-performing keywords.

the strongest keyword goes at the front of the title, with additional keywords worked into the rest of the feed.

from there, we look at the data and decide whether it's worth expanding further.

creating too many duplicates at once can have the opposite effect, especially if the campaign doesn't have enough budget or conversion volume.

**Revenue Stream 6: Comparison keywords on Search**

these are buyers who know the type of product they want

they just haven’t decided which brand deserves their money yet

these show up in searches like:

"best collagen powder" "top \[product\] 2026" "highest rated \[product\]"

the volume is solid and the intent is strong. it works especially well if you operate in a crowded category with recognizable brands

for landing pages, you can start with a product page to validate demand.

but this traffic usually performs best with a 3rd party comparison page.

this is usually cheaper than product category keywords like “buy Nike Air Max.”

**Revenue Stream 7: Competitor keywords on Search**

these are buyers who are looking for a competitor brand

this can work well, but there’s one thing you should be aware of.

you're allowed to bid on competitor brand names, but you generally can't use registered trademarks in your ad copy.

example queries:

\- "\[competitor name\]" - "\[competitor\] reviews" - "best alternative to \[competitor\]"

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HMNjNniasAAYiWH?format=jpg&name=large)

buying intent is high, volume is decent.

2 ways to capture it on google

you can run a search campaign bidding on competitor brand names or hero products

or pmax campaigns using those terms as search themes, custom segments as audience signals & ad copy built around the competitor

this works especially well if you have competitors with weak brand protection.

they are pumping budgets to warm up their traffic on other platforms, driving more search volume for their brand

perfect time to capture that traffic with your offer

**Revenue Stream 8: Trending keywords on Search**

sometimes a topic spikes in search volume because of a news cycle, a viral moment, a cultural shift, or a seasonal pattern.

if your product connects to that topic, you can build a campaign around it and capture the wave while it's rising.

for example:

a supplement brand might see a spike around a specific ingredient after it goes viral on tiktok.

a crisis hits and they're searching for ways to save money,

the holidays roll around and everyone's looking for presents.

a skincare brand might see demand surge after a certain routine or technique blows up.

we recently made $85k for a brand by catching one of those windows

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HMNjKn_bcAAEDZ1?format=jpg&name=large)

these short demand spikes can be extremely profitable if you prepare correctly

and it’d be such a big missed opportunity to not fully take advantage of it.

**Revenue Stream 9: Informational keywords on Search**

these are searches tied to a problem or desired outcome

\- "what is the carnivore diet" - "what causes bloating" - "best way to get more protein"

a lot of ecommerce brands dismiss these as low-intent traffic.

and yet… they have 5-10x the search volume and a lot less competition than bottom funnel terms.

they cost a fraction of what bottom funnel terms do.

people searching them have a problem and we're showing up with the answer in our ads.

we're helping clients go after keywords in the 50k to 250k monthly search range, paying under $0.07 cpc

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HMNjHmyaYAAKv3Z?format=png&name=large)

the reason i put it lower on this list is that it doesn't fit every category.

if you're selling t-shirts, for example, it's much harder to make it work.

that said, it applies to far more categories than many brands think.

one of our clients sells ties and they're generating solid sales from searches like "how to tie a tie."

also keep in mind that…

you need a strong content page that matches what people are searching for, a clear path from education to your product, fast page speed, and an easy path to purchase.

otherwise, you might get cheap clicks, but they won't turn into profitable customers.

**Revenue Stream 10: Offer Image Ads on Discover**

if you're not familiar with image ads on google...

they're pretty similar to native ads. you've got 3 core elements: the image, the headline, and the description. sometimes just the image on its own.

we've seen some really strong results running these on Discover.

a lot of our clients are spending [$10k-](https://x.com/search?q=%2410k-&src=cashtag_click)$20k per day here profitably.

and when it comes to image ads, offer-focused creatives are the easiest place to start.

the image pulls attention and drives the click, and everything in the creative is built around communicating the deal.

3 ways you can run this:

\> single product: one product on a clean background with a clear offer headline and a short urgency message underneath

\> multi-panel collection: same concept, but showing multiple products, variations, or colorways in a single image

\> there are also banners and carousels

**Revenue Stream 11: Problem-Aware/Educational Image Ads Image Ads on Discover**

this angle is built around a problem your audience is dealing with or an outcome they're actively trying to reach.

the ad is framed in a way that makes the viewer feel like they're about to learn something useful.

a headline here might look like:

"Doctors Explain the Hidden Reason Why Most Knee Pain Treatments Don't Work"

**Revenue Stream 12: Third-Party Recommendation/“Top X Products” Image Ads**

for this angle, you position your ad as a trusted third-party recommendation

a headline here might look like:

"Physical Therapists Are Calling These 6 Massagers a Game-Changer for Mobility After 40"

**Revenue Stream 13: YouTube Shorts Ads**

these are vertical video ads that run inside the youtube shorts feed.

the user behavior here is similar to tiktok and instagram reels. they want fast, entertaining, organic creatives.

a lot of users won’t even notice that it’s an ad, as long as you lean into organic-style content.

expect around 8 seconds of average watch time in this format.

a few notes on execution:

\- your hook has to land in one second. use ugc, contrarian statements, relatable claims to hold attention here - keep key visuals and copy in the upper frame, youtube's ui covers the bottom third- many users watch without sound so captions and text overlays go a long way

this is where many of our clients start when they come to youtube.

you can take your best performing meta creatives and test them here first.

**Revenue Stream 14: YouTube In-stream Ads**

these are long-form video ads that play before or during a video.

there’s no supporting images or copy.

in many cases, there’s no existing demand for the product and brand awareness is low… so the video has to do all the selling.

viewer can skip after 5 seconds, so those first seconds are everything.

you'll typically see around 10 seconds of average watch time here.

important notes on execution:

\- shoot landscape - open by calling out your customer's specific problem in the first 3 seconds - depending on the angle, you'll sometimes want your usp, the product or brand on screen immediately - give them a concrete reason to leave youtube right now (exclusive offer, urgency, etc) - always use text on screen and voiceover

best way to approach this is to study high performing vsls in your niche and use their structure as a framework.

i’ve also seen a lot of brands pull solid results with ai generated ads in this format.

**Revenue Stream 15: YouTube In-feed Ads**

these are thumbnail + headline ads that sit inside the organic youtube browse feed and search results.

think muted video ads you scroll past while picking what to watch

or the ads sitting in the recommendations on the right side while a video is playing.

there’s no audio unless a user actively chooses to watch (which happens less than 1% of the time)

in this format, your competition isn’t just other ads

the user is scrolling thumbnails looking for something worth watching

which means you're up against creators in your niche for that attention

so you need to match the dopamine seeking intent of people browsing.

important notes:

\- audio is basically irrelevant, so your thumbnail and creative has to work without it - study what's performing organically in your niche and match that in your thumbnail - long copy is visible here. spell out who this is for and what they're getting - lead with attention grabbing visuals and always add text on top

**Revenue Stream 16: Lapsed customers & Retargeting campaigns**

here, you focus on getting more out of the traffic and customers you already have.

that could be people coming from google, meta, email, or any other channel.

on the remarketing side, you have things like:

\- website visitors - cart abandoners - email subscribers - customer lists from lead magnets - waitlists for an upcoming launch or sale

pmax usually automatically retarget a lot of these people already

so we usually start there and expand into demand gen as spend grows.

on the retention side, one of the easiest wins is targeting lapsed customers.

it's probably not the strategy people get excited about, but can be very profitable.

next, we have a few bonus revenue streams.

i'm putting these here because they're not tied to a specific placement, and your level of control is fairly limited.

**Bonus 1: Automated Placements**

when you run pmax, google automatically finds placements across search, shopping, youtube, discover, gmail, and display.

the majority of your budget should flow toward the placements covered above.

but sometimes, the algorithm finds pockets of traffic elsewhere that can work well.

apps, websites, search partners, gmail placements, etc

i’m putting this here because results vary massively from brand to brand.

the same applies to conversational searches through AI Max.

your ads can sometimes appear on searches like “find me a good running shoe for flat feet under $150, something with solid grip for rainy days”

or within AI-generated recommendations inside AI Mode, AI Overviews, and similar experiences.

google’s hyping this a lot, and long-term, they could become a meaningful source of revenue.

but today, ai max is still relatively early.

you can control things like cpc and budgets, but the quality side is a different story.

the search terms your ads show on aren't fully visible, you can exclude some but not all

and if you've got automatic text generation on, it often writes your copy in ways that aren't always the best.

**Bonus 2: Expanding to Another Avatar or Angle**

everything we've covered so far assumes you're targeting your primary buyer and your primary angle.

but for a lot of products…

you can reach a different set of buyers by reframing what it's for or what problem it solves.

for example, one of our clients sells resistance bands.

their core strategy was built around people searching for home workout products.

when we dug into the search trend data, we found an interesting pattern:

search volume for "pilates resistance bands" was growing.

the volume wasn't as large as the main category keywords.

but competition was much lower.

so we built dedicated campaigns with ad copy, images, and landing pages tailored to pilates.

we updated everything to better match what pilates buyers were looking for.

that opened up an entirely new segment of buyers and created a new revenue stream they weren't capturing before.

i used Search as the example here. but this goes way beyond that

just like Meta advertisers can attack the same product from multiple angles, avatars, and use cases, you can do the exact same thing on Google.

**Bonus 3: Expanding into Another Country**

this one is pretty self-explanatory.

every new country essentially unlocks all of the revenue streams we've covered above.

if you enter five countries, you've effectively created five opportunities to run the same playbook.

of course, that comes with a lot more complexity. in many ways, every new country is like managing another account.

but it can be one of the biggest growth levers available.

every brand should have the first 4 revenue streams dialed in first.
