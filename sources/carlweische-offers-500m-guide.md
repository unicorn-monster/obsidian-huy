I think there are too few good guides on offers in ecom seeing as it’s the #1 factor for scale.

So this is my view on offers, how to build one, & how to position it to scale to 8-9 figures:

What makes a good offer

For an offer to be “good”, it has to meet one criteria:

It has to sell profitably to completely cold traffic.

This is the backbone of all scaling…

If your offer can’t sell to cold traffic (unaware & problem aware), it doesn’t matter how good your ads & pre-sell pages & upsells & all the rest is, you WILL NOT profit off of cold traffic.

& if you can’t scale on cold traffic, you will not scale.

Warm audiences are simply too small & have too much competition for you to scale past 7-8 figures.

Now that you know the requirement of an offer, here’s how to build one:

## 1\. Build it on a mass-market desire

If you want to sell to a large variety of people (aka cold traffic) your offer needs to target pains/desires that everyone has.

These are the fundamental human desires, literally everyone has them:

- reproduction (being attractive, finding a partner)
- status (being seen as higher value than others)
- belonging (being accepted by a group)
- safety (avoiding pain, staying healthy, not dying)

So, your offer has to be positioned as a way to get one of these things.

For example: a fitness brand we worked with in the past sold a testosterone supplement.

Or rather they tried to sell it but it just wouldn’t scale.

So we repositioned it as “the way to get girls” (reproduction) & it went absolutely ballistic on ice cold traffic.

Always tie your product to one of these fundamental desires.

## 2\. Build a TON of perceived value

We do this for 3 reasons (I’ll soon explain exactly how to do this):

1. For people who have never heard of you before to buy from you, they need to see massive value in what they’re buying, even if your pre-sell pages & overall conversion systems are dialled
2. It’s a positioning move against competitors. You always want to lean into a premium feel with your brand since it separates you from anyone who tries to copy you. When you have this premium feel, it’s also easier for cold traffic to trust you
3. It lets you charge more, thereby spend more, & thereby scale more

This is also why I advice against heavy discounts in general.

They hurt margins (less scale) & they make your brand feel cheaper (less premium feel) = harder to scale on cold traffic.

**So how do we increase perceived value?**

There are 3 ways to do so:

1. Change the narrative around the offer (by targeting fundamental human desires)
2. Solve problems others aren’t solving or be extremely unique (hard to do if your product doesn’t already do this)
3. Solve MORE problems for your target avatar, making your offer feel like a more complete solution

#3 is the one we’ll cover here since everyone should do it…

You solve more problems either through:

- Having a product that just naturally solves a lot of problems (if you sell a product you can easily tweak so that it does this, do that. putting effort into improving your product is always high ROI)
- Bundling products together to create a complete solution. For example by bundling together a few supplements or a few beauty products to create a complete routine that solves a big & holistic problem for the customer

You can also bundle in digital products like ebooks, courses, or community access, which is great because you get 100% margin on those.

With the fitness brand mentioned earlier, we added a pre-workout, a digital diet & training guide, & an online community to one of their products, & sold the whole thing as a 30-day challenge.

Across that whole revamp we cut their CAC by 66%, scaled at a 9.3x ROAS, & added multiple six figures to their monthly net profit.

Another good thing about bundles:

Even people who DON'T buy your bundle end up spending more on average, because the higher price of the bundle anchors your other prices to it, making your other products FEEL cheaper than they are.

& you don't need a massive catalog of products for this.

AG1 scaled ONE main product to hundreds of millions by positioning it as the complete all-in-one nutrition solution, adding small bonus products to it, & having that as their bundle offer.

Then, AFTER you've raised the perceived value, raise your price too to further increase the premium feel.

Usually you’ll be able to do this by a few dollars without CVR dropping at all.

We increased the price of a car-vacuum product from $79 to $89 & the conversion rate stayed the exact same.

So just keep bumping your price until you find the highest price you can charge before conversion starts dropping.

## 3\. Stack your persuasion elements

This isn’t really a part of the offer but it ties into selling to cold traffic…

Once the offer itself is strong, you layer on the stuff that makes people believe it & make sure these things are shown EVERYWHERE:

- A ton of social proof (testimonials, reviews, UGC)
- Authority (doctors, trainers, influencers, research studies)
- A strong guarantee like money-back so there's zero risk to the buyer
- Scarcity or urgency if you can (limited stock, limited time)

& also make sure that on every page you have, you have dedicated sections (not just FAQs at the end) for handling the most common objections cold traffic will have.

## 4\. Find your opportunity

This section might be a bit abstract but I’ll show an example at the end so it makes sense.

You're looking for one of these:

- An unsolved problem nobody's addressing (just solving an unsolved problem with your offer OR marketing your offer to a problem others aren’t solving, for example by including “mood” as one of the benefits of an overall health supplement)
- A new market for a product that already exists (selling the same product to a new avatar, the example below shows this)
- Or an unused traffic source (like organic instead of just paid, just so you reach people others aren’t reaching)

I want to be clear that these 3 don’t always work for all offers, but if you can get one to work (EXPERIMENT HERE) then you’re set for a ton of scale.

The best example for this is Nomisk.

They sold a little device called Maia that women put on their stomach to kill period pain.

Everyone in the space was selling basically the same device for around $50 to women, so instead of selling to women, we sold to their boyfriends & husbands, positioning it as a gift, with the whole angle being "do you love your girlfriend? this is the ultimate gesture of affection."

Brand new market, & we hit two fundamental desires at once: her need to escape the pain (safety), & his desire to be the hero who takes care of her (reproduction).

That let us push the price up to around $100 with no problem, & the brand went from 0 to $10M in its first year.

## 5\. Remember that offers expire

No offer works forever.

Every market gets more sophisticated over time, your customers see more & more ads, they get more skeptical with every brand they see, & slowly the same offer that used to print just stops converting like it did.

When that happens your margins get squeezed & it costs more to acquire each customer, & you're forced to either undercut on price or eat a worse cost per customer, neither of which you want.

So the move is to keep re-marketing the same product in new ways before that happens: a new narrative, a new desire, a new angle, a new audience, adding unique mechanisms.

Offers aren’t a one-time thing you build & forget, you have to always keep iterating on it.
