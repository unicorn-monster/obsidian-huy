# I. they have prospecting-focused accounts

in google ads, you’ve got 2 kinds of accounts

there are prospecting-focused accounts… and there are branded-focused accounts

a branded-focused account is when most of the spend is going toward branded traffic.

for a long time, people treated this as the only way to run google ads.

but if that’s how you’re running it, you’re probably tapping 20% of what the platform offers

there are 2 reasons for that.

first, you’re building the account around a capped customer pool.

there are only so many people searching for your brand every month.

once you capture that demand, that’s kind of it.

you can raise budgets, but you can’t create more traffic.

sure, you can grow branded search by pushing harder on other channels.

but you never want one ad platform depending on another just to grow.

second, google’s a monster for new customer acquisition right now

many of our clients are doing $500k to $1.2m per month in prospecting revenue, beating meta in a lot of cases.

and if you’re running it as a brand-focused account…

there’s probably a pretty huge chunk of revenue sitting there untapped.

because with prospecting, your addressable market is "everyone with the problem your product solves."

that could be 10x, 50x, or 100x larger than your branded search volume.

for most brands we work with, 80-95% of spend goes toward prospecting.

(it can be lower if you’re a well-known brand with large brand search volume or competitors are bidding on your brand terms.)

we keep branded campaigns efficient

then put every extra dollar into finding new customers.

on top of that, we spend time every day looking for new ways to get in front of new customers.

testing landing pages, placements, creatives, strategies, angles, avatars, etc

i’ll end this section by saying this:

if google only makes up a small slice of your total advertising spend…

there are probably a lot of gaps in your prospecting setup.

# II. they have wide search coverage across Search and Shopping

you won’t be able to sell your products on Search and Shopping if your ads don't show up.

and you definitely can’t scale if they're buried in position 5 or sitting on page 2.

strong search coverage is about two things:

\- getting your ads in front of as many relevant searches you can profit from as possible - taking as many positions as possible (ideally the top spots)

when coverage is poor, it looks like this:

\- **shopping**: your products show up for a tiny slice of relevant searches. when they do appear, you're 7th or 8th in the carousel - **search**: you're only targeting 20 keywords when there are 200 relevant searches. your ads sit at the bottom of the page

meanwhile, brands with strong search coverage:

\- show up for the majority of relevant queries across both channels - rank in top positions consistently - take up most of the screen in the search result page

like i said earlier, there are 2 layers to this.

first, you’ve got to show up for relevant searches

that starts with keyword research and understanding what terms you should compete on

[i made an in-depth article about this topic, check it out here](https://x.com/blvckledge/status/2057388919331979534)

then if you want to move into top positions, there are a few things worth paying attention to.

for search, you want to tighten up:

\- ad relevance (how closely the ad matches the keyword) - landing page experience - ad extensions (for better ctr)

for shopping, you want to optimize:

\- merchant center - product feed (especially product titles and images) - landing page

the second layer is taking up as much of the screen as possible.

take a look at this image where the brand is the ONLY one visible in the search page.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HLpiNotaMAA3o-2?format=jpg&name=large)

then compare that to a brand that doesn’t even show up in the shopping result for their own branded search.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HLpiRoFaIAAXjoD?format=jpg&name=large)

do you see the difference?

the first brand’s definitely taking the majority of the clicks from that search

whereas the second brand’s probably losing a ton of sales to their competitors

now, those are obviously the 2 ends of the spectrum, and you’ll normally be somewhere in the middle

still, you want to take as many positions as possible.

to make that happen, you need a strategy called feed duplication

the main idea is:

instead of having one generic product listing trying to rank for everything…

you create multiple versions of the same product, each focused on a specific search intent or use case.

google treats them as separate listings and often shows multiple variations from your brand in the same search result.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HLpiZnmacAAA4jb?format=png&name=large)

we've been running this for 200+ brands for 3 years with zero issues. it's within google's terms of service when done correctly.

i wrote a full article breaking this down step by step, so i'll keep it brief here.

if you want the complete breakdown with everything you need to execute it, [check it out here.](https://x.com/blvckledge/status/2032087432301310209)

here's the high-level version of how this works:

**step 1**: start with your top SKUs. products that already have search volume and serve more than one type of buyer.

**step 2**: for each product, map out 3–4 angles. use simple formulas: \[product\] + use case, \[product\] + demographic, \[product\] + benefit.

**step 3**: create manual feed duplicates for each angle with different titles, images, and other core attributes.

**step 4**: align everything to the angle. title, description, landing page.

**step 5**: add all variations to your campaign. if certain variations aren't getting impressions, pull them into their own campaigns.

**step 6**: track impressions, CTR, conversion rate, and ROAS. push budget toward what's working, fix what's underperforming, cut what's dead weight, and stay on top of your search terms report with negatives.

then you run the same process on more SKUs

one important note here: if you're using a manual feed, google won’t pull updates directly from your store for those duplicated products.

so if you change something in the back end like pricing, availability, or product details, etc…

you'll need to update those values in the manual feed as well.

otherwise the feed can become outdated and eventually cause issues.

this is one of the highest-leverage things you can do in shopping/pmax without adding a single new product.

# III. they treat landing page like creatives

one thing a lot of ecom brands don’t realize is:

landing pages are the Google Ads equivalent of creatives on Meta.

they’re one of the biggest (if not the biggest) factors in your performance.

and the same way you test creatives on meta...

you should be testing landing page variations, angles, and formats on google to:

\- lower acquisition costs - improve conversion rates - unlock new traffic sources from different customer avatars, problems, and use cases

we break this into 3 levels.

## 1\. match the page to the search query

there has to be clear alignment between what someone searched, your ad, and the page they land on.

the tighter that alignment, the better your conversion rate.

google also rewards it with a higher quality score, which lifts your rankings and lowers your cpcs.

so you get more out of the same spend and your campaigns have more room for scale.

start with dedicated pages for each product category.

adjust the headline, imagery, copy, and social proof based on what someone searched.

then go a level deeper into different intents or use cases.

for example:

someone looking for magnesium might search for "magnesium for sleep" or "magnesium for anxiety."

for the sleep search, you want to adjust your headline to "the magnesium formula designed for deep, restful sleep."

for the anxiety search, it's "the magnesium formula designed to help you feel calm all day."

it's a simple concept

but it's a common thread you'll find across every high-converting campaign.

## 2\. match the page to how the market shops

the next level is shaping the page around how customers shop in that market.

every market buys differently.

for some products, people want to buy in bulk to save money.

for others, they want complementary products to go with what they came for.

your landing page should support that buying behaviour.

sometimes that means:

\- adding sections - removing things that don’t matter - changing the flow of information - etc

here’s an example...

in categories like apparel, furniture, and home goods, people want to compare options before they buy.

colors, materials, styles, different versions.

and adding a related products section can give you a big lift in conversions

i know it sounds a bit unintuitive, you’d think more options = more friction = more chances for them to bounce

but we tested this across accounts and it ended up increasing conversion rate by 2-3x for one client.

of course, this depends heavily on the industry.

the point is performance usually improves when the page lines up with the buying behaviors of your market.

## 3\. use a different page format

this is where the biggest gains come from.

instead of tweaking the same page, you build a different format for a specific type of traffic.

a few examples:

\- 3rd-party comparison pages for comparison keywords (e.g., “top shampoo for men in 2026”) - quiz funnels for youtube traffic - us vs. them page for competitor searches - advertorials for top-of-funnel search traffic

all of these would perform terribly on a standard product page, because the intent doesn't match.

and each of them requires a different format to convert at its best.

i think of these pages as keys.

the right one gets you a pocket of revenue that used to be unprofitable to chase, and lets you spend at a whole new level.

take this brand for example…

one brand was struggling to get new customers on search

we rolled out those landing pages and the campaigns started scaling aggressively.

within just 3 months, they hit $26k/week in ad spend.

this is why i say you should treat landing pages on google the way you'd treat creatives on meta.

on meta, you test different creatives to reach different audiences.

on google, you test different landing pages to monetize different types of traffic.

you should constantly be improving existing pages while testing new formats, layouts, and concepts.

(if you’re not sure what to test, [check out this article where i break down the most profitable landing pages for google ads in 2026](https://x.com/blvckledge/status/2040000095928910266))

a lot of brands don’t really have a system for this.

they’ll have a designer or maybe a cro guy.

but very few approach landing pages like an actual growth lever.

how do you expect to scale far when you don’t have a dedicated landing page strategy?

just like with meta…

if a brand doesn't have a creative strategy, how far do you expect them to scale?

# IV. they give demand gen the same level of attention as meta

a lot of people believe that demand gen is only good for brand awareness, or that they're not profitable.

that was probably true a couple years ago. but now, it’s complete bs.

google rolled out a bunch of significant changes to demand gen in 2025 and 2026.

\- **channel controls**: you can choose which surfaces to serve your ads on

\- **better match**: targeting has gotten a lot sharper with gemini. it’s much better at matching your ads to the right person

\- **more inventory**: there are more placements in rotation now, and plenty of them are giving profitable returns

put simply:

the platform has become far more effective.

in my opinion, it’s slowly becoming the new meta ads for ecom

this year, we’ve seen a bunch of new winning placements and creative formats inside our accounts.

and many of our clients are scaling to [$20k-80k](https://x.com/search?q=%2420k-80k&src=cashtag_click)/week here: like this brand that we took from $14.6k/week to $82k/week in spend in just 4 months.

this creates a rare window where you get the best of both worlds:

\- you're competing against fewer advertisers than other channels - the platform is working better than it ever has

now for demand gen, the #1 lever is the creative

these are the main formats we’d focus on:

\- youtube in-stream ads (runs before or during videos) - youtube shorts (shows between vertical shorts while people scroll) - youtube in-feed (shows on homepage and youtube search as thumbnails) - google discover (native news feed in the google app and new chrome tabs)

you don't need all 4 formats to start.

the play is to pick a format, match the creative to how people consume on placement, and expand once you've got a winner.
