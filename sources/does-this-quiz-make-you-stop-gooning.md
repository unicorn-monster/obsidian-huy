WorkWithShadow is another example of a direct response web app. As I've said before, the direct response web app guys are the forefathers of quiz funnels. Often we breakdown DTC brands, who tend to have clunkier quizzes. These guys take an obscure psychological framework — Carl Jung's shadow work — and quietly turn it into a 23-question onboarding that closes a 2-month subscription. Let's get into it.

![[gooning-01.png]]

## The Psychology Behind The Quiz → WorkWithShadow
The structure follows an aspiration arc — not a problem arc. Goals first. Symptoms second. Identity third. They open with what you want — not what's wrong with you. By question 9 they're asking what's broken — but you've already told them what you're trying to fix. By question 23, you're typing in your age and gender like an afterthought. Most quizzes ask demographics first. WorkWithShadow asks them last. That's not a small thing.

![[gooning-02.png]]

**Q1 — Goals first, not demographics.** Notice the order: "What do you hope to achieve…" → Strengthen my relationship with myself → Heal from past emotional wounds → Improve my relationships with others → Boost my self-esteem → Foster personal growth. Every option is a positive future state. Not "what's wrong with you" — "what do you want." You haven't admitted a single problem yet and you're already invested. Because you just publicly committed to wanting growth. Then they hit you with social proof. Immediately. Question 1 ends and you don't get question 2 — you get this:

![[gooning-03.png]]

**Popup 1 — 123,629 people, mentioned in BI/Forbes/NYT.** Notice what they did: you answered ONE question. They paid that off with a credibility wall before asking for question two. Most quiz funnels stack social proof at the END. These guys frontload it. Because the biggest drop-off in any quiz is between Q1 and Q2. This screen makes you feel like you joined something — not started a survey.

Then a Yes/No that splits the entire quiz:

![[gooning-04.png]]

**Q2 — Are you familiar with shadow work?** This is the most important question in the funnel. Because it controls whether they teach you the concept — or skip the education. Yes → routes to popup-3 ("Great! Since you're familiar…"). No → routes to popup-2 ("No worries, we'll teach you"). Either way, the next screen is a reward — not another question.

![[gooning-05.png]]

**Popup 3 — "Great!" reward screen.** Beautiful image. Validation copy. Continue button. This is doing the same thing Inflow's Quinn does — it's the friend checking in mid-funnel. Most quizzes feel like forms. This one feels like someone is paying attention to your answers.

Then 5 questions of belief loosening:

![[gooning-06.png]]

**Q3 — How do you feel about yourself?** (verified via screenshot: 3 options — "I struggle to see my worth" / "I have moments of self-acceptance" / "I'm proud of who I am today", counter "3/23" top-right)

![[gooning-07.png]]
**Q4 — Expressing emotions**
![[gooning-08.png]]
**Q5 — Don't love yourself**
![[gooning-09.png]]
**Q6 — Relationships**
![[gooning-10.png]]
**Q7 — Trust others**

Q3 → Q7 are all asking the same thing in 5 different ways: how do you feel about yourself / your emotions / your relationships / your trust. The answers always have 3 options. A struggle option, a middle option, a confident option. Stack rank: low → medium → high. But the ORDER you read them matters — struggle is on top, so the struggle answer reads first. You read it first, you recognize yourself first, you click it first. The middle option exists to not feel like you're being railroaded. The confident option exists to make this feel like a real diagnostic. Almost no one clicks it.

Then Carl Jung walks in:

![[gooning-11.png]]

**Popup 4 — Carl Jung quote on family.** "The greatest tragedy of the family is the unlived lives of the parents." They earn this moment — you've now told them you struggle with self-worth, expressing emotions, trust, and connection. NOW Carl Jung shows up to explain why. This is the same move Inflow makes when Quinn introduces "Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria." The user thinks: "There's a name for what I feel?" You just had a lifetime of self-blame reframed as inherited family trauma. Massive tension release. Massive trust built. And the kicker — they didn't even sell you anything yet.

Then a format switch:

![[gooning-12.png]]

**Q8 — Single statement, italicized quote style.** Same quiz, different visual. Italic. Quote marks. New layout. It's not a question — it's a statement you agree or disagree with. Why? Because Q3-Q7 had the same structure 5 times in a row. Format fatigue is real. A single-statement question every 5 slides resets the brain. You feel like the quiz just got more interesting — even though they're still measuring the same thing.

Then they ask you what to fix:

![[gooning-13.png]]

**Q9 — Multi-select shadow aspects to address.** Multi-select, specific labels: Anxiety. Lack of Boundaries. Low Self-Esteem. Fear of Rejection. Procrastination. Overthinking. Anger Management. These aren't questions — they're labels for the user to self-diagnose with. Once you check 3 of these, the AI in your head says "yeah, that's me." Now they have your stated problem list. Whatever the offer is, it's about to be reframed as the answer to exactly these 3 boxes you just clicked. This is also a Klaviyo tag (`qShadowAspects`) — whatever you check here gets fed into your email retargeting later.

Then 5 more belief shifts:

![[gooning-14.png]]
**Q10 — Not good enough**
![[gooning-15.png]]
**Q11 — Feel unwanted**
![[gooning-16.png]]
**Q12 — Accomplishments**
![[gooning-17.png]]
**Q13 — Dreams**

By now you've been on this thing for ~3 minutes. You should be tired. But you're not. Because the questions are getting harder, not easier. Q10: are you feeling not good enough? Q11: how often do you feel unwanted? Q12: how do you feel about your accomplishments? Q13: how do you feel about your dreams? Watch the descent — worth → wanted → achievement → dream. They're walking you down through layers of self. Each one cuts closer. By Q13 you're not answering a question anymore — you're confessing.

Then the framework:

![[gooning-18.png]]

**Popup 5 — Ego/Persona/Self/Shadow diagram.** This is the smartest screen in the entire funnel. They've taught you the model. Ego. Persona. Self. Shadow. Once you know the names, the product can sell you "shadow work" and you'll know what they mean. Inflow does the same thing with executive dysfunction. You can't sell a solution to a problem the user doesn't have a name for. So the funnel gives them the name for free. This isn't education. It's vocabulary installation. (Screenshot verified: Carl Jung's Map of the Psyche diagram — Ego/Persona nested circle top, Self small gold circle middle, Shadow/Anima-Animus circle bottom, "Increasingly Conscious ↕ Increasingly Unconscious" axis label — with 4 plain-language definitions below and a Continue button.)

Then they accelerate:

**Q14 — Inadequacy**
![[gooning-20.png]]
**Q15 — Conflict**
![[gooning-21.png]]
**Q16 — Single-statement: criticism**

**Q17 — Failure**

Inadequacy. Conflict. Criticism. Failure. The whole back half of the quiz is about how you respond to bad things — not whether bad things happen, they assume that — but what you do with them. This is where they're scoring you. Avoidant types → one offer. Self-critical types → another offer. The behavior pattern is the segment.

Then another Carl Jung break:

![[gooning-23.png]]

**Popup 6 — "What you resist, persists."** Trusting others and embracing failure as a stepping stone are crucial for resilience. They drop another quote right when the questions are at their heaviest. This isn't decoration, it's pacing. If you put 12 hard questions in a row, you lose people. If you break it up with a famous quote and a nice image, you don't. These breaks are what let them run a 23-question quiz when most DTC brands cap out at 8.

Then 3 future-pacing questions:

![[gooning-24.png]]
**Q18 — Future**
![[gooning-25.png]]
**Q19 — Personal growth**
![[gooning-26.png]]
**Q20 — The unknown**

Q18: how do you feel about your future? Q19: how do you approach personal growth? Q20: how do you handle the unknown? The arc has flipped — Q3-Q17 was about how you feel right now, Q18-Q20 is about who you could become. This is exactly when they pivot. Because by here, you're tired of feeling bad about yourself. You want to feel hopeful. So they ask hopeful-shaped questions and let you walk yourself toward the offer.

Then a second multi-select that becomes the offer:

![[gooning-27.png]]

**Q21 — Negative patterns to break.** What negative patterns do you want to break? Self-doubt. Perfectionism. People-pleasing. Procrastination. Comparison. Self-criticism. Avoidance of conflict. Fear of failure. Distrust of others. Click 4 of those. Now the offer screen can say "Your plan helps you break: Self-doubt, Perfectionism, People-pleasing, Self-criticism" — using your exact words. This is also a Klaviyo tag (`qStruggles`) — so your email sequences talk to you using the same labels. You won't notice, you'll just feel like the brand "gets you."

Then one last quote — the close:

![[gooning-28.png]]

**Popup 7 — "The privilege of a lifetime."** "The privilege of a lifetime is to become who you truly are." This isn't a Jung quote randomly thrown in — this is positioning. Right before the calculating screen, they reframe the entire process. You aren't buying an app, you're claiming "the privilege of a lifetime." The next screen is going to ask for money. So this screen makes you feel like spending money on this is morally correct.

Then — and only then — they ask for demographics:

![[gooning-29.png]]
**Q22 — Age (after 21 emotional questions)**
![[gooning-30.png]]
**Q23 — Gender**

Age and gender are Q22 and Q23. If they'd asked these on Q1, half the users would've bounced. But they ask these AFTER the user has emotionally invested 4 minutes. Now the user is too far in to leave. It's the same psychology airlines use when they ask for your card after you've picked your seat. These last two questions are also Klaviyo tags (`qAge`, `qGender`) — so the back-end has age + gender + every emotional answer they captured. That's 23 segmentation tags they can use to personalize email, retargeting, ad audiences, and offer pricing.

Then the calculating screen does 3 jobs at once:

![[gooning-31.png]]

**Calculating — phase progress + Trustpilot review + commitment gate.** Look at how much is happening on this single screen. (Screenshot verified: header "Creating your personalized Shadow Work Plan", phase label "Setting goals 50%" progress bar, a 5-star Trustpilot review ["It has truly transformed my life… - Brian Ross"] scrolling behind, and a modal popup gating the loader.)
1. Phase progress — "Setting goals 50%" with an orange bar. The user feels like the system is working with their answers in real time.
2. Trustpilot reviews scroll behind it. Social proof while they wait.
3. A modal popup gates the loader — "Are you inclined to finish what you start?" (Yes/No). They don't let the loader run on its own. Every 30% of progress, a yes/no question pops up. The user can't bounce the tab — there's an active question on screen. This is engagement engineering. A loader bar that just runs is dead time. A loader bar with 3 mid-gates and a Trustpilot reel is 12 seconds of conversion lift.

The whole funnel architecture in one sentence: they open with goals, frontload social proof, teach you the framework, drop Jung quotes between hard questions, route you with a Yes/No on Q2, hit you with format switches every 5-7 slides, save demographics for Q22-Q23, and stack 3 retention mechanics into the calculating screen.

23 questions. Should kill any quiz. Doesn't kill this one. Because it doesn't feel like 23 questions. It feels like a guided journal. That's the difference between a quiz that segments — and a quiz that sells.
