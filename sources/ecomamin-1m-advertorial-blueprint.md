Standard advertorial (advertisement + editorial) funnel system: cold traffic → advertorial (educates, builds authority, pre-sells) → product page → thank-you. Claimed conversion 8-15% vs 1-2% direct-to-PDP on identical cold traffic. Largely overlaps with [[ugly-ads-method]]'s 15-beat skeleton and [[funnel-and-landing]]'s advertorial layer — notes below focus on the specific mechanics/numbers from this doc.

## The 3 psychological triggers claimed to drive the format
1. Pattern interrupt — clicking through to something that reads as a neutral article (not a sales page) deactivates the "someone's selling me" defense reflex.
2. Authority transfer — citing studies/mechanisms/journals through the body repositions the brand from "seller" to "expert recommending a solution based on the science," changing the frame the product is received in at reveal.
3. Belief shifting — the body of the piece is structured to name and dismantle the reader's existing (wrong) causal belief about their problem and install a new one before the product ever appears, so by reveal-time "they don't need convincing anymore, they just need to know where to buy."

## Ad-hook formula that drives the click
`[pattern interrupt] + [curiosity gap] + [promise of explanation]` — e.g. *"Your mattress isn't causing your back pain (physical therapists now know the real culprit)"*. The CTA should sell the click to INFORMATION, not to purchase ("Read the full breakdown" beats "Shop Now").

## 6-section page structure with word counts (distinctive bridge structure)
1. **Validate the struggle** (200-300 words) — mirror their exact experience, list what they've likely already tried, validate that it helped briefly, explain why it stopped working, tease that there's a real cause they don't know.
2. **Explain the mechanism** (400-500 words) — name the real cause plainly, walk the causal chain step by step, contrast against what they believed was the cause, explain specifically why their prior attempts failed (removes blame/shame), cite a study with real numbers.
3. **Introduce the SOLUTION CATEGORY — not yet the product** (300-400 words) — this is the distinctive bridge step most advertorial skeletons skip: explain the "professional"/expensive version of the fix (e.g. $2,000-4,000 of PT), name why most people never actually do it (cost, time, inconvenience), THEN introduce a new product CATEGORY that replicates the professional approach at home/cheaper — still without naming your specific product. Building desire for the category first means the eventual specific-product reveal lands as "thank god someone made this" instead of "here comes the pitch."
4. **Introduce your product** (400-500 words) — name it naturally (not with fanfare), who developed it (credentialed-sounding team), break the mechanism into the same components named in section 2/3, cite a specific (or generically-sourced) study/number per component, give a results timeline (day-by-day/week-by-week), one expert-sounding testimonial, price stated by direct cost-contrast against the "professional" alternative from section 3, then the guarantee.
5. **Social proof** (300-400 words) — 4-6 testimonials with real demographic specificity (age, job, years-struggled, $-amount-previously-wasted, realistic non-hyperbolic language), including at least one visibly-skeptical-convert angle.
6. **Soft CTA** (150-200 words) — stays in consultative tone, frames as "you'll spend money on this problem regardless" logic, explicit worst-case/best-case framing, guarantee restated, plain logistics (ship time, support access).

## Design rules for the page itself
Must read as editorial (Healthline/WebMD/Consumer-Reports register): white bg, black serif body text, minimal/small logo, author byline + publication date, clean layout. Must NOT look like a brand page: no bright colors, no CTA buttons scattered through the body, no countdown timers, no "buy now" language anywhere until the CTA section — author's claim: "if it looks like an ad, trust drops by 60%."

## Product-page-after-advertorial structure
Treats the visitor as 80-90% pre-sold: hero + benefit-restating headline that echoes the advertorial's specific claims, price shown as a direct before/after-guarantee-framed contrast, a 3-step "how it works" visual mirroring the mechanism sections, a direct comparison table against the "professional alternative" named in section 3 of the advertorial, FAQ addressing the same objections raised/answered in the advertorial, and a final CTA repeating the worst-case/best-case framing.

## Case numbers (illustrative, single brand)
Before (direct-to-PDP): 10,000 visitors/mo, 1.8% CVR, 180 customers, $47 AOV, $8,460 revenue, $20,000 spend at $2 CPC → net **-$11,540/mo**. After (advertorial inserted, same traffic/product/price): 55% advertorial completion, 11.2% CVR, 616 customers, $52 AOV (author attributes the AOV lift to buyer confidence), $32,032 revenue → net **+$12,032/mo**. Swing: **$23,572/month** from inserting one page.

## Traffic-source rule
Advertorials are strictly for COLD traffic (people who don't know the brand yet) — explicitly NOT for email list, retargeting, or branded search, since those audiences already have brand context the advertorial's "independent editorial" framing would undercut.

## 8 stated mistakes
Looking like an ad (bright colors/logo-heavy/CTA-scattered/countdown) · pitching too early (product shouldn't appear before section 4, ~70% of the page should be pure education before ~30% product) · fabricating studies/citations/fake-googleable doctor names · generic 5-star-emoji testimonials instead of specific plain-language ones · aggressive/urgency-loaded CTA instead of consultative framing · sending warm traffic to it · ad-hook/advertorial-headline mismatch (must match exactly or immediate bounce) · overcomplicating the funnel with popups/chat-widgets/multiple competing CTAs.

## Scaling levers once a base advertorial converts
Multiple advertorial ANGLES for the same product (mechanism-focused / comparison-focused / social-proof-focused / contrarian-"everything you've been told is wrong"-focused) to test which resonates per audience. Problem/solution laddering — separate advertorials per distinct problem the same product solves, each a different entry point. Retargeting sequence for advertorial-readers-who-didn't-buy across day1/3/7/14 with different angles (social proof / mechanism / limited-offer / guarantee). Mid-article email-gate ("enter your email to continue reading") feeding a 5-email follow-up sequence — author claims 10-20% conversion on that reclaimed list.

## Unit economics to $1M/yr (worked example)
$67 price, $18 COGS ($49 margin), 10% CVR, $1.50 avg CPC: 14,925 customers, 149,250 visitors, $223,875 spend → $731,325 gross profit, ~$507,450 net after ads. Monthly ~$83,333 revenue / $18,656 spend / ~$42,287 net.

## Author's claimed format-lifecycle timeline (treat as a sales-framing claim, not verified industry data)
2015-17 native ads peak → saturated. 2017-19 long-form VSL peak → fatigued. 2020-22 quiz funnels peak → commoditized. 2023-25 (per author) advertorials are the current peak, "still working." Author's claim: ~18-24 months of "8-15% conversion" window before broader saturation compresses it to a claimed 4-6% (still functional, not exceptional).
