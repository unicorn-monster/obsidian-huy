## What Native Ads Actually Are (And Why Most Founders Get This Wrong)

Native ads are not Meta ads that look like organic posts. That's the first misconception I have to clear up almost every time.

Native ads are placements on the open web. The recommended article widgets at the bottom of CNN, Fox News, The Daily Mail, Forbes, every major publisher.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HIrAtnlboAA4IAq?format=jpg&name=large)

The user isn't scrolling a feed looking for entertainment or cheap dopamine. They're already reading something.

They're calm. Focused.

In an article-reading state of mind. That changes everything about what kind of creative and copy works.

The platforms that serve these placements are Taboola, Outbrain, RevContent, MGID, Yahoo DSP and many more.

Combined, they reach almost every device with an internet connection. **Daily impression volume across the major networks is in the tens of billions.**

You don't target users on native the way you do on Meta. You can't pick a 35 year old woman in Texas who shops at Sephora and recently engaged with skincare content.

You target by site, by widget, by device, by country, and by signal. It's broader.

But here's why that's good:

If your product solves a mass desire or a mass problem (sleep, joint pain, weight, skin, energy, finances), targeting precision matters less than scale. You don't need to find the perfect customer. You need to find a lot of people, qualify them with your creative, walk them through an advertorial, and convert the ones who are interested.

Done right, native does two things Meta can't:

1. It opens up upper-funnel reach Meta can't profitably touch at scale.
2. It floods your retargeting pools with warm traffic that converts on Meta and Google at lower CPAs.

That second point is what most operators miss. **Native isn't just a standalone channel. It's a top-of-funnel engine that makes every other channel cheaper.**

## Who Should Run Native (And Who Shouldn't)

I'm gonna be very direct with this because I have so many ecom bros in my DM's asking me if they should get started with $100/d

**No.**

Native is not a starter channel. If you're doing under $50,000 to $100,000 a month in revenue, you're not ready. The math doesn't work at low spend, the learning curve is steep, and you'll burn budget on a channel that punishes impatience.

**Who should run native ads:**

- DTC brands already profitable on Meta and Google at scale.
- Brands with a product that solves a clear, mass-market problem. The verticals where native works best: joint pain, sleep, weight, skincare, hair, digestive health, menopause, hormonal health, energy, focus, finance, eye health. (basically any mass pain/ desire)
- Operators who can commit a four to six week testing window with a real budget.
- Brands ready to invest in proper advertorials. If you won't fund a real funnel, native is not for you.

**Who should not run native:**

- Brands under $50k/mo. Build Meta and Google first.
- Niche B2B or hyper-targeted products. Native is a mass-market channel.
- Brands that don't have the bandwidth to write or commission long-form advertorials.
- Anyone who wants to send paid traffic straight to PDP. I'll explain why in the next section.

I've watched brands stuck at $300k/mo on Meta scale to $1M/mo by adding native as a parallel channel. I've also watched brands that weren't ready burn $20k learning a lesson they could have read in a paragraph. The difference was readiness, not skill.

**For brands that crack it, native often ends up as the largest channel in the mix.** Bigger than Meta. Bigger than Google. The ceiling is that high.

## Understanding CPM, CPC, and CTR (The Math That Runs This Channel)

Here's the part most native ads "gurus" overcomplicate. The math is simple, and once you understand it, you understand what the creative is supposed to do.

The three numbers that drive everything:

- **CPM**: cost per 1,000 impressions. What the platform charges for delivery.
- **CTR**: click-through rate. The percentage of people who see your ad and click.
- **CPC**: cost per click. What you pay each time someone clicks through.

The formula that ties them together:

## CPC = CPM ÷ (CTR × 10)

Run the math:

- $5 CPM at 1% CTR: $5 ÷ (1 × 10) = **$0.50 CPC**
- $5 CPM at 2% CTR: $5 ÷ (2 × 10) = **$0.25 CPC**
- $5 CPM at 0.5% CTR: $5 ÷ (0.5 × 10) = **$1.00 CPC**

CPM is mostly set by the auction, not by you. CTR is the variable you actually control. So your only job at the top of the funnel is to drive CTR up.

Higher CTR equals lower CPC equals more clicks for the same budget equals more chances to convert.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HIrCGXhaYAASz3Q?format=jpg&name=large)

This is why the operators who crack native aren't always the best copywriters. They're the best at testing. They run 20 headline and image combinations against the same advertorial and watch which ones push CTR from 0.3% to 0.8%. That single change cuts CPC by more than half. Same budget, more than twice the volume into your funnel.

**The creative has one job: maximize CTR.**

Not sell. Not explain. Not even fully describe the product. Just earn the click.

## The Funnel Structure (Creative + Advertorial + PDP)

You cannot run native traffic to a product page. It just doesn't work.

To explain why, you need to know Eugene Schwartz's awareness stages.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HIrFaExbAAA_2L2?format=jpg&name=large)

Schwartz mapped buyers into five stages:

1. **Unaware**: doesn't know they have a problem.
2. **Problem aware**: knows they have a problem, doesn't know solutions exist.
3. **Solution aware**: knows solutions exist, doesn't know your product.
4. **Product aware**: knows your product, hasn't decided to buy.
5. **Most aware**: ready to buy.

Cold native traffic almost always lands in stage 1 or 2. Unaware or problem-aware. They're reading an article about something unrelated. Your job is to walk them from where they are to "most aware" before they hit the buy button.

Meta and Google can pull traffic that's already closer to solution-aware or product-aware because the targeting is sharper. Native can't. The audience is wider, less qualified, and earlier in the journey. So the funnel has to do the qualification work that the targeting can't.

That's why the funnel has three components. Each one has exactly one job.

**1\. The creative (image + headline)**. Job: generate curiosity and pre-filter.

The creative is not selling the product. It's not explaining the problem. It's not even teaching anything. It's doing two things only:

- Spark enough curiosity to earn a click.
- Pre-qualify who that click should come from.

A good native headline doesn't say what the product is. It just hints at something the right reader needs to know.

Example:

> "Android users: there's a security setting most people never change. Here's what it does."

That headline doesn't sell anything. It doesn't imply a product. It says "if you own an Android, this is for you" (pre-filter) and "there's something you don't know" (curiosity). **iPhone users scroll past. Android users click.**

And that's all the creative should do: Filter and click.

**2\. The advertorial.** Job: bridge the gap from problem-aware to product-aware.

The advertorial is the 600 to 900 word article the user lands on. It looks editorial. Reads editorial. Feels like content, not a sales page.

Its job is to walk the reader from "I have a problem I haven't thought much about" to "I now understand my problem, why it's worse than I thought, what causes it, and what the right solution looks like."

Notice what the advertorial doesn't do. It doesn't sell. It doesn't push for the order. It informs. It frames. It positions.

When I first started writing advertorials I made every one of them a 1,400 word half-disguised sales pitch. CVR sat at 0.4%. I rewrote one at 650 words of actual informational content with the product mentioned only in the last third. CVR jumped to 2.1%. Same product. Same offer. Different role for the advertorial.

**3\. The product detail page (PDP).** Job: convert.

By the time the reader hits the PDP they're warm. They've been told what their problem is, why it matters, and that this product is the right kind of solution. The PDP doesn't have to re-educate them. It has to close.

The PDP cannot be your generic Shopify product page. It has to match the angle and framing of the advertorial. If the advertorial talked about "the inflammation root cause most joint creams ignore," the PDP needs to lead with the same framing. Continuity from advertorial to PDP is the single biggest leak point I see in funnels I audit.

Three components. Three jobs. Each one does its job, and only its job. That's how cold native traffic converts.

## Advertorials (And Why They Do 70% Of The Work)

I want to spend more time on advertorials because they are the single most undervalued piece of the funnel.

Most founders think the creative is the hard part. It's not. Creative testing is fast. You can run 20 image-headline combinations in a week and know which ones work.

The advertorial is the hard part. It takes longer to write. It takes longer to test. **And it's where roughly 70% of the conversion lift comes fro**m.

Here's how to write one that converts. Six structural pieces, in order:

**1\. The hook.** The opening 100 words have to deliver on the curiosity the headline promised. If the headline said "Android users need to know about this security leak," paragraph one starts expanding that idea immediately. Not the product. The story.

**2\. The problem block.** Walk the reader through their problem in language that makes them feel seen. Specifics matter. "Most people over 40 wake up with stiff knees but don't realize they're forming a pattern that gets worse every year" works better than "joint pain is common."

**3\. The mechanism.** Introduce the cause of the problem in a way that's new to the reader. This is where you take them from problem aware to solution aware. "Most joint creams target inflammation. The new research is pointing at synovial fluid breakdown as the real driver." The reader thinks "I didn't know that."

**4\. The pivot to the solution category.** Not the product yet. The category of solution. "What's working is a specific class of compounds that target synovial fluid directly." Now they're solution-aware.

**5\. The product.** Now you name it. Now you position it. As the best example of the solution category you just educated them about. This is where the brand, the founder story, and the ingredient list show up.

**6\. The CTA and social proof.** Reviews. Before-and-afters. Specific results. A clear button down to the PDP.

The advertorial does the unglamorous work of changing how the reader sees their own problem. By the time they click through to your PDP, the sale is mostly made. The PDP just has to not screw it up.

A few practical rules from running and writing these for years:

- Keep them 1000 to 1800 words.
- Use a real-looking byline and a real-looking publication frame. Editorial trust signals matter.
- Insert two or three images that look like stock editorial photography, not product shots.
- Lead with the problem, not the brand. The reader doesn't care about your brand yet.
- Mention the product only in the back half. Most founders mention it too early.

## The Practical Guide: Setting Up Your First Taboola Campaign

Now the part most founders skipped ahead to. Here's how to actually start.

**Step 1: Pick your platform. Start with Taboola.**

There are five major native networks: Taboola, Outbrain, RevContent, MGID, Yahoo DSP. For a first campaign, start with Taboola. Bigger inventory, more US traffic, more publisher reach, more data to learn from.

Before you spend a dollar, do research. Open a native ad spy tool (AdBeat, Anstrex, PowerAdSpy are the standard options). Pull every active native campaign in your vertical. Look at the headlines, the images, the advertorials, the PDPs. Don't copy. Map the patterns. Which mechanisms repeat. Which angles repeat. Which products dominate.

You can also just go to a major news site, scroll to the bottom, and study what's running. Most of the top advertisers in your category will be there. Free education.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HIrG9R2aUAAWwgn?format=jpg&name=large)

**Step 2: Decide on an angle.**

Before writing a single line of creative, pick the angle. The angle is the path from curiosity (in the headline) to information (in the advertorial) to conviction (at the PDP).

For a joint pain product, the angle might be "the synovial fluid mechanism most joint creams ignore." For a sleep product, "the cortisol cycle that wakes you at 3am." For a hair product, "the DHT signaling pathway most hair products don't address."

The angle has to do three things:

- Be specific enough to feel new.
- Be broad enough to apply to a mass audience.
- Be defensible enough that your product actually delivers on it.

Pick one angle per campaign. Don't try to win with three.

**Step 3: Build the creatives.**

You need 15 to 20 creative combinations to start. Mix of headline angles, mix of images.

Image rules that hold up in 2026:

- Editorial-style beats polished. Images that look like a journalist took them outperform images that look like a brand designed them.
- Faces beat product shots in upper funnel.
- The "ugly" creative wins more often than the pretty one. The reader's brain pattern-matches polished images to "ad" and scrolls.

Headline rules:

- Specific numbers beat round numbers. "73 year old" beats "elderly person."
- Pre-filter by audience or condition in the headline (Android users, women over 50, people with knee pain).
- Curiosity gap, not curiosity hype. Imply something specific the reader needs to know.

For the start, always test like this: 3 headlines against 3 images. Cut hard with enough data

**Step 4: Write (or commission) the advertorial.**

This is the unsexy work. Block out two to three days to do it right.

Use the six-piece structure from the previous section. 600 to 900 words. Editorial framing. Product mentioned only in the back half. CTA to a PDP that matches the angle.

**Step 5: Build the PDP.**

Most operators forget this step and lose half their funnel performance to a generic product page.

The PDP needs to:

- Lead with the same angle and language the advertorial used.
- Reinforce the mechanism the advertorial introduced.
- Carry over visual continuity from the advertorial. Same tone, same color palette.
- Strip everything that isn't directly tied to the sale. No nav distractions. No cross-sells to other products. One product, one offer, one path.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HIrJX5nagAA78DW?format=jpg&name=large)

**Step 6: Set the budget.**

Native does not work at low spend. CPMs are higher when daily budget is low because you're competing for residual inventory.

Minimum starting budget I recommend: $400 per day. Below that, CPMs are inflated and you can't gather enough data fast enough to make decisions. At $400 to $800 per day, CPMs normalize and you start seeing real performance signal within 7 to 10 days.

CPMs typically drop 20 to 40% as daily spend scales from $400 to $2,000. That's not a coincidence. It's the auction rewarding scaled spenders with better inventory.

If $400 per day for two to four weeks is more than you can put at risk, you're not ready for native yet. Go back to Meta and Google and come back when you are.
