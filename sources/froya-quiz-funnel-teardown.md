**6 questions. No email gate. No results page. Straight to the offer.**

Moérie sells you a diagnosis. Frøya doesn't bother. 6 questions. A spinner. Then: "40% OFF, verified lowest price ever." That's the whole funnel. 8 steps. No email gate. No results page. No charts. No dashboard.

It's the shortest quiz funnel we've torn down. And it's doing $10-20M.

Uncomfortable question: is the whole "personalized diagnosis" thing even necessary? Or is a quiz just a better-converting product page?

## Overview
Frøya Organics. Norwegian-inspired hair care. Cold-pressed Arctic botanicals for thinning, shedding, aging hair in mature women. 350K monthly visitors. $10-20M est. revenue. 4.7 Trustpilot. Running since 2019. Almost all paid social into one quiz funnel, landing on a subscribe-and-save "Complete System" bundle. Same category as Moérie, completely opposite philosophy.

## The Quiz IS the Landing Page
![[froya-quiz-step-1.webp]]

No hero. No "Start Quiz" button. No gender fork. The page loads and you are already on Question 1. That kills the "should I start this?" decision — the single biggest drop-off point in any quiz funnel.

Progress bar already moving → sunk cost primed before you've spent a thing. "Discover Your Routine in 60 Seconds" caps perceived time cost at the moment commitment is cheapest. "88,429 transformations ★★★★★" sits inline, so you read social proof while answering, not at checkout when you're defensive.

## Q1 Is the Most Emotional Question They Have
![[froya-quiz-step-2.webp]]

Most brands open safe (hair type / age / gender). Frøya opens with "What's your biggest hair concern right now?" — most emotionally loaded thing you can ask a hair-loss sufferer, and also the primary segmentation variable. One tap, both jobs.

Q2: "When did you first notice this change?" Auto-advances on tap, no continue button. Builds the urgency frame — you just told yourself how long you've let this slide.

## The Blame Questions
![[froya-quiz-step-3.webp]]

"Which of these life stages/events have you experienced recently?" (High stress / Postpartum / Perimenopause or menopause). Then: "Does hair loss run in your family?"

Neither question configures a product. They exist to make you blame something that isn't you — stress, hormones, genetics, all outside your control. Two effects: shame drops (a woman who isn't ashamed doesn't bounce), and you supply the evidence for the pitch coming. When the offer says "aging, genetics and stress cause thinning" you can't argue — you said it.

## The Spinner Is Doing All the Work
![[froya-quiz-step-5.webp]]

Six questions in, the entire "personalization" layer: a spinning loader, a photo of pink Arctic wildflowers in snow, one line — "Aging, genetics, and stress can all lead to thinning and shedding. The good news? Arctic herbs target these root causes."

Nothing is computed. No model. The offer is basically the same no matter what you tapped. The spinner buys the feeling of a custom prescription — and Frøya never has to build the results page that would deliver it. Moérie spends five screens and a bar chart earning that feeling. Frøya rents it for three seconds. ("as little as 3 to 6 months" quietly resets refund expectations.)

## No Email Gate. At All.
Frøya never asks for email. Not once. Quiz → spinner → buy page. Most funnels treat the email gate as the whole point (trade "your results" for a lead). Frøya's bet: the gate is friction, and friction between a warm quiz-taker and the offer costs more than the lead is worth.

Abandoner recovery = retargeting pixel fires on load (roeye/lantern, device fingerprint). The ad account owns re-engagement, not the ESP.

- **Gate the email** when margin needs a long nurture, product is considered/expensive/unfamiliar, or you own a real email program that converts on send 3-7.
- **Skip the gate** when buying cold paid traffic, product is cheap/impulse, and media math needs the highest offer-view rate. Every point lost at the gate is a point of ROAS.

## The Results Page That's Actually a Sales Page
![[froya-quiz-step-7.webp]]

No answer recap, no score, no "hair profile." Just every persuasion lever at once:
- **Social proof** — "12,000+ Women Trust Frøya" + named UGC testimonial with a face.
- **Concrete timeline** — "Reduces Thinning and Boosts Growth in 8 Weeks."
- **Urgency** — "TODAY ONLY... internet-only price" (implies channel exclusive → kills comparison shopping).
- **Price-anchor superlative** — "VERIFIED LOWEST PRICE EVER" reframes a standing 40% discount as now-or-never.
- **Framing** — it's a *System*, not a shampoo → bundle prices + default subscribe-and-save. One-time buy becomes replenishment revenue.

![[froya-quiz-step-8.webp]]

## So Which Approach Wins?
Moérie's data-dense diagnosis wins when the purchase needs justification (higher price, skeptical buyer, mechanism that needs explaining — the charts are the argument). Frøya's minimalist qualifier wins when the buyer already knows they have the problem and just needs permission plus a discount. Every extra screen is another exit. The quiz here isn't a diagnostic tool — it's a commitment device with a segmentation bonus.

## What to steal
1. Kill your landing page. Open on Q1. The "should I start?" decision is your biggest leak.
2. Start the progress bar mid-way. Sunk cost before any investment.
3. Lead with the most emotional question, not the safest — it's also your best segmentation variable.
4. Add 1-2 blame questions → attribute the problem to something outside their control. They defend the diagnosis for you.
5. The spinner does the personalization work. A 3-second "analyzing" beat buys the custom-result feeling even when generic.
6. Test dropping the email gate on cold paid traffic. Let the pixel retarget. Measure offer-view rate, not list growth.
7. Sell a "System," not a product. Higher AOV, defaults into subscription.
