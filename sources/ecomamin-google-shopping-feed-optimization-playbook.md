Technical, SOP-style feed-optimization guide — more procedural than the other Amin docs. Covers ground not in [[ecomamin-shopping-masterclass]]/[[ecomamin-1m-google-shopping-ads-blueprint-a]]: GTIN mechanics, Item Group ID, Google's built-in Product Studio AI tool, feed-management tooling, and pricing-as-dominant-signal.

## Weekly habit: Merchant Center diagnostics
Products → "Needs Attention" tab → View History, weekly. Issues bucket into image / pricing / data-quality / policy / missing-attributes. Many issues show as partial impression-restricting warnings, not outright disapprovals — invisible if you only watch campaign metrics, and they compound silently. Not every flag is urgent (e.g. "image too small for YouTube Shopping" ≠ core Shopping problem) — judgment required, but only possible if actually checking weekly.

## Pricing is the dominant ranking/conversion signal
Nothing else (title, image, GTIN) fully compensates for a price gap on an identical/near-identical product — price is visible directly in the listing before any click. Matters most when: reselling a product other retailers also carry, in a low-differentiation high-competition category, or priced dramatically (not just slightly) above median. If price is fixed (MAP/wholesale minimums): everything else in this guide matters MORE not less; a small premium is survivable with strong branding/reviews, a large one isn't; check whether specific SKUs (not the whole catalog) have margin room for a real time-limited sale. **What gets you banned:** feed price lower than actual checkout price ("price baiting"), fake sale-price with no real promotion, showing Google's crawler a different price than shoppers see — detection is reliable in 2026, not worth the risk even short-term. **What's fine:** genuine time-limited sales via the sale_price/sale_price_effective_date attributes, real pricing changes, bundles/tiers to create perceived value without base-price manipulation.

## Title mechanics — deeper than "front-load keywords"
150 char max, but the **first 30-70 characters** matter most (truncation on many placements + Google weights title-start heavier for query matching). Google can **dynamically reorder existing title keywords per searcher** (e.g. reorder to surface "Hydro Peak" first for a branded search) but cannot invent terms not present in the title — so every keyword you want surfaced must exist in the title somewhere, even if not first. Title structure varies by category (table): well-known-brand = Brand+Product+Attributes; lesser-known-brand = Benefit+Type+Use-case+Brand; apparel = Brand+Product+Gender+Color+Size; home/furniture = Keywords+Style+Color+Brand; electronics = Brand+Model+Spec+Use-case; consumables/supplements = Benefit+Type+Format+Size+Brand; beauty = Benefit+Type+Ingredients+Size+Brand. Rule: lead with what people actually search, not with brand, unless brand IS what they search — verify via the Search Terms report, not guesswork. **Case cited:** furniture store running internal SKU-style titles ("Alley Sofa Model A") found via Search Terms report that winning traffic actually searched "kid friendly sectional sofa"/"washable sectional sofa" — rewriting titles to lead with those exact phrases (no other change) produced ~10x impressions/clicks. Never: ALL CAPS, excessive punctuation, unnatural keyword-stuffing, vague titles, internal SKU/model number as the primary identifier.

## GTIN (Global Trade Item Number) — underused high-leverage attribute
Validated GTIN → better search matching (Google knows exactly what the product is, not inferring from title) + auto-correct category assignment + competitive-context awareness + eligibility for some Shopping features + a general feed-trust signal. Find GTINs via: physical barcode (UPC 12-digit US / EAN 13-digit intl), ask the manufacturer directly (best for resellers, batch), GS1 registry (gepir.gs1.org) or Open Product Data or large retailer listings, existing Amazon/marketplace catalog data. If a product genuinely has no GTIN (custom/handmade/private-label/bundle) set **"Identifier Exists" = No** explicitly — an intentional "No" is treated completely differently from a blank field (blank = data-quality issue). Private-label sellers can buy their own GS1-registered GTINs cheaply via gs1.org. Mistakes: blank field on a product that has one, fabricated/incorrect values (fails validation → disapproval), reusing a GTIN across a different variant, not updating on supplier/version switch.

## Product images — Google's built-in Product Studio (AI, free, in Merchant Center)
Distinction: **primary image** = clean white/neutral-bg compliance shot (required). **Additional images** = where lifestyle photography lives and where CTR impact comes from. Lifestyle vs plain product-only shot: **2-3x CTR lift** for categories where the product is "experienced" (furniture, bedding, apparel, outdoor gear, decor, fitness, kitchenware). Merchant Center → Creative Content → Product Studio: upload/select a product → remove background / upscale / or generate a new AI scene by describing product + desired setting → review generated variations → add directly or download. Larger visually-distinct products (furniture, rugs, bedding) generate better AI scenes than small/flat/complex-shaped items. **Critical check:** verify the AI output doesn't alter the actual product's appearance/color — a misleading generated image causes real returns/CS issues. External tools (Google AI Studio/Gemini or similar) give more control: upload base product photo + a reference lifestyle image for style/mood + a simple plain-language scene prompt (no elaborate prompt engineering needed) → review for product-accuracy → add as additional image. Prioritize: top 20-30 best-sellers first, categories where lifestyle context matters most, fix existing image-quality problems (low-res, cluttered bg) before adding lifestyle variants, always ADD not replace the primary compliance shot, run an informal A/B (some products get lifestyle images, some don't, same category) and compare CTR after 4-6 weeks before rolling out catalog-wide. Product Studio also has an experimental video-clip generator — usable for PMax/Display assets, not yet for Shopping listings themselves; quality still inconsistent, needs review.

## Descriptions — real but secondary priority
Matters far less than title/image/GTIN — the common mistake is polishing descriptions on products that still have generic titles/no lifestyle image. First ... characters aren't primary UI real estate like the title, but description content feeds Google's understanding of secondary/long-tail intent and supports the shopper post-click. Do: lead with features/benefits not brand boilerplate, weave in secondary keywords not caught by the title, write full natural sentences, cover use-cases, stay accurate. Don't: keyword-stuff, copy manufacturer/competitor copy verbatim, generic copy-that-fits-anything, ALL CAPS, unverifiable superlative claims. Rule of thumb: if a product isn't ranking/converting, the description is rarely the cause and rarely the fix — fix title/image/GTIN first.

## Product Category + Product Type (2 different fields)
**Category** = Google's 6,000+ node taxonomy; auto-assigned correctly if GTIN is valid, best-guessed from title/description otherwise — check manually for niche/cross-category products (e.g. furniture with tech components). Miscategorization silently reduces impression share on the most-relevant searches even with good title/price. **Product Type** = your own internal taxonomy field, separate from Google's — dual purpose: extra matching context for Google, AND (more practically) lets you segment campaigns/asset-groups by type for differentiated bidding, budget allocation, and category-level performance analysis. Keep it simple/consistent — unused segmentation dimensions add no value.

## Item Group ID — critical if you sell variants
Groups color/size variants of the same base product so Google treats them as one product family, not competing unrelated listings. Missing this on a variant catalog = internal auction competition + budget inefficiency between your own variants + a worse shopper UX (can't see other options in-listing) + blocks some Shopping features. High-priority fix if variants exist and this isn't set.

## Custom Labels — organizational, not a ranking signal
5 fields (Custom Label 0-4), any value, purely for campaign segmentation/filtering — doesn't affect ranking directly. Useful categories: performance-tier (Best Seller/Standard/Slow Mover/Clearance), profit-margin (High/Mid/Low/Loss-Leader), price-tier, lifecycle (New/Core/Retiring/Discontinued), seasonality, promo-status. Pick 2-3 dimensions that actually match how you run the business — over-engineering the label schema creates maintenance debt nobody keeps up.

## Supplementary attributes — completeness beats minimum-required
Feeds with full attribute sets (color, size, gender, age-group, material, pattern, brand, MPN, condition, availability, shipping, tax, item-group-id) consistently outperform minimum-field feeds even with comparable titles/images — more data = better matching. Prioritize top-sellers and category-relevant fields first (size/color for apparel, material for furniture, etc.), work through the rest progressively, use a feed tool once catalog size makes manual entry impractical.

## Campaign structure options (Standard Shopping)
Single-campaign/multiple-product-groups (simplest, best under a few hundred SKUs). Multiple campaigns by category/performance-tier (Best Sellers / Standard / New Arrivals / Clearance as separate campaigns with their own budget+bid). Priority-based structure (High/Med/Low priority + negative keywords routing branded vs category vs broad queries to different campaigns with different bids) — most control, most maintenance overhead, for experienced/agency operators. Custom-Label-driven segmentation is the practical mechanism: label top-20-30-by-revenue as "Best Seller" → dedicated campaign filtered on that label with a higher tROAS/budget → exclude those SKUs from the Standard campaign's product groups to avoid overlap.

## Standard Shopping vs Performance Max — decision table
New to Shopping/limited budget → start Standard (more visibility into what's working). Established, consistent performance → test PMax alongside Standard, don't replace outright. Large catalog/many product types → hybrid. Running PMax with a weak feed → fix the feed first (PMax leans MORE on feed quality, not less, because you have less manual control over placement). PMax also requires headline/description/image/video creative assets beyond the Shopping feed for its Search/Display/Gmail/YouTube/Discovery placements — weak creative there weakens non-Shopping performance specifically.

## Bidding strategy detail
Options: Manual CPC (new campaigns, max control) · Enhanced CPC (transition step) · Maximize Clicks (early data-gathering/awareness) · Target ROAS (mature campaigns with data) · Maximize Conversion Value (good data, ROAS-flexible, revenue-max goal). **Target ROAS needs ≥30-50 conversions in the trailing 30 days** in that specific campaign to have enough signal — below that, performance is erratic. Set tROAS at/slightly below your CURRENT actual ROAS first (an unrealistically high target restricts Google to only very-high-confidence conversions, tanking volume), then raise gradually as the algorithm proves it can hold the target at volume. **Learning period discipline:** switching bid strategy, big tROAS/CPA jumps, big budget changes, or major product-group restructuring all reset/extend a learning period — batch changes deliberately, evaluate only after 2-4 weeks of stabilization, not immediately.

## Feed management tools — when you actually need one
Threshold: manual Merchant Center editing becomes impractical past a few hundred SKUs. Tools add: bulk rule-based title rewriting, conditional attribute transforms, custom-label automation from performance data, feed scheduling/sync, multi-channel (Meta/Pinterest/Microsoft) feed management, health-monitoring alerts. **Channable** (channable.com) — mid-market, accessible UI, good multi-channel support, responsive support team; fits small-to-mid catalogs (hundreds to tens of thousands of SKUs) and agencies. **DataFeedWatch** (datafeedwatch.com) — more DIY/technical, cheaper, strong rule-based transforms, active user community; fits budget-conscious technical operators. **Enterprise managed feed services** — a team manages the feed for you; fits very large catalogs (tens-hundreds of thousands of SKUs) with significant ad spend. You do NOT need a tool to start — everything in this guide is doable natively in Merchant Center; tools solve the bottleneck once manual work is genuinely too slow, not before.

## 17-item priority checklist (execution order)
Critical: (1) weekly Merchant Center diagnostics, (2) pricing audit vs top competitors. High: (3) title rewrite front-loaded on top sellers, (4) pull Search Terms report into titles, (5) verify/add GTINs, (6-7) lifestyle images for top 20-30 SKUs as additional images, (8) Item Group IDs for variant products. Medium: (9) description optimization after title/image are done, (10) verify category assignment, (11) Product Type structure, (12) Custom Labels for segmentation, (13) supplementary attributes by category relevance, (14) campaign structure review, (15) bidding strategy evaluation, (16) test PMax alongside Standard. When-needed: (17) evaluate feed tooling once catalog exceeds ~500 SKUs.
