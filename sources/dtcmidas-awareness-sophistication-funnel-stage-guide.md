If you run ads or write copy, you've probably heard these terms a hundred times: awareness levels, sophistication levels & funnel stages.

But I see them get thrown around interchangeably, and a lot of marketeers don't understand what they actually mean or how to apply them.

That's also why so many can't recreate a winning ad, while the best copywriters use these three to ship banger after banger.

Because they're not interchangeable. They describe three completely different dimensions, and you need all three to write ads and copy that convert.

The fastest way to keep them apart is to look at what each one is a property of:

- **Awareness** describes a person's relationship to a problem.
- **Sophistication** describes a market's relationship to claims.
- **Funnel stage** describes a person's relationship to your brand.

By the end of this article you'll be able to place any customer on all three dimensions and know exactly what your ad needs to say to them.

Let's go through each one, then look at how they combine.

## 1\. Awareness level

Awareness tells you how much someone knows about their problem and the possible solutions. It runs from unaware, to problem aware, to solution aware, to product aware, to most aware.

Take sleep as an example. "I'm always tired" is problem aware: they feel the pain but haven't connected it to any solution yet. "Magnesium could fix my sleep" is solution aware: they know a type of fix exists, but they haven't picked a product. "I want brand X's magnesium, I'm just waiting for a discount" is most aware: there's nothing left to convince them of, they just need a reason to act now.

**Why it matters:** the lower the awareness, the more education you need to do before you introduce your product. Pitch a discount to someone who doesn't even know they have the problem and you're skipping every step of the conversation.

## 2\. Sophistication level

Sophistication tells you how many similar promises a market has already heard. This one is not about an individual person. It's the scar tissue of the entire market.

In a fresh market, a simple claim is enough to sell. The first weight loss ads just said "Now! Lose ugly fat!" and that headline alone sold.

Thousands of diets and pills later, the exact same promise gets scrolled past. Everyone has heard it, nobody believes it anymore. That's the psychological effect of sophistication: skepticism, resistance, and buyers who research a lot more before they trust anyone.

At high sophistication, claims stop working and you need proof and a new mechanism. GLP-1s are the perfect example. They're selling like crazy in the most burned-out market on earth, because they bring a completely new mechanism (a hormone that shuts off your appetite), clinical proof, and results people can see with their own eyes.

## 3\. Funnel stage

Top of Funnel (TOF) means they never met you. Middle of Funnel (MOF) means they know you but aren't sold yet. Bottom of Funnel (BOF) means they're close to buying.

Notice what's missing here: funnel stage says nothing about how much someone knows about the problem, the solutions, or the category. It only tells you how warm they are to YOUR brand.

## How they combine

Because these are separate dimensions, they combine in ways most marketers get wrong. Here are 2 examples:

**Most aware + low sophistication**

Imagine you're a startup that invented the world's first consumer teleportation device. There are no other teleportation companies on earth.

Consumers have never seen an ad for this, so there is zero market skepticism to fight through (there can still be skepticism due to novelty, people doubting the tech is even possible, but nobody has been burned by a competitor's broken promises).

Meanwhile a tech enthusiast has been following your development updates for six months. He knows your brand, he knows the device, and he's just waiting for you to drop the pre-order link. This is a Most aware buyer in sophistication level 1 market.

**Solution aware + top of funnel + high sophistication**

Someone gets terrible hangovers and knows they need an electrolyte powder. They have just never heard of Liquid IV. And the market is crowded, dozens of brands making the same claim: "we hydrate you fast". Solution aware, TOF for the brand, and a sophistication level where the claim alone convinces nobody.

## How to use this

So before you write a single word of your next ad, ask three questions.

**How aware is the reader?** That sets how much education comes before the pitch.

**How sophisticated is the market?** That sets how much proof you need and whether you need a new mechanism.

**How warm are they to my brand?** That sets whether you introduce yourself or push an offer.

So before you write a single word of your next ad, place your customer on all three dimensions.

If you're able to align these 3 correctly, you'll have a message that will make you a lot of money.
