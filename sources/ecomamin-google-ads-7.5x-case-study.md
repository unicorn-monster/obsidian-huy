Account-architecture case study (pet grooming brand, UK, multi-product-line: dog/horse/pet/outdoor wash). Core thesis: the 740% revenue lift over 90 days came entirely from account STRUCTURE reflecting strategic decisions instead of being delegated to Google's algorithm — no new tactic, no new campaign type, no more spend.

## The 3 structural problems diagnosed
1. **PMax-heavy account.** PMax gives Google's algorithm broad control over placement/budget/audience. The core critique: "google handles the distribution" is not neutral — it means the algorithm chases whatever's easiest to convert (branded queries, retargeting, low-funnel searches that would've converted anyway), NOT what's most profitable or most aligned with strategic priority. Not a flaw in PMax itself — a flaw in treating PMax as a complete strategy instead of one tool within a designed architecture.
2. **One ROAS target across incompatible product lines.** Dog grooming, horse grooming, pet wash, outdoor wash, home grooming tools have completely different customers, search behavior, purchase frequency, AOV, competitive landscape — forcing one shared ROAS target makes the algorithm route budget toward whichever line hits the target fastest, not whichever line matters most strategically or has the best margin/LTV.
3. **Keywords organized around internal product names, not customer search language.** Real example: ad groups named "home wall hot shower" / "portable tank shower" / "soap gun" — nobody searches those phrases; real buyers search "best outdoor dog wash station" / "portable dog grooming shower". Mismatch causes two compounding losses: missing the queries buyers actually use, AND attracting tangentially-related queries representing a totally different intent stage into the same ad group/bid/landing page.

## The rebuild — core philosophy
"Your Google Ads account should be built to tell Google what to do, not to let Google figure out what to do." Google's algorithm optimizes brilliantly within constraints a human sets — it does not decide which customer segments matter most, which product line deserves priority investment, or which intent category is the highest-margin acquisition opportunity. Those are human calls that must be encoded into account architecture, with automation operating inside those boundaries.

## Tiered Shopping structure (the single biggest lever)
Instead of one Shopping campaign spanning the whole catalog (which the algorithm naturally skews toward whatever's easiest to hit ROAS with, usually already-popular/lower-margin items), split into 3 campaigns by performance tier, each with its own budget + own ROAS target + independent bidding:
- **Tier 1 — Best Sellers:** proven converters, highest search volume, largest budget, ROAS target calibrated for volume-at-acceptable-margin (not max efficiency). Result in this account: 3.38% CVR, £15,831 conversion value, £0.64 avg CPC.
- **Tier 2 — Growth Products:** strong potential but not yet proven at volume; slightly higher (more conservative) ROAS target than Tier 1 so the algorithm doesn't burn budget on unvalidated items, but still meaningfully funded so they can graduate to Tier 1. Result: 4.63% CVR (higher than Tier 1 — see note below), £8,731 value, £0.46 avg CPC.
- **Tier 3 — Long Tail:** low-but-consistent-search catalog depth, high ROAS target + modest budget so it only spends when conditions are favorable and doesn't compete with Tiers 1-2. Result: 4.18% CVR, £0.56 avg CPC.
- **Why Tier 2 out-converts Tier 1 (counterintuitive but consistent pattern):** Tier 1's higher volume/higher competitiveness pulls in a broader range of buyer-intent stages including earlier-stage lookers; Tier 2's narrower, more specific searches come from buyers who researched more and are closer to decision. Lesson: highest-volume campaigns aren't automatically highest-converting, and the strategic job is balancing volume/CVR/revenue across tiers, not maximizing any one dimension.
- **Custom Labels are the mechanism** that makes tiering possible — without them the tiered structure literally cannot be built; assign labels by margin-tier/performance-tier/seasonal-relevance etc. and build campaigns that target specific label values.

## PMax restructure — segment by CUSTOMER TYPE, not product name
Original: one broad PMax spanning nearly every category with minimal audience signals. Rebuild: separate PMax campaigns per customer-type/washing-category (dog wash, horse wash, pet wash, outdoor wash, home-wall wash), each with its own budget, its own tailored asset group, its own calibrated ROAS target. **Why this matters more than it sounds:** when one asset pool mixes dog-wash messaging with horse-wash messaging with home-wall-shower messaging, Google's dynamic ad assembly can't produce a maximally-relevant combination for ANY specific query — it produces "generically adequate for both rather than specifically compelling for either." Segmented pools let each campaign's creative stay tight and highly relevant to its specific intent signal. Budgets after rebuild ranged £19.00/day (pet wash) to £51.75/day (branded PMax).

**Isolate branded traffic into its own PMax campaign.** Unsegmented PMax with access to branded queries will often credit conversions from people who were already going to buy, inflating reported ROAS and creating a false read on how well PMax performs on actual non-branded acquisition. Isolating branded traffic lets you see the true split between "already-loyal" revenue and "genuinely new" revenue — critical for deciding where incremental budget should go.

## Keyword restructure — 3-dimension customer-intent framework (replaces product-taxonomy grouping)
1. **Customer type** — who is searching and their relationship to the product (e.g. B2B stable-manager buying professional horse-wash equipment vs. a consumer buying a portable dog-wash station for personal use) — different language, different queries, different converting landing page.
2. **Purchase-intent stage** — where in the decision journey the query sits (e.g. "best portable dog wash station" = active evaluation; "how to wash a dog at home" = early-stage, not ready, should NOT get the same bid or same landing page).
3. **Use-case specificity** — how precisely the query names the need (e.g. "outdoor dog grooming wash station with water tank" = knows exactly what they want vs. "dog grooming equipment" = early awareness).
Organizing keyword groups across these 3 axes (instead of by internal product name) lets bids/landing-pages/ad-copy match the actual customer situation instead of forcing every intent stage through one generic campaign.

## Differentiated ROAS targets — why one number across an account is a structural error
Each product exists in its own economic context: AOV, gross margin, repeat-purchase rate, customer LTV, competitive landscape. A £300-AOV/65%-margin/4x-annual-repeat product and a £40-AOV/35%-margin/single-purchase product cannot share a ROAS target — the ROAS at which each is actually profitable is a different number. Forcing a shared target makes the algorithm chase whichever product hits the number fastest (not the better economics), systematically under-investing the high-LTV line and over-investing the low-LTV line. Fix: set each shopping tier's / PMax segment's ROAS target from that segment's real unit economics, not a single account-wide aspiration.

**Advanced technique — LTV-weighted conversion value:** Google's bidding optimizes toward the conversion VALUE you feed it, which by default is just the transaction price — it has no visibility into lifetime value at bid-decision time. If a professional/B2B buyer segment has 3x the LTV of a one-time consumer segment, set that segment's tracked conversion value at 3x the transaction value so the algorithm is explicitly told to pay more for those conversions even at a lower immediate ROAS. Requires solid LTV data to implement correctly, but it's the correct strategic frame whenever customer segments have meaningfully different lifetime value.

## Merchant Center feed = "a campaign before the campaigns"
Feed quality determines shopping traffic quality before any bid or budget decision happens — it's the data layer Google uses to decide which queries you're even eligible to appear for. Concrete example from the case: "professional mobile pet washing station with integrated water tank, pump, and drainage, suitable for outdoor use with dogs up to 80lbs" matches a far broader/more-relevant query set than "portable tank shower pro." Highest-impact feed elements in order: **titles** (lead with the customer's actual language, not internal product naming) → **product type + Google product category** (as specific/granular as possible; product type should reflect customer-facing categories, not internal catalog structure) → **custom labels** (the tiering mechanism — impossible to build the tiered structure above without them) → **image quality** (clean/well-lit/neutral-bg images directly lift CTR — this is one of the most common and cheapest-to-fix leaks) → **pricing/availability accuracy** (inconsistent data risks disapproval/deprioritization). Feed is NOT set-and-forget — it needs ongoing maintenance as the catalog/prices/seasonality shift, or the initial cleanup gains decay back toward default.

## Landing page / CRO — "the free lever nobody spends equal time on"
Conversion rate is the multiplier on every other metric — doubling it doubles revenue at the same spend without touching the ad account at all. A 50% traffic-quality improvement into a 1.5%-converting page produces a fraction of the revenue impact of the same improvement into a 4%-converting page. The engagement included a full CRO audit delivered as a specific improvement checklist (not vague advice) — argued as a necessary companion to any account rebuild since campaign structure and landing-page quality are multiplicative, not independent, levers.

## Mental-model shift
Wrong-but-common frame: "Google Ads is a traffic channel, my job is cheap clicks + a converting landing page" → delegates the actual strategic questions (which customers, which products, what budget split) to whoever answers them fastest, which in a PMax-heavy account is Google's algorithm by default. Correct frame: "Google Ads is a customer-acquisition SYSTEM, my job is to design its architecture so it acquires the specific customers most valuable to my business, at volumes calibrated to my economics and priorities." Structural decisions (who, at what intent stage, for which products, with what budget weight relative to customer value) are the primary lever; tactics (bids, copy, LP polish) operate inside whatever structure exists and can't overcome a misaligned one.

**Why Google's own recommendations often point the wrong way for sophisticated multi-product accounts:** the recommendations tab / optimization score pushes toward consolidation, broad match, PMax, and fewer constraints — genuinely good average advice for a single-product/small-budget/no-segmentation advertiser, actively bad for a multi-line brand with real strategic priorities. A strategically-built account that deliberately limits PMax scope, runs tiered ROAS targets, and uses exact-match intent control will often show a LOWER optimization score — that's a feature of doing the opposite of "algorithmic defaults," not a bug.

## 5-question diagnostic framework (apply to any account)
1. How much of your Google Ads revenue is from people who were already going to buy from you? (isolate branded search + branded PMax + retargeting from the blended ROAS — the real cold-acquisition number is usually much weaker than the blended number suggests.)
2. Is your ROAS target the same across product lines with different economics? (if yes, you're over-investing in easy-to-convert lines and under-investing in your best-economics lines.)
3. Are keyword groups organized around how customers search, or how the business internally describes its products? (internal SKU/technical-spec language in ad group names is the tell.)
4. Are meaningfully different customer types within one category being served the same campaigns/bids/landing pages? (a B2B facility buyer vs. a consumer buying for personal use should never share a target.)
5. What's the actual quality of your Merchant Center feed — built from customer search language, or from an unoptimized product export? (compare feed titles against the real triggering search-terms report to see the gap directly.)

## 6-phase rebuild sequence (order matters because of Google's post-change learning periods)
1. Design the FULL post-rebuild architecture on paper first (campaigns, ROAS targets, budget splits, keyword-intent framework) before touching the live account — avoids the common failure mode of reactive incremental patchwork changes that never cohere into a real design.
2. Merchant Center feed optimization first, because feed quality caps traffic quality for everything built on top of it.
3. Launch the new tiered Shopping structure ALONGSIDE the old one initially (don't cut over cold) so new campaigns can accumulate conversion history before the old structure is paused — avoids a hard performance dip during transition.
4. PMax restructure (segment by customer type), done after Shopping is established so PMax has real performance context on which segments/categories are working.
5. Search keyword restructure — usually the longest phase since it requires real search-behavior research, not just reorganizing an existing list.
6. Ongoing monitoring/adjustment — expand winners, mine new intent patterns from search-term data, adjust ROAS targets as real economics clarify, keep the feed current as SKUs are added.

## Honest caveats on the result
The 740% lift is a function of how WIDE the pre-existing structural gap was in this specific account/market — not a universal multiplier. It does not mean PMax is always wrong or tiered-Shopping is always right; the specific architecture must match the specific business's product mix/customer profile/competitive landscape (only the principles transfer, not the exact config). And the rebuild is explicitly framed as "the beginning of a strategically managed account, not the completion of a project" — feed maintenance, ROAS recalibration, and CRO work continue indefinitely; structural improvements compound over 6-12 months more than they show in any single 90-day snapshot.
