## The 48 Laws of Google Ads (Ecommerce Edition)

#### 1) Track reality, not vanity

What it means: Optimize only to a verified Purchase with value; log micro‑events (ATC, checkout) as observed. If you optimize to shallow events, Smart Bidding will chase cheap clicks that don’t pay.

How to apply: In Google Ads, mark Purchase as Primary and everything else as Secondary. Deduplicate GA4 vs. Ads tags. Turn on Enhanced Conversions and test with Tag Assistant until purchase value and order IDs are consistent.

#### 2) Bid to profit, not revenue

What it means: ROAS targets must reflect margins today and LTV tomorrow. If you can’t pipe profit, adjust tROAS to beat break‑even by your required contribution.

How to apply: Pass profit as value (Revenue − COGS − shipping − fees) or use value rules/custom columns to weight. For prospecting, add an NCA uplift that approximates first‑order LTV.

#### 3) Run Brand Search as your moat

What it means: Own your name. Brand clicks are cheap, high‑intent, and prevent competitor conquesting.

How to apply: Separate Brand‑Exact with its own budget and ≥95% impression share. Use sitelinks (Top Categories, Best Sellers, Support), callouts (Free Returns), and exclude brand from all prospecting (PMax, non‑brand Search, Shopping) using brand exclusions/negatives.

#### 4) Split prospecting and remarketing

What it means: Acquisition and harvest require different bids, creatives, and frequency. Mixing them blurs CAC and strangles scale.

How to apply: Prospecting excludes purchasers and recent visitors; remarketing runs 7/30/90‑day windows with tailored creative and frequency caps. Use separate budgets and targets.

#### 5) Prefer simple structures with clear intent

What it means: Too many campaigns starve learning; too few erase control.

How to apply (baseline): 1 Brand Search, 1 Non‑Brand Search, Standard Shopping (Brand), Standard Shopping (Non‑Brand), PMax Hero, PMax Tail, 1 YouTube/Demand Gen prospecting, 1 dedicated remarketing (Display/Video). Add only when there’s a proven control gap.

#### 6) Use Data‑Driven Attribution (DDA)

What it means: DDA evaluates the full path (including Video views and Shopping assists). Last‑click under‑credits upper funnel and punishes scale.

How to apply: Switch account to DDA. Expect ROAS to look different than last‑click; judge channels via incrementality and MER (Law 10).

#### 7) Start with Max Conversion Value, graduate to tROAS

What it means: Let the system learn where value lives before constraining with a target.

How to apply: Use Max Conv. Value until you hit ≥30–50 conversions/week. Then set tROAS modestly (10–15% above current) and adjust only every 7–14 days.

#### 8) Weight new customers properly

What it means: If first‑order LTV exceeds AOV, pay more for first purchases.

How to apply: In PMax and eligible campaigns, enable use a NEW CUSTOMER ACQUISITION GOOGLE ADS TAG: [https://support.google.com/google-ads/answer/12077475?hl=en](https://support.google.com/google-ads/answer/12077475?hl=en)

#### 9) Fortify your signals

What it means: Modeled conversions still need quality first‑party data.

How to apply: Enable Enhanced Conversions, upload Customer Match regularly, and move to server‑side tagging if possible. Import delayed/offline revenue if orders settle later.

#### 10) Validate with incrementality (MER & holdouts)

What it means: Platform numbers ≠ total business impact.

How to apply: Track MER = Total Revenue / Total Ad Spend at least weekly. It’s unbelievably important to test the true lift that Search, Shopping, P MAX, and Youtube Ads provides individually.

### Feed & Merchant Center (Your Shopping Fuel)

#### 11) Engineer titles for search intent

What it means: Titles drive Shopping visibility and CTR more than brand copy.

How to apply: Lead your titles with search terms that are converting well for you in your google ads account. If you don’t have any winners yet, then duplicate your product listings and test multiple titles with different keywords leading the title.

#### 12) Always provide GTINs

What it means: GTINs unlock better matching, price insights, and rich placements.

How to apply: Audit “Identifier exists.” Fix with manufacturer data; where no GTIN exists (custom products), mark accordingly but keep data rich.

#### 13) Use conversion‑ready images

What it means: Images sell the click. Compliance issues quietly throttle impressions.

How to apply: 1200×1200 or larger, clean background, no watermarks/overlays, variant‑specific images, plus lifestyle images for PMax asset groups.

#### 14) Win with price & promos

What it means: Price competitiveness and sale annotations are material CTR levers.

How to apply: Use Sale price, Merchant Promotions, and enable Automatic Item Updates. Monitor Price Competitiveness and adjust where you’re persistently uncompetitive.

#### 15) Segment with custom labels for control

What it means: Bidding to the mean hides outliers.

How to apply:

label0: price\_tier (0–25, 25–50, 50–100, 100+)

label1: margin (low/med/high)

label2: role (hero/evergreen/seasonal/clearance)

label3: lifecycle (new/growth/mature/sunset)

label4: inventory\_risk (overstock/normal/low)

#### 16) Treat diagnostics like uptime

What it means: Disapprovals and mismatched shipping/tax silently cap scale.

How to apply: Weekly Merchant Center sweep. Fix policy issues fast; ensure shipping speeds and costs match your site.

### Search (Brand & Non‑Brand)

#### 17) Brand Search is insurance + revenue

What it means: It protects your SERP, converts cheap traffic, and stabilizes MER.

How to apply: Exact match, conservative RSA pinning (headline 1 with brand), full set of assets, and ≥95% impression share. Keep a shared negative list to prevent accidental non‑brand creep.

#### 18) Scale non‑brand with Broad Match + Smart Bidding

What it means: Broad + DDA discovers high‑intent traffic you won’t hand‑pick.

How to apply: Start with Max Conversion Value, then tROAS/tCPA once stable. Seed audiences (Customer Match, remarketing) as signals—not restrictions.

#### 19) Sculpt queries with negatives

What it means: Broad isn’t sloppy if your negatives are sharp.

How to apply: Shared lists for “jobs/free/DIY/used/manual/repair.” Add brand negatives to non‑brand Search. Review search terms weekly and iterate.

#### 20) RSAs should sell, not just score “Excellent”

What it means: Ad strength matters, but message‑market match converts.

How to apply: 8–12 headlines (3 with core keyword), 4 descriptions, minimal pinning except for legal/compliance. Two RSAs per ad group; rotate hooks/offers quarterly.

#### 21) Max out assets (extensions)

What it means: Assets raise CTR and qualify traffic.

How to apply: Sitelinks (Top Categories), Price assets (grid), Promotion (sale windows), Structured Snippets (materials/types), Image assets. Keep them fresh during promos.

#### 22) Use geo controls as a profit lever

What it means: Unit economics vary by location.

How to apply: Break out top states/DMAs when performance diverges; set separate budgets/targets. Use store shipping SLAs and tax differences as a guide.

### Standard Shopping (Explicitly including Brand & Non‑Brand)

#### 23) Run Standard Shopping alongside PMax—on purpose

What it means: Standard Shopping gives query control via negatives and priority settings; PMax offers reach and automation. Used together, you get control where you need it and scale where you don’t.

How to apply: Decide which SKUs live in Standard vs. PMax using item filters (custom labels). Use Standard for lines where query intent and margin control really matter; keep PMax for discovery and coverage.

#### 24) Use campaign priorities to route intent

What it means: Priorities (High/Medium/Low) determine which Shopping campaign enters the auction first. You can “ladder” traffic based on query intent.

How to apply:

High‑priority Non‑Brand with brand negatives to catch generic/category queries.

Medium‑priority Category (optional) for mid‑intent terms; add competitor and low‑intent negatives.

Low‑priority Brand with no brand negatives to capture brand queries.

Pair with budgets and targets that reflect conversion probability (brand can accept tighter tROAS, non‑brand looser for volume).

#### 25) Separate Brand and Non‑Brand Standard Shopping

What it means: Mixing brand demand into non‑brand hides true CAC and forces wrong bidding.

How to apply: Two campaigns minimum: Standard Shopping – Brand (Low priority, no brand negatives) and Standard Shopping – Non‑Brand (High priority, brand negatives). Consider a third Category campaign to isolate top categories with unique targets.

#### 26) Build granular product groups where it matters

What it means: You don’t need item‑ID granularity for everything—only for SKUs that swing results.

How to apply: Subdivide by Item ID for hero/high‑margin lines; keep evergreen or long‑tail grouped by custom labels (margin/price tier/role). Exclude chronic underperformers and sink their exposure to Tail/PMax with stricter tROAS.

#### 27) Smart bidding beats manual CPC in Shopping

What it means: The auction uses signals no human can adjust for in time (device, query, audience, context). Manual CPC drifts to the wrong impressions.

How to apply: Use Max Conversion Value → tROAS. Set budgets so each Standard campaign can hit ≥30–50 conversions/week. Use device/geo inventory separation only when you’ve proven extreme deltas; otherwise let Smart Bidding do the work.

What it means: Shopping reveals high‑intent queries you didn’t target in Search.

How to apply: Weekly, export Shopping search terms, add the best to Non‑Brand Search as keywords, and block the worst with negatives. Update custom segments in YouTube/Demand Gen with the winners.

#### 29) Guard against PMax cannibalization—SKU routing

What it means: PMax often wins Shopping auctions by design. If you want Standard to carry specific SKUs, keep them out of PMax.

How to apply: In PMax, use listing group filters (custom labels/Item IDs) to exclude SKUs assigned to Standard Shopping. Alternatively, run PMax with higher tROAS and Standard with lower tROAS so Standard wins Shopping‑intent impressions for its SKUs.

### Performance Max (PMax)

#### 30) Treat PMax as a Shopping‑led discovery engine

What it means: PMax is at its best when your feed and conversion signals are excellent. It will blend Shopping, Search, Display, and Video to find value.

How to apply: Launch after Laws 11–16 are solid. Provide strong asset groups, audience signals, and videos (Law 33) to avoid auto‑generated creative.

#### 31) Map asset groups to collections/intent

What it means: Message‑to‑market match raises relevance and CTR.

How to apply: One asset group per major category or collection. Filter items by custom labels so creative aligns with the products shown. Send traffic to collection or category landing pages.

#### 32) Use audience signals as hints, not shackles

What it means: Signals speed learning but don’t hard‑limit reach.

How to apply: Include Customer Match, cart abandoners, and custom segments built from your top Search queries and competitor URLs. Monitor Search Terms Insights and feed winners into Search/Shopping structures.

#### 33) Upload your own videos

What it means: Auto‑generated videos underperform and can misrepresent your brand.

How to apply: Provide at least one landscape and one square video per asset group. Show product, proof, and offer within the first 10–15 seconds.

#### 34) Keep brand demand out of PMax (unless by design)

What it means: Allowing brand into PMax will make it look miraculous while hiding true prospecting CAC.

How to apply: Use Brand Exclusions and run Brand Search/Shopping separately. If you choose to merge, do it consciously and measure with holdouts (Law 10).

#### 35) Prioritize hero SKUs in their own PMax

What it means: Budgets drift to easy volume; your bestselling lines deserve explicit funding.

How to apply: Run a PMax – Hero with lower tROAS (or Max Conversion Value) and a PMax – Tail with higher tROAS. Exclude poor performers from Hero; let Tail carry experimentation.

#### 36) Read the Insights tab like a strategist

What it means: Search categories, audience, and demand shifts are your roadmap.

How to apply: Promote persistent high‑value queries into Search; block irrelevant themes with negatives/brand exclusions; adjust creative to match emerging Search Category Insights.

### YouTube & Demand Gen (Performance Video)

#### 37) Win the first 3 seconds; prove value by second 7

What it means: Viewers decide instantly. Show the outcome, then the product.

How to apply: Script structure: Hook (call out the problem/benefit), Proof (demo/UGC), Offer (price/promo), CTA. Caption everything. Keep 15–45 seconds; test multiple hooks.

#### 38) Make video shoppable with feeds

What it means: Reducing the path to product increases conversion.

How to apply: Use Video Action or Demand Gen with your Merchant Center feed attached. Optimize to Conversions/Conversion Value, not views.

#### 39) Target with first‑party + intent

What it means: Your CRM and site behavior outclass generic interests.

How to apply: Customer Match (tiered by value), website audiences, and custom segments built from your top Search queries and competitor/category URLs. Layer in in‑market audiences where you see lift.

#### 40) Use Smart Bidding for video with guardrails

What it means: Start broad, then apply tCPA/tROAS once you have signal density.

How to apply: Begin with Max Conversions; shift to tCPA after ≥30 weekly conversions. Make sure the selected conversion is Purchase (not “Engagement”). Keep Engaged‑View Conversions enabled for proper DDA credit.

#### 41) Batch creative tests, don’t trickle

What it means: The algorithm needs choice; your team needs clear winners.

How to apply: Launch 3–5 distinct hooks at once per product line. Refresh every 3–4 weeks. Mix polished and UGC. Archive losers quickly; scale winners into PMax assets.

### Audiences & First‑Party Data

#### 42) Customer Match with value tiers

What it means: Spend history predicts propensity; Smart Bidding can weight it.

How to apply: Build High/Medium/Low CLV lists; apply Value Rules (+20% for VIP, −10% for low value). Update weekly.

#### 43) RFM lifecycle programs outperform generic retargeting

What it means: Recency/Frequency/Monetary segments let you customize offers and frequency.

How to apply:

Winback (90–180 days) with re‑activation offer,

VIP with exclusives/new drops,

One‑and‑done with risk‑reduction messaging (fit guide, reviews).

#### 44) “New customers only” means exclusions everywhere

What it means: Prospecting CAC inflates when repeat buyers leak in.

How to apply: Exclude purchasers (365 days) from prospecting in Search, Standard Shopping Non‑Brand, PMax, and YouTube. Turn on NCA where supported and track new‑customer share.

### Bidding, Budgets & Change Management

#### 45) Fund to the math and avoid “Limited by budget”

What it means: Starved budgets prevent learning and force poor auction positions.

How to apply: For tCPA, set daily budget 10–20× target CPA. For tROAS, ensure enough budget to hit ≥30–50 purchases/week per campaign. Reallocate weekly to winners; kill the rest.

#### 46) Change one dial at a time and respect learning

What it means: Big or simultaneous changes reset models and muddy causality.

How to apply: Adjust budget or target or structure—not all three. Make 10–15% target moves, then wait 7–14 days or 3 conversion cycles before judging.

### Landing Experience, Experimentation & Policy

#### 47) Message‑match your landing pages and run real experiments

What it means: Ad promise must equal page reality; otherwise the algorithm learns the wrong lesson.

How to apply: Send PMax/Shopping to collection pages that mirror the asset group theme. Keep load speed fast, variants in stock, returns clear, reviews visible. Use Experiments with 80/20 splits, one hypothesis at a time, and run 2–4 weeks minimum.

#### 48) Policy & brand safety are growth levers, not chores

What it means: Nothing sells when a campaign is disapproved. Proactive compliance prevents costly outages.

How to apply: Maintain a policy checklist (restricted claims, medical/cosmetic language, promos). Set brand‑safe inventory for video. Sweep Merchant Center Diagnostics weekly. Fix disapprovals immediately.

Low priority, no brand negatives → brand and SKU terms.
