## why most brands' Shopping setup is actively suppressing their best products

before getting into what the best Shopping setups look like, you need to understand what the average one is actually doing.

most auto-generated Shopping feeds share three problems that compound on each other:

**the title problem:**

Google uses the product title as the primary signal to determine which searches trigger your Shopping listing. it's essentially a keyword field, even though it looks like a product name.

most Shopify-generated titles look like this: "Brand Name Magnesium Complex 500mg 120 Capsules"

the brand name is occupying the first 30-40% of the characters Google indexes most heavily. someone searching "magnesium glycinate for sleep" may never see the listing because the title doesn't contain those words in the positions that matter most.

the correct title: "Magnesium Glycinate Sleep Supplement 500mg 120 Capsules | Brand Name"

the first 60 characters are indexed most heavily. they should contain the highest-converting non-branded search terms that describe what the product does and who it's for, not the brand name that a cold prospect doesn't know yet.

**the image problem:**

the shopping feed image is competing for attention in a grid of listings. if every brand in your category is using a white background product shot, every listing looks identical. the buyer's eye has no reason to stop on yours specifically.

more on this in depth below. but the point here: the default image strategy produces default results.

**the structure problem:**

one campaign with all products, same bid, no segmentation. this means Google's algorithm allocates budget according to its own priorities, which typically means the highest-conversion-rate products get most of the spend regardless of margin, and low-margin high-volume products eat budget that should be going to your most profitable SKUs.

## the feed: where Shopping wins are actually decided

the product feed is the engine of every Shopping campaign. campaigns, bids, and budget allocation are the variables. the feed is the foundation.

everything below can be optimised at the feed level, before a single campaign setting is touched.

**title architecture: benefit-rich keywords over boring SEO strings**

the instinct in most ecom teams is to optimise Shopping titles for search volume first and nothing else. high-volume keyword in position one, secondary keyword in position two, done.

the problem: high-volume keywords are also high-competition. and a title built purely for search volume often strips out the emotional and benefit language that makes someone click your listing over the one next to it.

the best Shopping titles do both.

they contain the high-value search keywords that get the listing triggered - these need to be in the title, full stop, or the listing doesn't appear. but they also contain the benefit language that makes the click happen once the listing appears.

"Magnesium Glycinate Sleep Supplement | Deep Sleep Formula | 120 Capsules" beats "Magnesium Supplement 500mg 120ct" because it answers the question the buyer is actually asking - will this do the specific thing I need it to do? - rather than just describing what's in the capsule.

the rule: keyword accuracy gets you seen. benefit clarity gets you clicked.

for products where the mechanism is the differentiator - supplements, skincare, fitness equipment - lead with the outcome, not the ingredient list.

"Hyaluronic Acid Firming Serum for Visible Results in 14 Days" outperforms "Hyaluronic Acid Serum 30ml" in CTR in almost every test, because the first version tells the buyer something the ingredient label doesn't.

**the description field: don't waste it on inventory copy**

the Shopping description field rarely displays in standard Shopping results, but it matters for two things: Google's relevance scoring, and Shopping ads that appear in more expanded formats.

use it for secondary keywords that didn't fit cleanly in the title, plus any credibility signals that increase relevance - certifications, materials, sourcing, the specific benefit mechanism, compatibility information.

don't write product descriptions that read like a warehouse catalogue. write them like ad copy, because in some placements, they function as ad copy.

**custom labels: the segmentation tool nobody uses properly**

custom labels are five additional fields you can populate in the product feed and then use as segmentation criteria in campaign structure.

most brands either don't touch them or use one label for "bestsellers" and call it a day.

the brands printing with Shopping use custom labels to do the following:

- margin tier: label A for high-margin products, label B for mid-margin, label C for low-margin. this lets the campaign structure bid aggressively on high-margin SKUs without subsidising low-margin volume with the same budget.
- performance tier: label bestsellers separately from new products separately from slow-movers. each tier has different bid logic. bestsellers can afford aggressive bids because the conversion data validates it. new products need a testing budget, not maximum exposure. slow-movers either get low bids or get excluded.
- seasonality flag: products that see seasonal demand spikes get flagged so promotional budget can shift toward them during peak periods without manually restructuring the campaign.
- promotional status: products currently running a sale or promotional price get labelled so Shopping campaigns can front-load their exposure while the price advantage is active.

the practical result of proper custom label use: the algorithm stops making budget decisions based on its own priorities and starts executing yours.

## the image: the most underused CTR lever in any Shopping account

the Shopping feed is a visual marketplace. the prospect is looking at a grid of listings from multiple brands simultaneously and making a decision about which one to click in under two seconds.

in that context, your product image is doing the majority of the work.

white background images are the default across most categories because they're easy to produce, compliant with Merchant Center policies, and they accurately show the product. they also look like every other listing in the feed, which means they generate average CTR at best.

the brands winning the click are thinking about contrast first.

**contrast in the feed:**

if the majority of listings in your category use white backgrounds, a lifestyle image will visually pop. if the majority use lifestyle images, a clean studio shot against a coloured background stands out.

the goal is to be the listing that looks different from everything around it. not different for the sake of it - different in a way that also communicates quality, desirability, and category relevance.

the easiest way to identify the contrast opportunity: manually search your highest-volume Shopping queries and screenshot the results page. look at the 10 listings appearing. what colour palette dominates? what image style dominates? what's the visual gap?

that gap is where your image strategy should sit.

**emotional vs functional imagery:**

a product shot shows what the product looks like.

a lifestyle shot shows what the buyer's life looks like when they have the product.

for categories where the outcome is the desire - fitness, skincare, sleep, wellness, home, pet - the lifestyle image typically generates meaningfully higher CTR than the product shot because it sells the transformation, not the object.

someone searching "deep sleep supplement" and clicking through Shopping results isn't looking for a picture of a bottle. they're imagining waking up rested. the image that most vividly shows them that outcome gets the click.

this doesn't mean every Shopping listing needs a lifestyle image. categories where product specifications drive purchase decisions - electronics, tools, specific apparel sizes - often see better results with clean product shots because the buyer wants to see exactly what they're getting. the principle is match the image to what the buyer wants to feel, not what makes the brand feel most professional.

**practical implementation:**

Merchant Center policies allow lifestyle images that show the product being used in a real context. you're allowed people, environments, and context. what you can't do is add promotional text overlaid on the image, use before/after comparisons in the Shopping image, or use graphics that obscure the product.

test both image styles in Experiments if you're on a Standard Shopping campaign. for PMax, upload both image types as assets and let the algorithm determine which performs better in Shopping placements. monitor the asset performance report after two to three weeks.

## social proof signals in the feed: the trust layer that costs nothing to improve

Google can display star ratings and review counts directly on your Shopping listing if your Merchant Center account is eligible and your product feed is set up correctly.

a listing with 4.7 stars from 847 reviews looks categorically different from an identical product listing with no rating displayed. the buyer sees authority signal before they read a single word of the title.

this matters because Shopping is a trust-compressed decision environment. the buyer is evaluating multiple options simultaneously with minimal information per option. social proof signals, when present, do the trust work that a landing page would normally have to earn.

**how to get ratings displayed:**

- connect Google Customer Reviews to your store. this pulls verified post-purchase reviews from buyers who opted into the survey, which are then eligible for display in Shopping.
- submit your third-party review feed from a Merchant Center-approved review platform if you're collecting reviews externally.
- the minimum threshold for ratings display is 50 reviews and 3.5 stars or above. if you're below 50, generating review volume is a business priority, not just a vanity metric.

the review count threshold matters more than the star rating above a certain floor. a 4.4 from 1,200 reviews consistently outperforms a 4.8 from 23 reviews in CTR because volume signals that the product is proven. the buyer is implicitly reasoning: 23 people might be friends of the brand, 1,200 people are probably real.

maximising review collection through post-purchase email flows, SMS requests, and packaging inserts isn't just good for the website. it's directly feeding the Shopping social proof layer that improves CTR account-wide.

## promotions and pricing: the psychological levers most brands don't pull

Shopping results show price as a primary visible element. the price can't be hidden, which means price context becomes everything.

the brands printing with Shopping understand two principles most others don't.

**the anchoring principle:**

when a buyer sees a product at £45 next to a similar product at £38, the £38 product has a price advantage.

when a buyer sees a product at £45 with a "was £65, now £45" promotion running through Merchant Center promotions feed, the calculation changes. the £45 price now has a reference point that makes it feel like a deal, rather than just a number to compare against competitors.

promotion feeds in Merchant Center let you surface promotional pricing, percentage discounts, and special offers directly in the Shopping listing. the listing will show a "special offer" or promotion badge that draws the eye and adds context to the price that a standard listing doesn't have.

this is especially effective during seasonal periods - Black Friday, Boxing Day, end-of-year clearance - but the anchoring effect works throughout the year on any product with a genuine promotional price.

**free shipping as a CTR lever:**

"free shipping" in the Shopping listing is not a nice-to-have.

for most categories, it's a decision-forcing mechanism. two listings at the same price: one shows a shipping cost, one shows "free shipping." the free shipping listing wins the click at a significant rate in virtually every split test i've seen.

if your economics support free shipping - and for most ecom brands at a reasonable average order value, they do when you price it in - make it visible in the feed.

the Merchant Center promotions feed supports "free shipping" badges. alternatively, set the shipping cost to £0 in the Merchant Center settings for eligible products. the "free shipping" text appears in the listing automatically once the shipping data is set correctly.

## local inventory and location signals: the shipping speed shortcut

Google can display a "nearby" or "in stock nearby" signal on Shopping listings for brands using the Local Inventory Ads feature or Merchant Center's location data.

even without the full Local Inventory Ads setup, location signals in the Shopping feed affect buyer perception in a specific way: they imply that shipping will be fast.

a buyer who sees a listing showing inventory near their location assumes the product will arrive sooner than a listing with no location signal. for categories where delivery speed influences purchase decisions, this perception advantage is real and measurable.

if you're a UK-based brand shipping from the UK, make sure your Merchant Center settings reflect that clearly. your Shopping listings should display as "ships from UK" rather than generic shipping information, because "ships from UK" for a UK buyer implies domestic speed and implicitly signals legitimate operation - the opposite of the Shenzhen-shipping-time association discussed earlier in other contexts.

## bid segmentation: the structural layer that separates good from great

once the feed is properly built, the campaign structure needs to reflect the product segmentation established in the custom labels.

the worst Shopping campaign structure: one campaign, one ad group, all products, auto-bidding left to optimise for whatever it decides is most important.

the best structure i've seen run:

**tier 1 - bestsellers and high-margin heroes:**

separate campaign with the highest budget allocation and most aggressive ROAS target (lower than account average, meaning Google bids higher per conversion). these products have conversion data that validates aggressive bidding. they need maximum exposure.

**tier 2 - new product testing:**

separate campaign with a dedicated testing budget and maximize clicks or manual CPC to accumulate impression and click data before introducing smart bidding. products here are in the "prove it" phase. they get exposure, their data gets collected, they either graduate to tier 1 or get excluded.

**tier 3 - clearance or low-margin catch-all:**

if these products need to move, they get separate campaigns with aggressive promotional pricing and lower bid targets. if they don't move, they get excluded from Shopping entirely. low-margin products consuming Shopping budget are a drain on the campaigns that are actually building the business.

the campaign structure forces the algorithm to operate within your profit logic, not its own. without it, PMax and Smart Shopping will always route budget toward the path of least resistance, which is usually the highest-volume products regardless of margin.

## the search terms report: the intelligence source most brands ignore

Shopping campaigns generate a search terms report, exactly like search campaigns.

most brands don't look at it.

the Shopping search terms report tells you every query that triggered a Shopping impression over a given period. it tells you which queries converted and which ones didn't. it tells you exactly where your budget is going at the query level.

the practical applications:

- query mining for new title keywords: queries converting in Shopping that don't appear in your current product titles should be added. the algorithm found them despite the missing keyword because of other relevance signals. adding the keyword makes the match more deliberate and more scalable.
- negative keyword identification: queries with spend but zero conversions, especially at volume, are wasted budget. add them as negatives at the campaign or account level.
- product split opportunities: if a specific variant (colour, size, material) is showing up constantly in converting queries, that variant may warrant its own product listing with a dedicated title rather than being grouped under a parent product.

reviewing the Shopping search terms report monthly and acting on it is one of the highest-ROI maintenance tasks in any ecom Google account. it costs nothing but time and consistently reveals budget leaks and title optimisation opportunities that no external tool would surface.
