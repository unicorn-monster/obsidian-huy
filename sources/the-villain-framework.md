Summary (paraphrased). Central claim: across the 8-figure funnels the author has analyzed, the one recurring shared element isn't quiz structure, upsell flow, or creative — it's a defined enemy/villain. Examples cited: Apple vs IBM, Nike vs laziness, Dave Ramsey vs debt, Bioma vs "Big Diet." The key distinction argued: the villain is essentially never a named competitor — it's a system, industry, or long-standing lie the customer has internalized. Naming that villain shifts the brand from "salesperson" to "ally" — the claim is that a customer who already sees themselves as the hero of their own story isn't looking for another hero to sell to them, they're looking for someone who recognizes the same enemy they do, which builds a kind of trust that ad spend alone can't buy. (Source links to a Google Doc "Villain Framework Guide" for further detail, not captured here.)

**4 recurring villain archetypes the author says show up across funnels:**
- **The Greedy Industry** — an entire industry framed as profiting from the customer's problem persisting (e.g. dental/pharma/diet-industry framings).
- **The Old Way** — blaming an outdated METHOD rather than the customer's effort/willpower, which removes personal shame ("the old approach doesn't work anymore," not "you failed").
- **The Hidden Cause** — reframing the root cause entirely to something the customer didn't know to blame (e.g. "it's not your metabolism, it's your gut bacteria"; "it's not your ads, it's your landing page").
- **The Rigged System** — positioning a system/algorithm/structural rule as deliberately working against the customer, with the product as the workaround.

**Examples referenced as real funnels:** Steel Bite Pro (supplement, cited as $100M+) built around framing the dental industry broadly as incentivized to keep patients returning rather than curing problems; Bioma's quiz walks through every diet the user has tried (keto, paleo, fasting) before delivering a verdict reframing gut microbiome as the real, previously-unblamed cause; Dan Kennedy (marketing, cited as $100M+) is described as having built his positioning by casting mainstream advertising agencies ("Madison Avenue") as the enemy of small business owners who'd been burned by them.

**3-step method given for finding a brand's villain:**
1. List everything the customer has already tried before finding you (diets, software, gurus, professionals hired) — the villain tends to live inside these failed attempts.
2. Ask who profits from the customer's problem continuing — follow the money; the villain is whoever benefits from the problem NOT being solved.
3. Name the lie — identify a piece of conventional wisdom the customer has been told that isn't actually true; calling out "common knowledge" positions the brand as a truth-teller.

**Stated rules:** never attack a named individual — attack systems/industries, not people (a systemic attack reads as principled, a personal attack reads as petty); the villain must already resonate as an existing frustration the customer feels — you're not manufacturing a new enemy, you're naming one that's already there (if it doesn't land immediately, it's the wrong villain); and the villain should explain why past attempts failed for reasons outside the customer's control, not because they were personally inadequate.
