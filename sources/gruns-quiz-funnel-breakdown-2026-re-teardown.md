I went back through the Grüns Cubs quiz this week. Six questions. A scored diagnosis. A projection chart. An offer. And not one field asking who you are. No email. No name. No phone. Nothing. A funnel that did $300M+ ARR and got bought by Unilever for ~$1.2B doesn't forget the lead capture.

## Overview
1M+ visitors. $300M+ ARR. Launched 2023. Acquired by Unilever for ~$1.2B in under three years. 4.1 Trustpilot. The kids' line (Grüns Cubs, ages 2–13) runs on its own quiz subdomain — **quiz.gruns.co** — separated from the main store. Its own domain, funnel, conversion math. (Broke this down in May; this is the re-teardown, because the thing everyone skipped is the most interesting decision in it.)

## The Zero-Data Bet
Every quiz funnel has an email wall (Moérie, Noom, every hair/skin/gut diagnostic). Grüns does not — not before results, chart, or offer. No lead-capture step anywhere.

The logic, ruthless: the email wall exists to retarget non-buyers, but it's also the single highest drop-off step — so you're taxing every buyer to salvage some non-buyers. Grüns sells a monthly consumable to a parent who just read a red-flagged report about their kid's gut = peak motivation *right now*. An email sequence adds nothing the offer page isn't already doing better, in the moment, with a 52% discount attached. So they deleted the wall. Every finisher flows straight to checkout — no "check your inbox" dead-end, no privacy hesitation.

Trade: first-purchase conversion at peak intent, over a retargetable list. Low-consideration, impulse-priced product → speed to checkout wins. Make the bet consciously.

## Landing Is Question One
![[gruns-quiz-funnel-breakdown-2026-re-teardown-01.webp]]

"What is your kid's poop telling you?" No splash screen, no "Start" button. Question one — *How old is your child?* — sits right under the headline. You're not deciding whether to take a quiz; you're already in it. Note the word **assessment**, not quiz. And a "Skip Question" escape on step one — they'd rather have you moving than accurate.

## The Bristol Scale, Cartoonified
![[gruns-quiz-funnel-breakdown-2026-re-teardown-02.webp]]

Best question in the funnel (I under-rated it last time). The Bristol Stool Scale is a real clinical instrument — and unbearably awkward in a consumer funnel. So Grüns drew it as cartoons with three labels: "Hard pebbles / lumpy sausage? Backed up." / "Smooth sausage or little cracks? **This is the goal.**" / "Fluffy or watery? Too fast."

One option is labelled "the goal" — which makes every other answer (five of six) a diagnosable deviation, in the parent's own words, by their own hand. An embarrassing subjective observation becomes an objective self-diagnosis; ick drops, perceived rigour rises. Borrowed medical authority, wearing a cartoon.

## They Plant the Verdict Before They Deliver It
![[gruns-quiz-funnel-breakdown-2026-re-teardown-03.webp]]

Under the picky-eater answers, a green "Why we ask?" card: "Picky eaters eat from a very short list of foods — usually big gaps in fiber, vitamins, key minerals." Next question (diet) gets a "Did you know?" card: "Most kids on a standard American diet get less than half the fiber they need. That gap shows up fast."

Every card points at one culprit — **fiber** — conveniently the thing the gummy delivers. By the results page the parent has been told three times (while thinking about their own kid) that fiber is the missing variable. The results screen doesn't argue; it confirms. This is **conclusion-seeding**: install the diagnostic frame mid-quiz, and the recommendation feels like the parent's own realisation. Bonus: six questions feel like a real assessment when each one teaches you something.

## The Loader Buys the Verdict Its Credibility
![[gruns-quiz-funnel-breakdown-2026-re-teardown-04.webp]]

"Building your kid's gut health profile…" — three animated lines (analyzing diet & fiber, reading digestion & energy signals, checking against what kids their age need). Nothing is computed; the verdict was written before you arrived. But six taps don't feel like they deserve a diagnosis — a loading bar does the emotional accounting, converting "I tapped some buttons" into "something analysed my child."

## Negativity By Design
![[gruns-quiz-funnel-breakdown-2026-re-teardown-05.webp]]

The scorecard: Gut Comfort *Below optimal*. Daily Energy *Room for improvement*. Digestive Rhythm *Below optimal*. Support Opportunity *High*. Four rows, three bad; the only "High" is the one that means buy something. No answer path produces a clean bill of health — the scale is engineered so the exit is always the same door.

Then the guilt dodge: the verdict reads "a fiber gap that's built up **quietly over time**." It snuck up; nobody could have seen it. The villain is the gap, never the mother. Blame the parent → they close the tab. Blame an invisible, gradual deficiency → they reach for their card.

## Here → Goal
![[gruns-quiz-funnel-breakdown-2026-re-teardown-06.webp]]

A "Gut Stability" line over 12 weeks. "They're here" pinned at the bottom; "Goal: steady digestion and nutrition" up and to the right. The chart proves nothing — it converts a diagnosis into a plan already in motion, with a timeline and one obvious vehicle. Diagnosis without a path out is just anxiety; add the path and you have a purchase.

## The Discount You "Qualified" For
![[gruns-quiz-funnel-breakdown-2026-re-teardown-07.webp]]

"Based on your profile, you qualify for up to 52% off your first order." Everyone gets it — but **qualify** does enormous work: a blanket promo becomes a personalised reward you earned by completing the diagnosis, which retroactively proves the whole thing was tailored to you. Then the risk stack at the decision moment: 30-Day Happiness Guarantee, 100M+ gummies sold, Rated Excellent by 100,000+ parents. One button — "Claim Your Discount" — straight into checkout. They never learned your name.

## What to steal
1. Cheap/impulse product → delete the email gate; you're taxing buyers to salvage non-buyers.
2. Put question one on the landing page. No splash, no Start button.
3. Turn a clinical scale into a tap-friendly image grid — borrowed authority, zero awkwardness.
4. Label one answer "this is the goal." Every other answer becomes a diagnosable problem.
5. Seed the mechanism mid-quiz with "Why we ask?" cards, so the verdict feels self-generated.
6. Blame the invisible gap, never the customer. Guilt closes tabs.
7. Say they **qualify** for the discount. Same promo, completely different meaning.
