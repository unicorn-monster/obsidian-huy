## I - The audience pool for problem-aware buyers in any niche is becoming exhausted.

What is the problem-aware pool?

Think of it as the buyers who already know they have the problem, already know there's a category of products that solves it, and are actively shopping for the best deal.

Most brand owners run ads to this pool because it's "easier to convert." But if you look closely, they're just becoming complacent. Their CPAs aren't getting better. Their CPMs keep climbing. They cap out around [$200-300k](https://x.com/search?q=%24200-300k&src=cashtag_click)/month and can't figure out why.

Whenever you run another testimonial UGC ad, whenever someone else launches a "30% off" sale, whenever another brand drops a "buy 2 get 1 free" creative, the problem-aware pool shrinks for everyone.

This obviously gets complex and requires a level of marketing systems thinking to fully see, but if the creative you publish to a saturated audience hurts more than it helps, and it isn't counterbalanced by creative that opens up the unaware market, your ad account becomes a dying engine.

Why is that bad?

Because the audience you can reach influences the spend you can absorb. The spend you can absorb influences your revenue ceiling. The creative you run trains the algorithm on which buyers to find, which controls your scaling potential, which controls the long-term enterprise value of the brand.

That's why you advertise, right?

To equip your brand with the demand and customer base to achieve the business you want?

But that's just it…

Before you can hit $1M months, build a real brand, exit for 9 figures, or compete with the big players in your space, you need an audience capable of being introduced to your product from scratch. 99% of brand owners don't even know how to talk to those buyers, because they're happy hitting "duplicate ad set" on the same 5 testimonial creatives.

The takeaway here that we will touch on later is this...

Does the creative you publish lead to a beneficial scale change in your account? Or are you unconsciously running ads that silently shrink your audience and trap your brand in a problem-aware ceiling?

That's layer one.

## II - The three forces capping your ad account

As the audience matures, you have to bring something genuinely new to the table or your ad becomes invisible to it.

I want to introduce you to one of the most important thinkers in direct response history.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HK2lzKBWUAAg7PE?format=png&name=large)

His name is Eugene Schwartz.

He doesn't post on Twitter (he died in 1995), but his book Breakthrough Advertising is still the most cited copywriting text on the planet. When you read it, you can instantly tell that he is a calm, nuanced, deeply principled thinker about how markets evolve.

He dedicated his life to understanding how buyers move through stages of awareness, and while there's much more to his work than what creative you run on Facebook, that's what we're going to focus on.

In a nutshell, Schwartz believed that there are 3 forces that determine whether an ad scales, two of those forces being almost always ignored.

The 3 forces are:

**Market saturation** - Every category gets sold to. Buyers see the same claims, the same testimonials, the same offers. Eventually they tune all of it out. Think every supplement ad you've scrolled past in the last month.

**Sophistication stage** \- As a market matures, the same claim stops working. Stage 1 was "lose weight." Stage 5 is "lose weight for menopausal women over 50 who refuse to take Ozempic." If you're running stage 1 ads in a stage 5 market, you're invisible.

**Audience awareness** - Not every buyer knows they have the problem yet. Some buyers are problem-aware. Most are unaware. The biggest pool of money in any category is the unaware pool, but it's the hardest to crack.

When those 3 things converge, they result in one of three outcomes: a brand stuck at [$50k-](https://x.com/search?q=%2450k-&src=cashtag_click)$200k/month forever, a brand that briefly scales then collapses when CPMs catch up, or a brand that breaks through into the unaware pool and scales to [$1M+](https://x.com/search?q=%241M%2B&src=cashtag_click) months.

He didn't call them "attractors," but the idea is the same. Brands tend to fall into the basin of their creative strategy.

The "third path," or the good outcome here, is a brand that can consistently introduce new buyers to the product who didn't even know they had the problem yet.

When we look at most ecom ad accounts today, it's pretty easy to see how this is playing out.

When brand owners compete for the same problem-aware buyers, they optimize for engagement rather than transformation (saturation). The algorithm only measures how much you've clicked, watched, or purchased, so brand owners are much more likely to abandon educational creative for whatever will get a 2x ROAS this week. That's an obvious problem.

When engagement is optimized, the creative that buyers consume does not require any new awareness, so that buyer pool atrophies. The "substrate" being consumed is the unaware market, which is downstream of education-led ads.

AI accelerates ad production, yes, but it also accelerates imitation, and when there's only problem-aware creative to imitate, you can see where that goes. AI itself isn't the issue. The issue is that it can mimic what looks like a winning ad without requiring any market sophistication understanding from the brand owner running it.

So, the default outcome here is your ad account becomes saturated incredibly fast because it's optimized for creative that looks like it should scale but structurally cannot.

That leads to layer three - what you can do about it, and how you can profit from it in a meaningful way.

## III - Why unaware ads may be the last bastion of real scale

The biggest market in every category is the buyer who doesn't know your product exists yet.

For the past few years, a certain type of ad has dominated Facebook.

Specifically, creative that delivers the offer without earning the attention.

It's fast food for the ad account.

So we're going to call this category of creative "fast ads" - because brand owners use the same psychological triggers to get cheap clicks as discount retailers who realized urgency, scarcity, and discount stacking spike conversion in the short term.

On the fast ad end of the spectrum, you have testimonial UGC, "30% off" statics, "buy 2 get 1 free" carousels, before-and-after ads, and 15-second pain-point spots that close the buyer in a hurry. Makes sense why every account looks the same.

The early adopters of Facebook ads could post a basic UGC video and scale to $50k days, but the landscape has changed.

On the slow ad end of the spectrum, or creative that requires the buyer to think, you have unaware ads, advertorial-style scripts, long-form VSLs, and ads crafted in a way that makes the viewer lean in before they ever realize they're being sold to.

I personally want to focus on unaware ads.

Why?

Because they are something you can use, in any niche, to unlock the meaning economy of direct response we are heading into. And, unaware ads are the most scalable and durable creative format. A testimonial ad might run for 30 days. An unaware ad can run for 437 days and counting.

On top of that, most brand owners walk around with a creative library they've never thought through. They feel like they've "tested everything," but they've never tried to write an ad that opens up a buyer who didn't know they needed the product.

It also doesn't hurt that some of the most legendary scaled brands were forged through unaware creative: Hims, Manscaped, Liquid Death, Athletic Greens, Lume.

The defining factor of an unaware ad is that AI cannot write one without serious operator input.

For understanding, it helps to distinguish a problem-aware ad from an unaware ad:

Problem-aware ads sell the offer. Unaware ads earn the attention.

Problem-aware ads package the discount. Unaware ads question the buyer's reality. Problem-aware ads start with the product. Unaware ads start with a story, a stat, a question, or a symptom. Problem-aware ads inform a buyer who already wants the category. Unaware ads create a buyer who didn't know the category mattered.

Problem-aware ads communicate. Unaware ads convert someone who wasn't looking.

If you think about it, only a brand operator who understands their market deeply can write an unaware ad, because the format requires a situated point of view about who the buyer is, what they're secretly worried about, and what they don't yet know they want.

AI can simulate the structure, but it lacks the desire, objection, and emotional context that lead to writing in a direction that genuinely opens a buyer.

More importantly, AI flattens the surprise that makes unaware ads work. This is a crucial point. You can tell AI to write something novel, but the buyer can sense the formula. It's no longer a surprise. You destroyed any chance of stumbling onto the angle that actually breaks through.

The more I use AI in the agency, the more useful I find it, but on the other edge of the sword, I find it exhausts originality extremely fast. I can't help but believe (even as someone who is pro-AI in the creative process) that most ads that scale past $500k/month in 2026 will be unaware in structure. Slow ads.

IV - The meaning economy of direct response and how to thrive in it

The unaware market has been emerging as the only scalable pool for years now.

AI has only accelerated this.

Why?

Because the problem-aware pool is the scarcest audience in ecom right now.

Before social media, brands praised retail. During the early Facebook ads era, brands made cheap clicks their god. Today, more is the god. More creatives, more ad sets, more agencies, more spend. Most brand owners have more ads running than ever and less audience to reach than ever.

In my eyes, the unaware market will be sold at a premium.

Brands that can reach it will dominate.

And who better to reach it than the operators who understand how to actually open a buyer? The brand owners who fight against the carbon-copy problem.

But how is an unaware buyer opened?

A buyer is opened the moment their attention is ordered toward something that disrupts their default state.

When the buyer is scrolling, scattered, and pulled in competing directions, that's mental entropy. It feels like skipping, ignoring, blocking. Chaos.

When the buyer's attention is captured by a story, a stat, a question, or a symptom they recognize, that's mental order. It feels like leaning in. Curiosity.

So, an unaware buyer isn't found out there in the audience pool, they're created the moment your creative orders their attention around something they didn't know they were already thinking about.

The unaware market is created through the process of ordering buyer attention.

The act of ordering buyer attention (taking the noise of the feed and creating a story that coheres) results in scale.

If you've ever scrolled past 50 ads and stopped on one that wasn't even trying to sell you anything, you've directly experienced this.

What does this have to do with unaware ads?

Problem-aware ads (saturated, pre-packaged, algorithm-optimized) skip the ordering process and deliver pre-packaged offers. The buyer's attention stays scattered. They received an offer but didn't generate any new desire. That's why they bounce.

Unaware ads (story, stat, symptom, question, education-led) require both the brand and the buyer to engage in the ordering process. The brand orders the market's attention through the act of writing the script. The buyer re-orders their reality by realizing they have a problem they didn't know they had.

What's the opportunity here?

The market doesn't need more brands competing for the same "30% off" buyer. It also doesn't need more brand owners trying to clone the latest TikTok ad format to build the next quickly-dead ecom store.

Instead, it needs operators who deeply understand their buyer, study the unaware end of the awareness spectrum, and ship creative that opens markets nobody else is talking to.

By all measures, I believe this is the most future-proof and deeply scalable way to build an ecom brand. Sure, you aren't running the same easy ads everyone else is, but you are causing a greater cascade. That is, you are providing the root. The creative that influences a buyer that influences their first purchase that influences the LTV that influences whether your brand exits at 8 figures or quietly dies at $200k/month. Even running tons of UGC doesn't have that much power.

## V - How to write your first winning unaware ad (and why it's the most valuable creative skill you can develop)

Here's what I could put together to get you started on the right track, then we'll talk about who can actually run these for you.

Write to open the buyer, not to sell them. Most ad accounts close on the back end anyway, and you can layer that later. Start with a story, a stat, a question, or a symptom...something the buyer recognizes but hasn't named. An unaware ad begins with curiosity, not an offer.

Write about what your buyer genuinely cares about. Focus on a single emotional truth. Use this as a chance to research and learn what your buyer is actually feeling. Read 1-star and 3-star reviews of your competitors. Read upvoted Reddit threads. Read YouTube comments under videos in your niche. Don't accept one survey response as gospel.

Resist the template. You'll find how you like to structure your unaware ads as you get better at it. It's a skill, remember that. For now, just write. Have a debate with the buyer's resistance. Pre-handle their objections inside the script. Then worry about format and ask AI for help if you want. Do the marketing thinking first.

Ask "Do I actually believe this would stop a buyer who'd never heard of my product?" It's easy to write what closes a problem-aware buyer, but the point of this is to convert someone who didn't know they had the problem. This is the most difficult part. Resist the urge to slip into your usual hook.

Study unaware ads, consume long-running unaware creative. Your sense-making capacity as a brand operator is shaped by your inputs. You can't expect your competitors' carbon-copy testimonials to teach you this. You must actively search the Facebook Ad Library, the TrendTrack top-performers, and the longest-running ads in adjacent niches.

Build a body of unaware concepts, not a content calendar of testimonials. Brands don't scale to [$1M+](https://x.com/search?q=%241M%2B&src=cashtag_click)/month off one ad. They scale off a body of coherent creative work, layered into a full-funnel ad set. Each ad builds on the last. AI can't replicate a coherent unaware library built through deep buyer research over months.

Those are the only three sources that will reliably show you unaware ads that have actually scaled.
