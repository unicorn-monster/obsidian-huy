# i - keyword type

before you start doing keyword research, you need to understand the different types of keywords.

this shapes everything that comes after. your match types, ad copy, landing page, bids, etc.

there are 4 main keyword types you can profit from:

## 1\. branded terms

these are people searching your brand or product name directly

example: "nike", "nike shoes", "air force 1"

this is the easiest money you'll make on google as people already know you and are looking for you.

the goal is to capture as much of this demand as possible at the lowest cpc you can.

you do NOT want competitors stealing this traffic.

## 2\. transactional intent

these are buyers ready to purchase right now

example: "buy iphone 15 pro", "standing desk", "women's running shoes"

the intent here is strong and there’s usually plenty of search volume behind it.

but since these are non-branded searches, every brand in your space is competing for them.

which is why these keywords tend to get expensive.

along with branded campaigns, this should be your priority at the start.

## 3\. comparison intent

these are people who already know what type of product they want and are now deciding where to spend their money.

example: “casper vs nectar mattress,” “huel protein powder alternative,” “nike shorts reviews"

we break them down into 3 categories:

**\> best-of searches** ("best \[product category\]", "top \[product\] 2025", "highest rated \[product\]")

**\> competitor searches** ("\[competitor brand\]", "\[competitor brand\] reviews", "\[competitor brand\] alternative")

**\> head-to-head searches** ("\[competitor\] vs \[another competitor\]", "\[your brand\] vs \[competitor\]")

this is some of the best traffic on google if you have the right funnel and landing page experience for it

we've helped many brands in crowded spaces generate six figures per month at 3x roas here

## 4\. informational intent

these are people searching for a problem your product solves.

example: "how to lose weight after 50", "benefits of carnivore diet”.

the search volume here is huge and CPCs are much cheaper because fewer brands are competing for it.

but since the intent in those searches is lower, it usually takes more work to convert.

not something i’d prioritize early on.

but once you're ready to scale past 6 figures in spend, this is where the real volume lives.

**important note:**

for keyword types 1 (branded) and 2 (transactional), you can launch them right away with a standard product or collection page. but for keyword types 3 (comparison) and 4 (informational), you’ll need a dedicated lp to convert that traffic. i’ll go deeper into that in the landing page section.

# Ii - keyword research

i wrote a full article breaking this down step by step, so i'll keep it brief here.

if you want to see our complete process, check it out here. \[Placeholder for previous article\]

here's the high-level version of how this works:

go to tools → keyword planner → discover new keywords → enter your prompt term.

usually this is the main phrase tied to your product or category. (i.e., "magnesium supplement," "collagen peptides")

filter by average monthly searches above 30.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HJPjDjCbYAAjvEb?format=jpg&name=large)

use the refine keywords feature to go deeper.

google will show you sub-categories and themes inside your results

if the same theme keeps showing up across multiple searches, that’s a signal to build a dedicated ad group around it.

before adding keywords into campaigns, check these 3 things:

\> **competition level** (how crowded the auction is): use it to inform your bid strategy and cost expectations.

\> **top of page bid** estimates (estimated CPC ranges for positions 1 through 4): use these to plan your budget.

\> f**orecast tool** (estimated impressions, clicks, and projected spend): use this to see potential search volume and how much room there is to scale.

use exact match for specific long-tail terms with around 1,000 to 2,000+ monthly searches

example: "creatine for women," "magnesium glycinate 400mg"

use phrase match when the main keyword matters, but you want to pick up extra variations around it.

use broad match to find related searches, conversational queries, and adjacent terms… especially once you have enough conversion data

NEVER apply it to branded terms.

and don’t use it on short keywords either. keep it to terms with at least 3 to 5 words.

you’ll also want these in place first:

\- enough conversion data for smart bidding to optimize properly - a strong negative keyword list to filter out irrelevant traffic & branded terms

# iii - campaign structure

first, you need to keep your branded and non-branded traffic in separate campaigns

non-negotiable.

create a negative keyword list with all brand variations:

\- brand name - misspellings - brand + product - domain name - product line names if they're well-known

add all your brand name variations as negatives inside your prospecting campaigns.

and do not let brand terms bleed into prospecting.

run it on a regular basis to check the search terms that have been served and add non-branded ones as negatives

we often run separate campaigns for each major product category (if they get at least 2-3 conversions per day)

when building ad groups, focus on three things:

**1\. search volume**: group enough related terms together to generate meaningful data

below that threshold, you won't accumulate enough data to make reliable optimization decisions.

if you have low-volume keywords that matter, group related ones together to hit that number.

**2\. keyword relationship**: every keyword inside an ad group needs to share the same category and intent.

this way, you can show highly relevant ads and landing pages for those searches.

for example:

"best creatine powder" and "how does creatine work" don't belong together.

these keywords should have different headlines, different messaging, and different landing pages.

# iv - ad copy & extensions

## 1\. ad copy

you can (and should) use all the maximum headlines and descriptions allowed.

every ad needs these five elements:

\- your USPs - main keyword - current offers or discounts - social proof - clear CTA

for exact match ad groups, you can write your own headlines

or you can use dynamic keyword insertion to automatically pull the user’s query term into the headline.

keep your copy tightly aligned to the keyword.

if someone searches "creatine for women" and your headline says "premium supplements for athletes", you lose them before they even click.

the job of the ad is to confirm you have exactly what they searched for.

for comparison and informational keywords, your angle needs to shift.

transactional copy doesn't land here.

lead with education, credibility, or a specific claim that speaks to where they are in the research process.

\---

## 2\. ad extensions/assets

extensions help increase your search page real estate and push competitors down

minimum you should have:

\- business name and logo - 4+ sitelinks (8-12 recommended) - 10-12 callouts - structured snippets

also worth adding:

\- image extensions, especially important on mobile where they dramatically increase visual footprint - price extensions to pre-qualify traffic before they click and reduce wasted spend - promotion extensions when you have an active offer - headline and description extensions for temporary campaigns

just to give you a good idea of what a great search ad looks like

\- headline matching the query - price shown upfront - in-stock status - 4.6-star rating - 2,700 reviews - free delivery - 30-day returns - image extensions

every additional element is a reason for the right buyer to click and a reason for the wrong buyer not to.

# v - bidding and budgets

## 1\. branded campaigns

automated bidding strategies like target roas are designed to find the users most likely to convert and bid up hard to reach them.

that works well when you're trying to find new customers.

but on brand campaigns, that’s how you can end up paying 5-10x higher than you should for clicks.

manual CPC is what we use 90% of the time for branded.

\- exact match: set max CPC 5-10% above the keyword average - phrase match: set max CPC 5-10% above your exact match bids

## 2\. prospecting campaigns

your bidding strategy depends on how much conversion data your campaign has.

if you have <15 conversions in the last 30 days, use maximize clicks and set a max CPC cap

you can switch to target roas once you reach 15+ conversions in the last 30 days

or you can switch to target cpa once you reach 30+ conversions in the last 30 days

set it 20% below your profitable ROAS target to give google's algorithm room to learn

once it stabilizes, increase the target gradually as the data builds.

# vi - landing pages

your landing page is one of the most important factors behind your search ads performance

it affects not just conversion rate, but directly impacts your quality score, which determines your ad placement and your CPC.

and a lot of ecom brands are leaving serious money on the table because of their landing page strategy.

as i mentioned above, there are 4 categories of keywords you can profit from

but the majority of brands only tap into branded and bof transactional intent keywords

when they try to scale into the other keyword types...

they struggle to turn a profit and can’t push spend very far.

the reason usually comes down to one thing.

**they’re driving that traffic to product pages or collection pages**

think about it for a sec

if someone searches for a query with comparison intent like “top magnesium supplements for women” or informational intent like “how to reduce anxiety”...

they’re not ready to land on a page of full of specs, features that just ask them to buy.

what you need instead is what we call google ads-specific landing pages

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HJPjY8IbMAAbG-G?format=jpg&name=large)

these are pages built specifically around the keyword, traffic source, and intent behind the search.

they’ve helped us:

\- lower cpcs - reduce acquisition costs - capture clicks at as low as $0.07 cpc - open up new traffic sources that standard PDPs and collection pages couldn’t convert

many of our clients have added six and seven figures in revenue directly because of them.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HJPjhZ5bIAAdb1S?format=png&name=large)

to show you how much this matters:

we worked with a home goods brand paying $6 average CPC on their core queries.

after building a dedicated landing page optimized around those exact terms, CPC dropped to $1.73.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HJPjfDRa4AA-8XZ?format=jpg&name=large)

now before we get into the specific formats, you need to understand this

a general rule across all landing pages is that the page must match the intent of the keyword & ads

if someone searches for a specific problem, product, or comparison…

the page should immediately confirm they’re in the right place.

that means:

\- the headline reflects the exact search intent - the actual keyword and related phrases appear throughout the copy - the visuals reinforce what the user searched for - the copy matches the awareness level of the visitor - the page structure is built around the specific angle or problem being searched

the tighter the match between keyword → ad → landing page…

the higher your quality score, the lower your CPCs, and the better your conversion rate.

that’s what make google ads-specific landing pages so powerful

they’re about as close of a match as you can get between what someone searched for and what they land on.

## 1\. branded terms

these can be sent to the homepage, collection page, or product page depending on the search term.

the landing page should reflect the intent behind the branded query.

for example:

broad branded searches → homepage

branded category searches → collection page

branded product searches → product page

## 2\. transactional intent

send these to PDPs or collection pages around the exact query.

in some cases, you can run this traffic to a 3rd-party comparison page.

this tends to work especially well in crowded markets like supplements or beauty, where shoppers are more skeptical.

from our data, it can pull significantly lower CPCs than a standard PDP.

## 3\. informational intent

advertorials, listicles and blogs with a soft CTA are the best format for this traffic.

they match the research mindset of the visitor and ease them into the funnel.

one thing a lot of brands miss here is that the SEO side matters a lot for this traffic.

from what we’ve seen firsthand...

without strong seo, you’ll see way fewer impressions, lower click volume, and higher CPCs.

you need proper blog content, internal linking, multiple supporting pages, and proper technical seo behind it.

## 4\. comparison intent

you can start by sending this traffic to a PDP to validate search volume and conversion potential.

but long term, this traffic performs best with dedicated comparison-style landing pages.

best-of searches → 3rd-party comparison pages

competitor searches → 3rd-party comparison page or "us vs. them" page

head-to-head searches → "us vs. them" page

# vii - ai max

once you have a profitable baseline, test AI Max.

we've been running it across client accounts since launch and the results have been solid.

for those who don't know, ai max is a feature that helps you:

\- go after relevant searches that might not be on your keyword list - customize ads & select a suitable landing page in real time based on the query

it pulls from your website content & existing ads

AND combines that with signals like user behavior, search patterns, browsing history

then it interprets the real meaning behind each search query:

\- the implied need or problem - the stage of the buying journey - the context of the search

based on that, it decides if the query fits your products and tailors the ad to match

say you sell pet carriers.

someone searches "do I need to bring something to carry my dog on a plane?"

they never mentioned "pet carrier."

but according to google, ai max can read between the lines and figure out the intent behind the question

it knows this person needs a carrier and adjusts your ad to match that context.

and they end up seeing a headline like "TSA-Approved Dog Carriers for Air Travel - Fits Under Seat"

on top of that…

ai max gives you more control over what goes out in your ads than you had with DSA

for example, you can block specific terms from showing up in your ad headlines

they also recently introduced a new feature called AI Brief.

it's a way to give AI Max direction around your messaging and targeting so it doesn't go off-script.

\- **messaging guidelines**: tell it what your ads should and shouldn't say (“never mention prices")

\- **matching guidelines:** set boundaries for the searches you want to capture or avoid ("prioritize searches for healthy pantry staples")

\- **audience guidelines:** define who you want to reach and how to speak to them. ("for health-conscious buyers, lead with clean ingredients.")

this is where search is heading and google’s clearly leaning into it

it’s definitely worth testing once the fundamentals are locked in.

# viii - optimization workflow

what drives sustained growth is the process you run after launch.

## 1\. weekly tasks

\> pull the search terms report and add new negatives. this is ongoing work that never stops, especially in the early weeks

\> check if brand terms are leaking into prospecting and if non-brand terms are leaking into branded

\> monitor CTR and CPC by ad group.

## 2\. ongoing

\> A/B test ad copy continuously. rotate headlines, test different social proof angles, try new CTAs

\> monitor quality score by ad group. low quality score is usually an alignment problem between keyword, ad, and landing page

\> flag underperforming ad groups with low CTR, high CPC, or low CVR, then diagnose: is it the keyword, the ad copy, or the landing page?

\> test different landing page formats to better match the intent of the traffic

remember, the accounts that grow sustainably are the ones running a tight optimization loop every single week.
