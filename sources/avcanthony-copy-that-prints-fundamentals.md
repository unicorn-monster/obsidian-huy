Everyone's looking for the secret.

Better prompts. New formats. The psychological trigger nobody's talking about. The framework that's finally going to make their copy convert.

It's never that.

It's almost always that they skipped the basics. The boring fundamentals that actually decide whether copy works. Then they wonder why the "advanced" stuff isn't saving them.

You don't have a prompt problem. You have a fundamentals problem.

Here are the five things you run before you write a single word.

\*\*1. Define the problem. The SPECIFIC problem.\*\*

Not the category. The exact named thing.

Not "back pain." Sciatica.

Not "knee pain." Neuropathy.

Not "trouble sleeping." Insomnia. And go further, sleep-onset vs staying-asleep. Different problems, different copy.

Why it matters: the category is crowded and vague, so when you target it, the prospect isn't sure you mean him. The precise named condition makes him stop and go "that's exactly what I have." Specificity is recognition, and recognition is trust. The second he knows you're talking about HIS exact problem, he believes you understand it well enough to solve it.

Name the precise thing. The right person leans in.

\*\*2. Get clear on your avatar. And who it's NOT.\*\*

Defining your avatar is just as much about who you're NOT talking to as who you are.

The second you try to talk to everyone, you talk to no one. "People with back pain" is so watered down that nobody feels seen. Get specific enough that the wrong people feel left out. That's how you know you went deep enough.

But here's where most people stop too early. They build a stack of characteristics and think that's an avatar:

Woman. 58. Lives in the suburbs. Has sciatica. Fixed income. Tried physical therapy.

That's not a person. That's a census form.

This is a person:

Linda gets up from her desk and has to brace on the chair for a second before her leg will cooperate. She's stopped taking the stairs. She cancelled the trip to see the grandkids because she couldn't picture sitting on a plane for four hours. She's tried the stretches off YouTube and a heating pad that's now permanently on her couch, and what scares her isn't the pain today, it's the thought that this is just how it's going to be from now on.

See the difference?

One is a list. The other is someone you can picture. You know her day. You know what the problem stops her from doing. You know what she's afraid of.

When you know your avatar THAT well, you stop guessing what to write. She tells you. Every line comes from her actual life instead of your imagination.

Don't build a stack of traits. Write one real person you can see in the research. The person behind all those Reddit posts and Facebook groups.

\*\*3. What do they actually want? (Life Force 8)\*\*

Underneath every problem is a deep desire. Cashvertising calls them the Life Force 8. The wants nobody has to teach you to have.

Survival and life extension. Enjoyment of food. Freedom from fear and danger. Comfort. Companionship. To be superior to others. To care for and protect loved ones. Social approval.

Why it matters: nobody buys a product. They buy a want satisfied. Your job is to connect what you sell to one of these primal forces.

"Fix your sciatica" is a feature. "Get back on the plane and go see your grandkids" is a Life Force. Aim at the force, not the feature.

\*\*4. What have they already heard? (Sophistication)\*\*

This is the one everyone skips, and it quietly kills more copy than anything else.

The simplest way to think about it: sophistication dictates what your audience already believes about why you're different. And that belief is the whole reason they buy. They have to believe you'll get them a result the last thing didn't. The stage tells you what it'll take to make them believe that.

Five stages.

Stage 1: You're first. Nobody's heard the claim. So you just say it. The promise alone is enough, because it's new. There's nothing to overcome yet.

Stage 2: Competitors show up making the same claim. The bare claim isn't enough anymore. You win by making it bigger, bolder, faster than the other guy.

Stage 3: They've heard every version of the claim and stopped believing it. The claim is dead. Now you make them believe again with a mechanism, a new WAY it works. This is where a Unique Mechanism earns its place. The mechanism is the reason the old dead promise is suddenly believable again.

Stage 4: Everyone's got a mechanism now. So you out-mechanism them. Yours is better, more complete, solves what theirs can't. You're not introducing a mechanism, you're proving yours beats the field.

Stage 5: They've heard it all and believe nothing. Claims dead, mechanisms dead. All that's left is identification. You stop selling and reflect them back to themselves so well they trust you out of recognition alone.

Here's the part most people get backwards. They force a UMP into everything because someone told them UMPs convert. But a UMP is a Stage 3 and 4 tool. It exists to rebuild belief that's been worn down by repetition.

If you've got a genuinely new product with a mechanism nobody's ever seen, you might be at Stage 1 or 2. You don't need to "redefine the problem" or construct some elaborate unique mechanism to separate yourself. The newness IS the separation. Forcing a UMP there is solving a problem you don't have, and it makes fresh copy sound like tired copy.

So don't reach for the UMP by default. Read the stage first. The stage tells you whether belief is something you can win with a plain claim, or something you have to rebuild with a mechanism.

Why it matters: write a Stage 2 claim to a Stage 4 market and you sound like every dead promise they tuned out years ago. Construct a heavy UMP for a Stage 1 market and you're overcomplicating something that would've sold on novelty alone. Match the move to the stage, because the stage decides what they're willing to believe about you.

\*\*5. How much do they know? (Awareness)\*\*

This is NOT the same as sophistication, and confusing the two is where a lot of people fall apart.

Sophistication is what the MARKET has heard. Awareness is what YOUR PROSPECT knows about their own problem and your solution.

Five levels.

Most aware: they know your product and just need the deal.

Product aware: they know your product exists but aren't sold yet.

Solution aware: they know solutions like yours exist, but not yours specifically.

Problem aware: they know they have the problem, but don't know solutions exist.

Unaware: they don't even know they have the problem yet.

Why it matters: this decides where the copy STARTS. The unaware prospect needs you to open on the problem. The most aware just needs the offer and a reason to act now. Start in the wrong place and you either bore the ready buyer or lose the cold one.

That's the whole thing. Five steps. If your copy isn't converting. Stop looking for fancier prompts. And start looking at the fundamentals.
