i started running ecom brands at 17. for the first two years, everything ran on meta ads. i launched brand after brand, poured money into facebook campaigns, and watched the pattern repeat itself every time.

a winning creative would emerge. roas would spike. i'd scale the budget. three weeks later, creative fatigue. roas collapsed. i'd chase new hooks, new ugc, new audiences. brief recovery. then the cycle started over.

it wasn't that meta didn't work. it's that meta never let me sleep. the channel demanded constant creative production, constant audience testing, constant manual optimization. every single day something needed attention or the whole thing unraveled.

two years in, i had tried a skincare brand, a home goods brand, and a general wellness brand. none of them lasted. each one died the same way: meta performance became unpredictable, margins eroded as cpms climbed, and the creative treadmill burned me out before the brand could build any real foundation.

then i found google ads. and everything changed.

## the pet brand origin

the fourth brand was a pet accessories company. dog harnesses and dog beds, initially targeting the uk market. i'd been studying google ads for a few months, consuming everything i could find, and decided to run the new brand on google only. no meta. google shopping and search, full focus.

the decision felt risky at the time. meta was what every ecom course taught. google felt unfamiliar. but the alternative was running the same meta playbook that had already burned three brands and hoping for a different outcome.

within 30 days of launching, the google campaigns were profitable. not "promising" profitable. actually profitable. 4.2x ROAS at modest spend.

within 90 days, revenue was at £40k/month.

by the end of year one, the brand had done **$1M in revenue.** i was 19.

## why google was the right choice for a pet brand

the first thing i noticed about google compared to meta was intent.

on meta, i was interrupting people. someone scrolling their feed, seeing a dog harness ad, occasionally stopping because the creative was good enough. the conversion rate was always a function of creative quality. if the hook didn't land, nothing converted.

on google, someone was searching "best dog harness for pulling." they wanted a dog harness. they were actively looking. the only question was whether my product page could earn the click and the sale.

the conversion rate difference was immediate and significant. google shopping traffic converting at 6-8% vs meta cold traffic converting at 1-2%. same product. same price point. completely different buyer readiness.

the second thing i noticed was **stability.** on meta, i'd check performance 8-10 times a day because things could change dramatically within hours. on google, i checked once a day and the numbers were remarkably consistent. quality scores didn't evaporate overnight. the shopping algorithm didn't have a mood. impressions held steady week over week once the campaigns were established.

the third thing was **no creative fatigue.** the pet brand ran with the same core shopping feed and the same product images for months at a time. i wasn't churning out new ugc every two weeks. i was optimizing the feed, mining search terms, adding negatives. actual strategic work instead of creative survival.

## the setup that worked

the campaign architecture that drove the first $1M was not sophisticated. it was disciplined execution of the fundamentals.

**google merchant center feed first.** the first thing i did before launching a single campaign was optimize the product titles. dog harness titles became: "no-pull dog harness for large breeds - padded, escape-proof - adjustable - black." dog bed titles became: "orthopedic dog bed for joint pain - washable cover - non-slip base - medium/large." these titles matched exactly what dog owners searched for and the query-to-title match drove quality scores from the start.

**tiered shopping campaigns.** hero products (the harnesses, which had the highest margin and best reviews) got their own dedicated campaign with 60% of the budget. growth products got 25%. everything else got 15%. this prevented the algorithm from defaulting to cheaper, lower-margin products.

**geographic bid adjustments at city level.** london converted at 2.8% while manchester converted at 4.1% and birmingham at 3.6%. once i found this in the data (around week 6), i bid up Manchester and Birmingham significantly and bid down London. same budget, better allocation, better results.

**breed-specific keyword clusters in search.** "harness for german shepherd," "harness for labrador that pulls," "dog bed for golden retriever hip pain." these breed-specific terms converted at roughly 2x the generic "dog harness" terms because the message specificity made the buyer feel the product was designed for their specific situation.

## the problems that almost broke it

i'm not going to pretend the first year was smooth. there were three significant issues that cost real money and took time to fix.

**no conversion tracking for the first 6 weeks.** i'd set up the google ads conversion tag but made a mistake in the implementation. the campaigns were running on what i thought was real data. it wasn't. when i finally audited the tracking properly, my actual ROAS was 40% lower than what the dashboard showed. i made bidding decisions based on bad data for six weeks. rebuilding from that set me back a month.

the lesson: conversion tracking is the first thing you check. not campaigns, not bids. tracking.

**scaling too fast in month 4.** the brand had a great month 3 (£65k revenue). i doubled the budget immediately. the algorithm hadn't had time to learn the new budget level and performance deteriorated. i should have increased 20% per week rather than jumping 100% overnight. the aggressive scale cost me 3 weeks of mediocre performance.

**a VAT law change that killed the business.** after 12 months and $1M in revenue, uk tax law changed in a way that made the brand economics unworkable for my specific business structure. it ended the operation. this was outside my control and the reason i now tell every client: build your google foundation alongside meta, don't put 100% dependency on any single channel including google.

that lesson is the most valuable one from the whole experience. google is more stable than meta. but no channel is permanent. build the ecosystem.

## what i'd do differently

if i were launching the pet brand today with everything i know now:

launch bing simultaneously with google from day one. takes 2 hours to import. the pet accessories audience is older and desktop-heavy, which maps perfectly to bing's demographics. that's 15-20% more revenue with almost zero additional setup work.

build one advertorial for the top-converting search cluster within the first 30 days. "why does my dog pull on the leash and how to stop it" as a tof advertorial driving to the no-pull harness collection would have unlocked a significant new traffic layer and organic ranking over time.

set up the post-purchase sequence in month 1 not month 8. the cross-sell from harness to bed, from bed to joint supplement, from harness to training treats. every customer we acquired in the first 6 months could have had a 20-30% higher LTV if we'd had upsells in place from the start.

run google from day 1 alongside meta, not as a replacement for it. the airbag strategy. the VAT law wouldn't have killed us if google was 40% of revenue and meta was 40% and email was 20%. we'd have pivoted, rebuilt, survived. at 100% google, when google became unworkable, the business was done.

but $1M at 19 teaching me all of those lessons? i'd take that trade every time.

## the compounding difference between meta and google

looking back now with 7 years of experience and [$20M+](https://x.com/search?q=%2420M%2B&src=cashtag_click) generated for other brands, the pet brand experience crystallized one insight that shapes everything i do at ad imperium.

meta is a rented audience. every campaign is a fresh fight. the algorithm doesn't remember yesterday. quality scores don't compound. the moment you stop paying, the moment a creative fatigues, the moment ios changes the tracking rules, you're starting from zero.

google is built infrastructure. the quality scores you earn in month 1 lower your CPCs in month 6. the negative keyword list you build in month 2 makes every future dollar more efficient. the remarketing audience you accumulate is yours and grows richer with every visitor. the GMC feed trust signals improve over time.

time working on a meta account makes it temporarily better. time working on a google account makes it permanently better. that's the structural difference and it's why the pet brand could run without me looking at it for days at a time while my meta brands required daily crisis management.

i've now applied the lessons from that first pet brand to dozens of ecom clients. the jewelry brand that hit [$1M+](https://x.com/search?q=%241M%2B&src=cashtag_click)/month. the health brand that did 21x ROAS in australia. the supplement brand from $28k to $94k/month. the pattern is the same every time: build the foundation correctly, maintain it with discipline, let quality scores and remarketing pools compound, and the channel rewards patience in a way that meta simply doesn't.

every 18-year-old and 19-year-old starting an ecom brand today is doing it on meta because that's what everyone teaches. the opportunity is in the channel that everyone is ignoring.

## what i tell every young founder now

when someone my age asks me where to start with ecom advertising, i tell them this:

meta is great for learning creative and consumer psychology. the feedback loop is fast, the testing is immediate, and understanding what makes people stop scrolling is genuinely valuable. spend 3-6 months learning that.

then build google alongside it, not instead of it.

the brands that scale past $100k/month are the ones running both. meta meta creates demand and tests creative fast. google captures that intent, converts it at a lower cpc, and keeps earning through branded search.

the pet brand that hit $1M taught me that google alone could work. the brands i've worked with since have taught me that google plus meta, built as one ecosystem, is the formula for sustainable 7 and 8-figure ecom.
