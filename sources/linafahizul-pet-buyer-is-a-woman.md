pet supplements is a [$2b+](https://x.com/search?q=%242b%2B&src=cashtag_click) market and most brands are still marketing like the buyer is a man who owns a german shepherd.

she's not.

she's a woman who calls her dog her son or her widdle baby. yes, you read that right.

and that single misread of who's actually buying is costing the category more revenue than anyone in it wants to calculate out loud because the women who call her dog her son is not a quirky edge case.

she is the market.

she is the person who researches joint supplements at midnight because her dog is limping slightly and she noticed before the vet did. she is the one who reads the ingredient list, then reads it again, then goes to reddit to find out whether anyone has had a bad experience with this brand.

she is the one who buys the probiotic and the omega and the calming chew and the hip and joint formula because she's not optimizing for the cheapest solution to a physical problem.

she is caring for a family member and she will spend accordingly, and she will be loyal beyond what any acquisition model predicted, and she will tell every person in her life what she uses and why, and she will leave a review that converts the next ten buyers. she is the exact customer every supplement brand claims to want.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HEq3npgbYAAiTGQ?format=jpg&name=large)

and most pet supplement brands are actively repelling her with marketing that wasn't built for how she thinks about any of this.

here's what the marketing looks like when it was built for the wrong buyer.

**the headline is about performance.** clinical efficacy. veterinarian-formulated. supports joint health and mobility. maximum strength. these are words that answer the question a different buyer is asking. the question of whether the product works in a technical, measurable, here-are-the-studies sense. and she cares about that too, she absolutely does, she is a comprehensive processor who will read every word on that page and cross-reference it against what she already knows, but that is not the first question she arrives with.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HEq5cakakAAmBUJ?format=png&name=large)

the first question she arrives with is not does this work

it's does this brand understand what this animal means to me.

because the woman who calls her dog her son is not purchasing a supplement, she's purchasing reassurance. she's purchasing the feeling that she's doing everything she can for someone (not something) who cannot advocate for themselves.

she's purchasing the reduction of a specific anxiety that lives in the background of her daily life, the one that says he's getting older and i don't know what's coming and i need to feel like i'm ahead of it.

the supplement is almost incidental to the emotional transaction she is trying to complete.

and a brand that markets to the physical outcome without acknowledging the emotional one is missing the entire purchase motivation and then wondering why their conversion rate is stuck.

the pet supplement brands that are actually breaking through right now are not the ones with the most clinical claims, they're the ones that opened their marketing with the thing she's actually feeling.

the 2am google search.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HEq31JQaUAAEOlr?format=jpg&name=large)

the moment she noticed he was slower on the stairs.

the guilt of not knowing sooner. the particular tenderness of watching her little boy get older and being unable to stop it but desperately wanting to slow it down. when a brand opens with that, she stops scrolling because someone finally described the experience she's been having. recognition gate, open. safety gate, starting to open.

and she is now a completely different quality of reader than she was three seconds ago.

the proof problem in this category is significant and almost nobody is addressing it correctly. the testimonials on most pet supplement pages are clinical.

before and after mobility scores.

percentage improvement in x.

vet-verified outcomes.

and those things matter and she will get to them. but they're not what converts her first. what converts her first is the woman who says "i don't know how to explain this except that he seems like himself again" and means it.

the testimonial that says "i bought this because i was scared and i stayed because it worked" and doesn't try to quantify what worked means. the before and after that shows not just the dog's mobility but the owner's face, the relief in it, the specific quality of joy that comes from watching something you love feel better. that's the proof she's looking for.

not because she's irrational or anti-science but because the purchase she's making is an emotional one and emotional purchases require emotional proof.

the clinical data validates what she already decided to believe.

the story is what made her believe it.

the double spend problem hits particularly hard in this category because of something specific to the female pet owner psychology that most brands have never thought about.

she's not just spending money on this supplement, she's also spending emotional energy managing the possibility that it might not work.

that she might do everything right and it still might not be enough.

that she'll buy the best thing she can find and watch him decline anyway... and a brand that doesn't acknowledge that fear, that just promises results without holding space for the complexity of what she's navigating, feels tone-deaf to her. not wrong. tone-deaf. and tone-deaf is enough to lose the sale to a competitor who understood that the anxiety underneath the purchase is as real as the purchase itself.

the category code is also almost universally wrong for her.

most pet supplement branding looks like it was designed by someone who was thinking about the product's function, not its buyer's emotional state.

clinical white packaging.

green for natural.

bold type for efficacy.

it reads like it belongs in a veterinary supply catalog, which makes sense if the buyer is a vet or a performance-oriented owner who wants to know exactly what's in it and why. it does not read like something the woman who has seventy-three photos of her dog on her phone and refers to herself as his mom would choose off a shelf.

the brands breaking through aesthetically are the ones that understood the category code was wrong and built something that felt like it belonged in the life she actually lives.

warm.

considered.

the kind of thing she would feel proud to have on her counter, not because it looks impressive but because it looks like it was made by people who get it.

the subscription dynamics in this category are particularly interesting because the female pet supplement buyer is actually an ideal subscription candidate if the model is built correctly. (let me know if you want me to do a breakdown on this) she's not subscribing to automate a commodity purchase, she's subscribing to stay ahead of something she's afraid of.

the subscription isn't convenience, it's vigilance made effortless. it's her saying i want to be the kind of person who never runs out of this because running out means a gap in his care and a gap in his care is not something she is willing to accept. that motivation is more durable than convenience.

it doesn't erode the way convenience-based subscriptions do when the cabinet fills up.

it reinforces itself every time she remembers why she started.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HEq54x1aUAAr4HR?format=jpg&name=large)

but, it requires the brand to understand that the motivation exists, which requires the brand to understand who she is, which requires the brand to have done the work of actually looking at its customer and seeing her instead of seeing a demographic filter

91% of women feel brands don't understand them. in a category where the primary buyer is a woman who has an emotional relationship with her pet that most brands have never even acknowledged, that number is probably conservative.

the pet supplement brand that figures out it is not selling joint health and mobility to a general pet owner but selling peace of mind and proof of love to a woman who considers this animal family will not just improve its conversion rate.

it will build the kind of customer relationship that the rest of the category is still trying to manufacture with discount codes and referral programs.

the market is $2 billion and growing.
