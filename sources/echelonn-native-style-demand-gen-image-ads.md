## II. Our Native-style Ads Playbook

But first, let’s get into the fundamentals of this format…

### How Image Ads Work

Before we get into the different creative angles, it's important to understand how image ads on Demand Gen work.

Every image ad has 3 core components (Image, headline, description) and your logo.

With Demand Gen, you’ll be building a pool of creative assets that Google's algorithm can mix and match.

Not individual ads.

What this means is:

You provide multiple versions of each. Google then tests different combinations and serves the assets most likely to drive a conversion for that specific user. Someone scrolling Discover might see one image with a particular headline. Another person browsing YouTube might see a completely different combination from the exact same campaign.

Think of it more like giving the algorithm a creative toolkit.

The more high-quality images, headlines, and descriptions you provide, the more opportunities Google has to find winning combinations across different users and placements.

We typically build multiple assets around different creative angles and let the algorithm determine which combinations perform best.

Here are some of the creative angles (with funnels) we've seen work well.

### 1\. Informational Angle

This angle is built around a problem your audience is dealing with or an outcome they're actively trying to reach.

The ad feels educational rather than promotional. The goal is to make the user feel like they're about to learn something useful.

![](https://rhetorical-garage-a1e.notion.site/image/attachment%3A32ac7895-3a19-4e65-8b7f-e7ab584960fb%3Aimage.png?table=block&id=371a60cf-8ed8-8016-8e0e-e4a04fac6745&spaceId=d9fb0601-20b1-4779-a1c6-d68592cdd197&width=640&userId=&cache=v2&imgBuildSrc=requestProxiedImageUrl)

#### Headlines

For informational ads, the headline's job is to create curiosity around the problem.

Some formats that work well:

Curiosity

"Doctors Explain The Hidden Reason Why Most Knee Pain Treatments Don't Work"

"Why Are More People Switching To This Sleep Solution?"

Problem-Solution

"A Smarter Alternative To Expensive Skincare Treatments"

"The Recovery Method Athletes Are Using Instead Of Ice Baths"

Contrarian

"Why Most Skincare Products Fail To Deliver Long-Term Results"

"The Common Sleep Advice That Might Be Making Things Worse"

Outcome-Focused

"Improve Recovery And Reduce Soreness In 10 Minutes A Day"

"How Thousands Are Improving Their Sleep Without Medication"

#### Images

The image should amplify curiosity and work together with the headline

Some ideas for you to test:

Problem Visuals

Someone visibly experiencing the problem

Relatable everyday situations

Emotional frustration

Transformation Visuals

Before vs after (This can be tricky, since these visuals may not comply with Google policies, particularly in health, beauty, and other sensitive categories. As a result, ads can be suspended or heavily restricted in reach. It's still worth testing, but be aware of the risk.)

Problem vs solution

Old way vs new way

Clinical / Editorial Visuals

Close-ups of the affected area

Scientific-looking imagery

Educational-style photography

Curiosity Visuals

Unusual devices

Unexpected behaviors

Visuals that make people stop and ask questions

##### Descriptions

The description should reinforce the promise and give enough context to earn the click.

A few formats:

Outcome Reinforcement: "Discover the approach helping people recover faster and feel better day to day."

Credibility: “Based on methods trusted by professionals and experts."

Curiosity Expansion: "There's a reason more people are moving away from traditional recovery approaches.”

Aspirational: "Built for people who want to stay active, mobile, and pain-free for longer.”

For informational image ads, we use either an advertorial or a quiz.

### 2\. Comparison Angle

This format takes multiple products in your category and ranks them, positioning your ad as a trusted recommendation.

![](https://rhetorical-garage-a1e.notion.site/image/attachment%3Af2891ec6-d290-49cc-9a74-595af1170111%3Aimage.png?table=block&id=371a60cf-8ed8-8046-ac4f-d31ae10d2265&spaceId=d9fb0601-20b1-4779-a1c6-d68592cdd197&width=740&userId=&cache=v2&imgBuildSrc=requestProxiedImageUrl)

#### Headlines

The goal is to make people curious about the rankings, comparison, or review.

Some formats that work well:

Best-Of Lists

"The 7 Best Recovery Tools For Active Adults"

"We Reviewed 42 Sleep Products. These Were The Clear Winners."

Expert Recommendations

"Physical Therapists Are Calling These 6 Massagers A Game-Changer For Mobility After 40"

"Experts Reviewed 12 Recovery Devices. Here Are Their Favorites."

Review Format

"We Tested 10 Popular Skincare Tools. Here's What Actually Works For Women Above 50."

"Which Recovery Device Is Actually Worth Buying?"

#### Images

The image should support the comparison while maintaining curiosity.

Editorial Product Layouts

Multiple products displayed together

Clean flat lays

Magazine-style photography

Lifestyle Comparison Images

Product being used naturally

Research or evaluation cues in the scene

Multiple options visible

Side-By-Side Comparisons

Two products shown together

Clear visual hierarchy

Subtle implication that one option stands out

#### Descriptions

The description should reinforce credibility and position the content as helpful recommendation.

Credibility: "We evaluated effectiveness, ease of use, and overall value across more than 500 pans to create this list."

Research-Based: "We put the category's most popular products side by side to see which ones are actually worth buying."

Curiosity Expansion: "One option consistently outperformed products that cost twice as much."

Authority: "Recommendations based on expert opinions and thousands of customer reviews."

#### Landing Page

For comparison ads, we send traffic to a third-party comparison page.
