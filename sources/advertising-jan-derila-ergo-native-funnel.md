## A Quick Note on Why This Brand Is Worth Studying

Derila Ergo is not a complicated product. It is an ergonomic memory foam pillow with a butterfly shape designed to keep your neck aligned while you sleep.

That's it. Just a pillow.

And yet this funnel is scaling at a volume that most supplement brands would envy. They are running hundreds of advertorial angles across Taboola and Outbrain. They have a real sales page with proper social proof, real customer reviews, real phone numbers in four countries.

his is a brand that has built a direct response machine around one SKU.

The lesson before we even start: you don't need a complicated product to build a high volume native funnel. What you need is a clear pain point, a strong mechanism, and a funnel that earns trust at every step.

Let's go through each stage.

## Stage 1: The Ad Creatives

They are running five ads for this campaign. Two headlines. Three image concepts. And the combination tells you everything about how they think.

**The two headlines:**

"The #1 Mistake Tinnitus Sufferers Make Every Night (I Did It for Years)"

"If Your Ears Ring, Stop Sleeping Like This Immediately"

Both are command style. Both imply the reader is currently doing something wrong. Both create a curiosity gap that only closes with a click.

**Notice what they are NOT doing**. They're not saying

"discover the pillow that changed my life."

They're not leading with the product or the solution at all. The headline is entirely **about the problem and the implied mistake**. The reader clicks to find out what they're doing wrong, not to buy a pillow.

This is how you write for native. Lead with the fear of missing information. The product comes later.

**The three image concepts:**

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HBsB3LJXwAEiCpn?format=png&name=large)

**Creative 1: The Banana Phone**

A bald man holds a bunch of bananas against his ear like a telephone. Eyes closed. Relaxed expression.

This is the boldest creative in the set and probably the highest volume one.

**Here's why it works:**

It stops the scroll because it makes no immediate sense. Your brain cannot process it and move on. It has to figure out what it's looking at. That involuntary pause is a micro engagement , and on native placements where you have about two seconds of attention, a micro engagement is everything.

It also carries a visual pun that only becomes clear after you've clicked: "ringing in the ears." A phone. Your ear. The joke lands in retrospect, which makes people feel clever for getting it.

And on a practical level: it looks nothing like an ad. It looks like something a friend sent you in a group chat.

On a page full of editorial content, this image reads as content too.

This is what the Nano Banana prompt philosophy looks like in a real, scaling campaign. Weird beats pretty. Confusion beats clarity. A man holding fruit to his ear beats a product lifestyle shot every single time on the open web.

**Creatives 2 and 3: The Suffering Woman**

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HBsCVymWEAARd5m?format=png&name=large)

Two variations of the same concept: a middle aged woman sitting on a bed or closeup to camera, hands pressed against the sides of her head, clearly in pain from the ringing.

These are the "safe" creatives in the set. They work by pure identification.

The target audience: people with tinnitus, often 45+. They see this image and recognize themselves. Relatable without requiring any cognitive work.

The close up version (hands pressed to temples, distressed expression) creates more emotional intensity.

**Creative 4: The Glowing Vortex**

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HBsCp1QXMAA-DKv?format=png&name=large)

A silhouetted man sits alone in a dark room. Above his head, a glowing golden spiral swirls in the air. A visual metaphor for the constant ringing.

This one is doing something different from the others. Instead of showing pain, it shows the experience of tinnitus. The unceasing, inescapable spiral that follows you everywhere, even into a quiet room at night.

Where the banana is confusing, this is arresting. Where the women creatives are relatable, this one is almost cinematic. It stops the scroll for a completely different reason and speaks to a different emotional state. Not "I am suffering" but "I cannot escape this."

**The testing logic**

Five ads. Two headlines. Three image approaches.

They are not guessing which one works. They are finding out at scale, quickly, and then pouring budget behind whatever combination generates the lowest CPC and highest CTR.

**This is the real lesson of the ad stage:** the best teams do not try to predict the winner. They build a set that covers enough psychological angles, launch everything, and let the data decide.

**What to steal:**

**Run at least three image concepts per campaign angle**, not one. Weird and relatable. Abstract and literal. Emotional and pattern interrupt. Let the platform tell you which reader you're actually talking to.

And if you're writing headlines for tinnitus, sleep, pain, or any health product: the "you're doing something wrong" frame ("stop doing X," "the #1 mistake") will almost always outperform the "here's something good" frame. **Loss aversion is stronger than gain motivation**. Every time.

## Stage 2: The Advertorial

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HBsDGjEWQAAYzz9?format=jpg&name=large)

This is where Derila's funnel really separates itself from average native campaigns.

Most brands send traffic straight to pdp.

Derila Ergo sends it to a full story-based advertorial first. This is not an accident. This is the entire strategy.

**The Hook**

The advertorial opens in first person, from the perspective of a tinnitus sufferer:

"If you've ever laid in bed with your heart racing, ears ringing, and the ceiling spinning like you're on a boat in a storm... You know the kind of helpless, bone deep panic I'm talking about."

No product mention. No brand name. No solution hinted at.

Just a feeling, described so specifically that anyone who has experienced it stops everything and keeps reading.

This is the most important sentence in the entire funnel. Not the headline on the sales page. Not the discount offer. This sentence. Because if the reader feels seen here, they will follow the story wherever it goes.

**The Failed Solutions Section**

"ENT visits. CT scans. Thousands on hearing aids I didn't need. White noise machines that only made the buzzing feel louder."

This section is doing something clever. It's pre handling every alternative the reader has already tried. It's saying: we know you've been down this road. We know nothing worked. We're not going to insult you by pretending this is just another thing to try.

It builds identification and trust at the same time. The reader isn't skeptical of this person. They are this person.

**The Mechanism**

This is where the advertorial shifts belief. Not just "here's a product that helps your tinnitus." Instead:

"What if your tinnitus and dizziness weren't caused by your ears... but by your neck?"

Cervicogenic tinnitus is a real, documented phenomenon. Neck tension and cervical spine misalignment can compress nerves connected to the inner ear, causing ringing and dizziness. There is published research on this.

But here's why it works so well as a mechanism in the funnel: it reframes the problem entirely.

The reader has spent years thinking about their ears. They've seen ENT doctors. They've had hearing tests. Nothing worked, because they were looking in the wrong place.

This mechanism tells them: you weren't failing. You were just given the wrong map.

And the moment someone accepts a new explanation for why a problem exists, they become open to a new category of solution. The pillow isn't "another thing to try." It's the first thing that addresses the actual root cause.

New mechanism. New belief. New reason to buy.

**The Social Proof**

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HBsDm6IXAAAX9I9?format=jpg&name=large)

The testimonials in this advertorial are well constructed. Here's what makes them work:

They confirm the mechanism, not just the product. Users don't just say "great pillow." They say things like "I didn't even realize how much neck tension was triggering it." This reinforces the belief shift the advertorial already created in the reader's mind.

They use specific timeframes. "After just a week." Not "after a while." A specific, believable, low commitment window.

They describe life after, not just symptom reduction. "I finally feel like myself again." That's an outcome, not a feature.

**The CTA**

The advertorial closes softly. Not "BUY NOW."

More like: you've already tried everything else, and none of it came with a 30 day guarantee. What do you have to lose?

The advertorial has done all the selling. The CTA just opens the door.

## Stage 3: The Sales Page

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HBsD1aBXIAAgZnp?format=jpg&name=large)

Here's where a lot of native funnels fall apart.

They build a great advertorial, drive a warm, curious, half convinced reader to the product page and then hit them with a generic ecom layout that kills all the momentum.

Derila Ergo's sales page is smarter than that.

**It continues the mechanism language**

The page opens with: "Your Pillow Is Causing Your Pain." Not "here are the features of our pillow." Not "buy now and save 70%."

It immediately speaks the same language as the advertorial. The reader who just came from that tinnitus story lands here and feels continuity. Same belief system. Same framing. The trust they built in the advertorial doesn't reset.

This is the most underrated thing good native funnels do. The transition from advertorial to product page feels seamless, not jarring.

**The before/after visual**

The page shows a split image: a normal pillow (labeled "Worn Out Pillows") with red pain signals on the sleeping person's spine. Next to it, the Derila Ergo with green signals showing alignment.

Simple. Visual. Immediate.

This is showing, not telling. You don't need to read a single word to understand the product's value proposition.

**The feature framing**

The features on this page are written as outcomes, not specs.

Not: "Memory foam construction." Instead: "Cloud Like Softness Every Night."

Not: "Hypoallergenic materials." Instead: "Anti-Allergen Layer For Clean Rest."

Every feature is translated into what it means for the reader's actual night of sleep. This is basic direct response copywriting done correctly.

**Social proof that's real**

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HBsEJzfWsAAv6Gs?format=png&name=large)

The reviews on this page are from verified customers with names, countries, and dates. They are not hyperbolic. "Incredibly comfortable." "Great neck support." "The most comfortable pillow I've ever had."

Short, believable, and real. 30,000+ satisfied customers cited with a real rating. This is proof of scale, not just quality.

**The risk reversal**

60 day return policy. Free shipping. 70% discount. Real phone numbers in the US, UK, Germany, and Australia.

This is a brand that is confident enough in their product to take all the risk away from the customer. And they make that confidence visible everywhere on the page.

The discount is permanently "70% off" with a countdown. Classic scarcity play, but it works because by the time someone lands here, they are already warm. They don't need much more convincing.

## The Full Funnel Logic

Step back and look at what this funnel is actually doing across all three stages:

The **ad** gets the right person to click by looking like content, not advertising.

The **advertorial** earns trust by describing the reader's experience better than they could describe it themselves, then shifts their belief about what's causing their problem.

The **sales page** speaks the same language as the advertorial, translates every feature into an outcome, removes every risk, and makes buying feel like the obvious next step.

Each stage does one job. No stage tries to do everything.

**This is why it scales. There's no wasted friction anywhere in the funnel.**

## What You Can Steal for Your Own Campaigns

**1\. The mechanism is the most important thing you're probably not writing.** Most advertorials go straight from "you have this problem" to "here's our product." Derila Ergo inserts a belief shift in between. A new explanation for why the problem exists. This makes your solution feel inevitable.

**2\. Your sales page should speak the same language as your advertorial.** Most brands treat the advertorial and the product page as two separate things. Derila Ergo treats them as one continuous conversation. The reader should feel like they never left the story.

**3\. Feature copy is killing your conversions.** "Memory foam" tells someone nothing. "Cloud like softness you'll feel from the first night" tells them exactly what they're buying. Every spec needs to be translated into a lived experience.

**4\. Real risk reversal at the product page level matters enormously.** By the time a native reader hits your product page, they've already been sold once. Don't lose them to hesitation. A visible, clear guarantee with real contact information removes the last barrier between curiosity and purchase.
