### The Opportunity Nobody's Talking About

Demand Gen image ads are static creatives running inside Google's Demand Gen campaign type. They show up in Google Discover - the feed that appears on every Android phone's home screen and in the Google app.

Here's why this matters:

Image ads are faster and cheaper to produce than video. You can test 20-30 concepts in the time it takes to produce one YouTube ad. That speed is the entire game - more tests means more winners, faster.

Google Discover is high-intent scroll behavior. Users are actively browsing content. They're in discovery mode. A native-looking image that hooks a pain point stops the scroll the same way a viral article does.

Almost nobody is doing this well. Most advertisers either don't run image ads at all, or they run branded product shots that get scrolled past instantly. The gap between "doing it" and "doing it right" is where the money is.

| Channel | Production Cost | Time to Test | Winner Identification |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| YouTube Ads (video) | High ($2K-$10K per creative) | 2-4 weeks | Slow - needs spend to read |
| Demand Gen Image | Low ($50-$200 per concept) | 2-3 days | Fast - CTR tells you immediately |

Most accounts at scale run BOTH - image ads to dominate Discover, video for YouTube. This playbook is the image half.

### Rule #1: Discover Only (Then Expand)

This is the single most important setting for image ads and the one that 90% of advertisers get wrong.

Initial campaign: Restrict placement to Discover ONLY.

The YouTube home feed and Gmail placements send cheap, low-intent impressions that don't convert for static creative. They'll inflate your impression count, tank your CTR, and make it impossible to read whether a creative is actually working.

### Before You Launch: The 4 Prerequisites

Image ads sit in the same place as video in our scaling process. Don't launch until all four gates are cleared:

1\. Foundation proven. Brand Search is profitable. Shopping or PMAX is stable and delivering baseline revenue. If your foundation campaigns aren't working, image ads will burn money just as fast.

2\. Tracking verified. Conversions are firing correctly. In-platform numbers reconcile with Shopify (within reasonable attribution windows). You need clean data to read results.

3\. Creative supply ready. You need a pipeline of native image concepts to test. Not 3 images - more like 15-20 initial concepts across different pain points and styles. This is a volume game.

4\. Advertorial page built. The page you'll send the click to must be ready before you launch. Native image → advertorial → product page. That's the funnel. No advertorial = no launch.

### Part 1: The Creative Framework

![](https://growwithvysta.notion.site/image/attachment%3Ac92c7f6c-6d2b-41a2-b3f8-b3739694d954%3Aimage.png?table=block&id=398b8763-6928-806c-9081-c97403cff126&spaceId=227850c6-e019-4dca-a0f1-51a2198638af&width=1410&userId=&cache=v2&imgBuildSrc=requestProxiedImageUrl)

Image ads live or die on the creative. It must read like organic content built around a pain point - never like a brand ad. Here are the five principles that make native image ads convert:

#### Principle 1: Lead With the Pain Point - Not the Product

Cold Discover users don't care about your product yet. They don't know it exists. They don't know they need it. What they DO know is the problem they're living with every day.

Build the creative around the pain, and let the advertorial introduce the product.

Examples of pain-point-first angles:

| Product Category | Pain Point Hook | Why It Works |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Gut health supplement | "Overnight Gut Cleanse" / parasites / bloating | Names the symptom the scroller feels right now |
| Pain relief device | "Side sleeper shoulder pain" | Names the exact sufferer - they self-identify |
| Skincare | "Why your moisturizer is making it worse" | Creates doubt about their current solution |
| Sleep aid | "Still waking up at 3am?" | Describes the exact experience |

The pattern: Name the problem the scroller already feels. The product comes later - on the advertorial.

#### Principle 2: Native and Clickbaity - It Shouldn't Look Like an Ad

Discover sits inside a content feed. Creative that looks like an ad gets scrolled. Creative that looks like a post, a meme, or a news story gets the click.

Three styles that consistently clear the 5% CTR bar:

News-headline style. Frame it like an article or announcement. Think "New study finds..." or "Doctors are now recommending..." The format signals "this is information" not "this is commerce."

Cartoon / illustrated. Hand-drawn, meme-style, or cartoon visuals read as content, not advertising. This is one of our highest-performing formats across multiple accounts. The illustrated style immediately signals "this is interesting content" rather than "someone is trying to sell me something."

Curiosity & clickbait. A hook that opens a loop the advertorial closes. "The one thing dermatologists won't tell you about..." - make them NEED to know more. The advertorial delivers the payoff.

#### Principle 3: No Brand Name, No Brand Logo - Split Test Organic Images as the Logo

This is the detail that separates amateurs from professionals.

A consistent brand logo signals "this is an ad from a company." It kills the native effect instantly. The user's brain categorizes it as advertising and scrolls past.

What NOT to do: Stamp your brand logo on every image. Put your brand name in the creative. Use consistent brand colors that scream "this is a corporate ad."

What TO do: The brand name should NOT appear anywhere in the ad creative itself. For the logo field in Google Ads (which shows as a small icon next to your ad in Discover), don't use the brand's actual logo. Instead, split test random organic-looking images as the "logo." Think nature photos, abstract patterns, generic icons - anything that makes the ad look like it came from a content publisher rather than a brand.

This one change alone can increase CTR by 30-50% because it removes the last visual signal that tells the user "this is an ad."

#### Principle 4: Build 1:1 and 1.91:1 - Keep Text Under 20%

Two aspect ratios do the work on Discover: square (1:1) and horizontal (1.91:1). They claim the best real estate and crop cleanly on mobile.

| Ratio | Format | Why |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1:1 | Square | Dominates mobile Discover feed |
| 1.91:1 | Horizontal | Takes premium placements, reads like a news article thumbnail |

Text must stay under 20% of the image area. Heavy text reads as an ad and gets penalized by Google. One short overlay line maximum. Let the image do the work and let the headline field carry the copy.

#### Principle 5: The Click MUST Land on an Advertorial

![](https://growwithvysta.notion.site/image/attachment%3Aaec178b3-c65b-4f95-821b-4d2a0537484b%3Aimage.png?table=block&id=398b8763-6928-80c1-99ee-da70f3e66ddd&spaceId=227850c6-e019-4dca-a0f1-51a2198638af&width=1410&userId=&cache=v2&imgBuildSrc=requestProxiedImageUrl)

A native, clickbaity image promises a story. The destination has to deliver one.

The funnel:

Native Image (pain-point hook, earns the click) → Advertorial (tells the story, builds belief, then pitches) → Product Page / Offer (the warmed-up visitor converts)

Never send a native image click to a product page or homepage. The user clicked because they wanted to learn about their problem. If they land on a product page, they bounce. The advertorial bridges the gap - it educates, builds trust, and then introduces the product as the solution.

Every single winning image campaign we've ever run points at an advertorial. No exceptions.

### Part 2: The Testing Method

#### Bidding: Start on Maximize Conversions - No Target

Launch on Maximize Conversions with the target OFF. No tCPA. No tROAS. Nothing.

A target on a cold, testing image campaign throttles delivery before it can find the audience. The algorithm needs room to spend into Discover and surface the right users. A tCPA cap on day one starves the test - you never find the winner because you never got enough data.

Start here: Maximize Conversions, no target. This is where you start AND stay while scaling on Discover.

Don't do this: Adding tCPA/tROAS too early. It caps delivery on cold traffic, starves the test, and you never find the winner.

#### The 5% CTR Bar

![](https://growwithvysta.notion.site/image/attachment%3Aeeb956e6-731e-4912-9c60-60f417c4d5b8%3Aimage.png?table=block&id=398b8763-6928-80cb-87a6-e0869d5254aa&spaceId=227850c6-e019-4dca-a0f1-51a2198638af&width=1410&userId=&cache=v2&imgBuildSrc=requestProxiedImageUrl)

CTR is the fastest read on whether the creative resonates. On Discover, a strong native image clears approximately 5% CTR. Below that, the creative isn't working.

This is not a soft guideline. It's a hard bar:

| CTR | Verdict | Action |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 7%+ | Strong winner | Isolate and scale immediately |
| 5-7% | Winner | Isolate, scale, iterate on the angle |
| 3-5% | Mediocre | Iterate the creative - the angle might work but the execution needs improvement |
| Under 3% | Dead | Kill it. Don't waste another dollar. The creative isn't resonating. |

For context: standard branded image ads or product shots typically get ~1% CTR on Discover. Native-style ads that follow this framework consistently clear 5-8%+. That's a 5-8x improvement from the same placement, same audience, same budget - just better creative.

#### The 4-Step Workflow

![](https://growwithvysta.notion.site/image/attachment%3A63ff2d1c-8eab-4c17-ad6b-f750fdf89d3e%3Aimage.png?table=block&id=398b8763-6928-80b2-b0dc-dc64534a57e6&spaceId=227850c6-e019-4dca-a0f1-51a2198638af&width=1410&userId=&cache=v2&imgBuildSrc=requestProxiedImageUrl)

Step 1: Batch native concepts. Load 15-20+ pain-point / native images into Maximize Conversions test campaigns on Discover. Different angles, different styles (cartoon, news-headline, curiosity), different pain points for the same product.

Step 2: Read CTR fast. CTR reads in days, not weeks. Within 48-72 hours you'll know which creatives are clearing the bar. Kill anything under 5% - the creative isn't it. Don't blame the audience, don't wait longer, don't hope it improves. It won't.

Step 3: Isolate the winner. Once you identify the top performers, isolate them. Turn off the lower-performing ads in the ad group so budget concentrates on the winners. The goal is to get down to 1-3 proven ads per ad group.

Step 4: Scale and refresh. Push budget on the winners while new-customer CAC holds. But know this: native images fatigue FAST. Much faster than video. Your next batch of creative iterations must be testing before the current winner fades. Always have a pipeline.

### Part 3: Scaling Logic

Once a creative clears 5% CTR and you've isolated it into a focused campaign, the scaling mechanics kick in:

1\. Reward / punish on CAC. Step budget up while new-customer CAC holds. The moment CAC breaks your threshold, pull back. Don't wait for it to "recover." React same-day.

2\. Daily 10-minute routine. Every morning: check pacing → check CAC trend → check creative fatigue → make 1-2 micro-moves. Flag CAC swings to the team early. Don't let a bad day become a bad week.

3\. Feed the pipeline. Native images fatigue faster than any other format. A winner today might be dead in 7-10 days. The next batch must ALWAYS be in testing before the current winner fades. If you're not constantly feeding new creative iterations, you'll hit a wall.

The expansion play (after Discover is proven):

Once you have winners consistently clearing 5% CTR on Discover with profitable CAC, you can expand:

Launch a SEPARATE campaign targeting YouTube In-Feed on tCPA (using your proven CAC as the target)

Launch a SEPARATE campaign targeting Gmail on tCPA

Never mix these placements into your Discover campaign - they need their own bid strategy and will behave differently

### Part 4: How to Measure It (This Is Where Most People Get It Wrong)

Do NOT judge Demand Gen image ads on in-platform ROAS.

Demand Gen under-reports. It converts on a delay. The attribution window doesn't capture the full picture. If you're looking at in-platform ROAS and making decisions based on that number, you're going to kill winners and scale losers.

The measurement stack:

| Metric | Tool | What It Tells You |
| --- | --- | --- |
| CTR (≥5%) | Google Ads | Does the creative resonate? The fast, daily read. |
| New-Customer CAC | Triple Whale / Northbeam / other 3P | Is it actually acquiring new customers at a profitable cost? |
| NC ROAS | Triple Whale / Northbeam / other 3P | What's the true return on new customer acquisition? |
| Top-line revenue | Shopify | Is total revenue actually going up? Are you adding NET NEW customer revenue? |
| Blended lift | Shopify + 3P | Did account-level new-customer revenue actually move when you scaled? |

The critical check: Look at top-line revenue in Shopify. Is it going up as you scale Demand Gen? If you're spending more but Shopify revenue isn't moving, you're not adding net new customers - you're just cannibalizing other channels or retargeting existing customers. The Shopify number is the source of truth.

### Part 5: Common Disapprovals and How to Fix Them

Google Discover is strict on policy. Here are the three most common disapprovals and how to handle them:

| Disapproval | Trigger | Fix |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Misleading claims | "Guaranteed results," "Lose 10 lbs," absolute promises | Soften to outcome language. Let the advertorial carry the proof, not the image. |
| Before / after | Any visual comparison showing transformation | Blur or drop the "before." Focus on the after or the mechanism. Discover is stricter than other placements on this. |
| Too much text | Image looks like a flyer, not content | Cut to one short overlay line. Keep text under 20%. Let the headline field do the heavy lifting. |

Important: Fix the asset and re-upload, THEN appeal. Don't just hit "appeal" on a broken creative - Google will reject the appeal and flag your account for repeated violations.
