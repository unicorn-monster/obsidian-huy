A brand doing $50k/month through their quiz funnel (just quiz, not total revenue). Fine, not great. Traffic split three ways: product page, a "7 Reasons Why" advertorial, the quiz. PDP & 7 Reasons Why were doing about 3x the quiz revenue across both. The quiz had not been iterated in a few months, but it had a solid outline: light intro question → future-pacing → specific problem questions → twist the knife → profile + offer.

Baseline: **14% start rate, 12% completion rate, 5.4% purchase after completion.**

![[iterate-01.png]]

So here's exactly what we did & the effect each had:

## 1. Congruent Opening
We A/B tested the front of the funnel — even tested killing the first question entirely. Before, they just had the question & answers. So we changed the framing. Here's why: their top ads were already 2-3 minutes long, super informative. By the time someone clicked, they already knew the "potential problem." So the quiz opened with: "Let's see if this applies to you, and if it does, we'll help you find the right solution." The framing was a follow-up from the problem the ad explained, and how this quiz helped so many others like them achieve a specific outcome.

Start rate picked up a lot. And once people started, almost nobody dropped.

## 2. Completion Doesn't Equal Sales
Quiz was sticky now, but completion-to-conversion stayed about the same. The problem: 11 questions straight, then the interesting stuff at the end. People were zoning out — going through it, but not actually being sold. Since this is women, we added social proof early: big headline + before/afters after question 3. Then a problem mechanism — we already had a solution mechanism at the end, so we added the problem side right after their deepest question: a strong image and a little graph showing the real mechanism — how bad it actually was.

That alone made a big impact. Completion-to-purchase finally bumped.

![[iterate-02.png]]

## 3. Positioning AOV & Retention
Now we wanted to punch up AOV — get them to buy more immediately. We changed the end graph, made it timeline-focused: where you are today vs where you can be, anchored with today's date and 4 months out (before it was just "where you are now vs where you will be" — no date anchor). They already had the bundle on the product page. So: "you're the perfect fit — based on results from women like you, you can get there in 4 months. But you've gotta stay consistent. This is why you're the perfect fit for our buy 3 get 1 free." Custom UTM auto-selects the 3-pack + free gift when they get to checkout. The checkout also had a discount + code in it, so we added this to the loading screen too: "Applying discount code: [code]." Makes it feel exclusive, like they earned it by finishing the quiz. Plus they're shown why they're the perfect fit & how they specifically should stick for 90 days ("here's your specific 9-day deal because you're such a good fit").

**AOV jumped $16 on average** ($58.36 → $74.86).

![[iterate-03.png]]

As a result of these changes, the quiz became a clear hero PDP. The ads aligned more clearly with the reframing of the quiz. More spend went to the quiz & more spend went into the ad account as a whole.

These changes can apply to almost any sort of landing page — listicle, advertorial, quiz, whatever. The principles remain the same: congruent to ads, build trust & inform them, give them a reason why you're offering it to them.
