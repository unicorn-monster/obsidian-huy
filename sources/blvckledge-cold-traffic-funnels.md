# i - informational search → advertorial

informational searches are keywords tied to a problem your product solves.

the people searching here are problem-aware.

they know something's off, or they want something to improve, but they don't know your product can help yet.

examples:

\- "how to lose weight" - "what causes bloating" - "best way to get more protein" - "natural ways to lower blood pressure" - "how to improve gut health"

these queries carry 5 to 10x the search volume of bottom-funnel terms and cost a fraction of the cpc.

we're helping clients target keywords in the 50k to 250k monthly search range, paying under $0.50 per click.

here's how to find them.

start by listing 10+ pain points, problems, or outcomes your product addresses.

say you're selling magnesium. the informational keywords you'd go after might include:

"low energy" "improve sleep quality naturally" "what causes leg cramps at night" "what helps with chronic stress"

each one of those is a different angle the market is already searching for every single day.

to surface them, go to your search term report, filter for "contains," and drop in identifiers like:

\- "how to" - "best" - "tips" - "guide" - "benefits" - "side effects" - "what is" - "when to"

you'll pull up relevant terms you can build around.

then take those to keyword planner to validate volume and expand your list further.

small thing that’s worth checking first:

some keywords are seasonal

so before you build a strategy around them, check the seasonality in google trends first

once you have a solid list, build a search campaign and send the traffic to advertorials/listicles around the chosen topic.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HIrHIMjaEAAoW6o?format=jpg&name=large)

important note here.

the reader landed on your page because they searched for information.

so it has to inform. if the readers don't find your content useful, they won't stick around.

aim for 70–90% educational, 10–30% product

walk through the problem, explain what causes it, introduce your product naturally like a recommendation.

then show how it addresses the problem and explain the benefits.

now, this funnel works best when the website has a solid seo foundation in place.

from our experience…

without SEO, you’ll often see way fewer impressions, lower click volume, and higher CPCs.

# ii - demand gen image ads → advertorial

one of our biggest discoveries this year have been demand gen image ads

they mostly run through google discover and youtube in-feed placements

for those who don’t know…

in-feed is those thumbnail & headline ads you see inside the organic youtube browse feed and search results

discover is google's content feed, where people scroll through articles and news.

it shows up when someone opens the google app or lands on a new tab in chrome mobile

unlike search or shopping, the users are just going through content.

and you have to catch their attention mid scroll (similar to meta).

usually the image is what gets them to pause for a second, then they read the headline, and decide if it’s worth clicking

the intent is close to informational search, you’re hitting problem-aware audiences here

so the ad has to lead with a problem or outcome they already care about

something like: "doctors explain the hidden reason why knee pain treatments don't work."

on the creative side, the visual needs to reinforce that:

a few directions that work well here:

\- a person visibly experiencing the problem in a relatable, everyday context. - a split scene showing the before and after states. - a close-up of the affected area or body part, shot in a way that feels clinical or editorial.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HIrHPIrbUAA3OW_?format=jpg&name=large)

the whole point is to grab attention and build just enough curiosity to earn the click

just make sure you don’t go too aggressive with the creatives.

google can flag them as clickbait or personalized hardship, which leads to the ads getting limited or disapproved.

from there, you send them to an advertorial built around that same angle

the easiest place to find angles for this is your informational search campaigns.

if a campaign is already converting there, the demand is validated.

take it into demand gen image ads and build the advertorial around the same hook.

# iii - ai youtube ads to vsl/sales page

this year I've been seeing a big rise in AI ads across ecom.

and from what I’m noticing inside client accounts and other big brands…

they’re working in pretty much every vertical you can think of.

some of the best performing formats i’m seeing in ad accounts are

**1\. vsl**: a credible voice walks through the problem like they're having a conversation with the viewer.

**2\. podcast style**: framed as a two-person conversation. one person asks questions, the other answers. usually the "host" starts skeptical with a question/objection and gets convinced over the course of the ad.

**3\. animation**: use a voiceover and illustrated visuals to walk the viewer through a concept. each point in the script is paired with a visual that shows it happening

even though the format is different, they usually follow the same direct response structure:

\> open with a bold, contrarian statement about their main problem (“the real cause of x isn’t y”)

\> educate on the hidden reason that problem exists

\> agitate the pain + quantify the stakes with a stat or worst-case scenario

\> explain why typical solutions don't work (and what's missing)

\> introduce your mechanism/ingredient as the missing piece & show how your product works

\> hint at a specific, believable outcome

\> stack social proof throughout the video (research, before/afters, testimonials, etc)

\> soft CTA with zero friction ("tap below, no email needed")

these often pair well with a vsl/sales page on the back end.

the video handles the education and trust-building. the page closes.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HIrHzSQbUAAsWmy?format=jpg&name=large)

the page needs to continue the same narrative the video started, so there's no drop in momentum when the viewer clicks through.

# iv - demand gen image ads to comparison page

now, the 3 funnels i mentioned above worked best when your product solves a specific problem

which is harder to pull off in certain industries

this last funnel is a lot flexible, and i think every brand could implement it

instead of anchoring the ad around a problem, you frame it as a “review” blog

traffic goes to a comparison page, written to read like a third-party breakdown of the top products in your category.

think "top 7 magnesium supplements we tested in 2025" or "best collagen powders: ranked by results."

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HIrHZAibIAAA0R6?format=jpg&name=large)

this format mirrors how buyers research before purchasing

the page walks them through the choices, what matters, where each one wins or loses, and then points to the best option

for the headline, lean into the listicle framing:

\- "6 supplements physical therapists recommend" - "top 3 picks, ranked by results" - "we tested 12 collagen powders. here's what we found"\\

for the image, the goal is to add curiosity and draw the viewer in rather than revealing the conclusion.

a few directions for this:

\- a clean editorial flat lay of the products from the category - a lifestyle scene where a person is using one of the products in a natural context, with subtle visual cues that suggest comparison or research, like a notepad nearby or multiple products visible in the background. - a split-panel layout showing two products side by side with a clear visual hierarchy that implies one is the preferred choice, without explicitly saying so in the image.

this funnel works for almost any brand regardless of product category. you just need a category and a credible angle to frame the list around.
