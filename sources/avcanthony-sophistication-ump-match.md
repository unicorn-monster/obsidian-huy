This One Thing Determines If Your Mechanisms Scale

So one of our members from GTE dropped this banger of a post a while back called "Ultimate Definition of Angle", and I find myself constantly coming back to it, and I have to share another golden insight I picked up on it that will be one of the most important things you read, and it lead to a HUGE breakthrough on a new store :)

As we all know, the people we sell to have limiting beliefs that prevent them from buying.

And our UMP, unique mechanism of the problem, is the new information we give to the audience, that makes them see their problem as uniquely solvable by our product.

However, the type of limiting belief that you have to shift, depends on the market sophistication of your audience.

See how he broke it down:

Stage 1 Markets (Unsophisticated)

The available belief to shift: "This problem can be solved"

• Angle: Simply claiming a solution exists

Stage 2-3 Markets (Developing Sophistication)

The available belief to shift: "There's a better/easier/faster way"

• Angle: Mechanism differentiation

Stage 4-5 Markets (Sophisticated)

The available belief to shift: "The real problem isn't what you think"

• Angle: Problem redefinition

Stage 5+ Markets (Hypersophisticated)

The available belief to shift: "Everything else failed because of this hidden factor"

• Angle: Root cause revelation

This is SO important because a mismatch between the UMP and the stage of sophistication, doesn't actually cause the necessary belief shift to make someone buy.

This leads to times where you think your mechanism is strong, and it might be logically sound, but nobody is buying.

Let's see an example with this dog nail trimming product.

You'll see how on THIS landing page, there is no mention of "an underlying cause".

They don't say things like, "This is the REAL reason you can't trim your dog's nails."

Why?

Because at this stage of sophistication, (stage 2-3), the market hasn't been exposed to other new products who have claimed the same thing.

The only way to trim your dog's nails has been nail clippers. That's it.

And this audience isn't constantly being bombarded by new products using similar claims. They just know nail clippers.

So because of that, all we need to do with our product is show, "hey, there's a better way to do this, and here's why it's better"

So if we talk to this audience and say something like,

"Here's the REAL reason you can't trim your dog's nails" (which is how I think most people here are writing UMPs) or "The problem with trimming your dog's nails isn't X, it's Y"

It falls flat.

It also feels forced, and doesn't really make sense.

Because here's the thing, this audience CAN trim their dog's nails just fine with clippers. It's just a pain in the ass and has it's own issues that make it difficult to solve the problem.

Now, let's contrast this with a stage 4-5 belief shift market, like menopausal supplements.

You'll see language like "Here's what most doctor's get wrong. It's not a "pinched nerve", it's ligament degradation." They are redefining the problem entirely.

Why?

Because this audience has heard so many claims from so many products. You can't just say "here's a better way to solve your problem" because they've already HEARD that from the last product and got burned.

Like imagine if this LP just said, "Our supplement is the best way to solve sciatica!"

🙄

This level of sophistication is where it makes sense to redefine the problem.

So as you can see, it's SO crucial for you to understand the sophistication level of your audience.

And to correctly identify what the necessary belief shit is for your level of sophistication.

Because writing strong mechanisms isn't a one size fits all.

It's about leveraging knowledge gaps to create the necessary belief shift for buying.

But you have to make sure that the knowledge gap matches the sophistication of your audience.

Because you can have the most scientific mechanism in the world.

But if you're not actually shifting the belief that matters for that specific sophistication, no body will buy.

So the next time you go to analyze your losing copy, put yourself in the Avatar's shoes and think: is this mechanism the bridging the correct knowledge gap for my audience's sophistication level? Does it make sense? Or am I just using whatever sounds good to me?
