Summary (paraphrased). The article distills a framework originally posted by Alen Sultanic on Facebook into a step-by-step quiz-question structure the author says applies across categories (weight loss, neuropathy, beauty, supplements, info products).

![[hidden-system-01.png]]

**Core diagnosis of why most quiz funnels underperform:** most quizzes only ask *identifier* questions (demographics, preference-collection with "zero function") — they identify a customer, but don't create one. Because there's no functional purpose to the questions, buying intent actually drops the deeper someone goes into the quiz, which is why so many underpowered quiz funnels end in a "book a call" CTA instead of a direct purchase — the economics only work with a low-volume, high-touch close.

**The state-transition model:** ads put someone into an "information-seeking" state; to buy, they need to reach a "solution" state; but the only route from information-seeking to solution is THROUGH a "problem" state — you can't skip it. Most quiz funnels try to jump straight from information-seeking to the pitch, skipping the problem-state step entirely.

![[hidden-system-02.png]]

**The fix — "functional questions":** instead of neutral fact-collecting questions ("How old are you?"), phrase the question/answer pair in first-person, identity-toned language ("This year, I'm... [between X-Y and I feel ___]") so that agreeing to an answer is also an act of self-description, not just data entry.

**The 4-stage transformational arc:** (1) capture in information-seeking state via ads; (2) move into a problem state using functional questions; (3) move into a solution state via the results page; (4) pitch the offer. This is executed through 3 question phases, roughly a third of the quiz each:

- **Phase 1 — Positive/aspirational questions** ("I want to...", "I dream of..."): the visitor arrived feeling bad, so opening on aspiration first makes them feel good before anything gets heavier — sets an emotional baseline to shift away from.
- **Phase 2 — Neutral/behavioral questions** ("I tend to...", "Sometimes I..."): non-accusatory observations about current habits, gradually making the quiz feel more personal without confrontation yet.
- **Phase 3 — Negative/identity questions** ("I am..." framing): the article argues "I am" phrasing produces negative *ownership* of the problem, not just acknowledgment — the idea being that someone has to accept they have a problem before they'll accept they need help, and whoever positioned the acceptance is positioned as the helper. Suggested optional additions here: a future-pacing fear question (what happens if they never fix it) for maximum emotional weight, followed by an optional "pressure-release" question offering a menu of desired outcomes to restore a feeling of choice right after the heaviest section (and optionally branching each choice to its own personalized result).

![[hidden-system-03.png]]

**Results page structure suggested:** (1) thank/comfort line normalizing their situation ("you're not alone in this"); (2) the "bad news" — reveal the underlying mechanism causing their problem, framed so it isn't their fault; (3) the "good news" — the mechanism behind why the offered solution addresses that specific root cause; (4) the pitch/CTA.

**Strategic framing for why this matters:** at any moment only a small slice of a market (author estimates 1-3%) is actively "ready to buy," and every competitor is fighting over that same slice. The pitch of this framework is that converting people who start in a problem-unaware or problem-avoidant state into a solution-ready state expands the addressable audience well beyond that ready-to-buy sliver — reframed as "stop identifying customers, start creating them."

**Implementation checklist given:** map the funnel, identify question/capability gaps, write questions in the positive→neutral→negative sequence, build mobile-first (one question per screen, large tap targets), test the full flow yourself across multiple answer paths before launch, then iterate off real data.
