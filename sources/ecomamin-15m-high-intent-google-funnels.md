## the branded search layer: the most underrated campaign in any account

your branded search campaign is the most efficient campaign in your account.

it's capturing people who already know you and already intend to buy. the CPCs are low. the conversion rates are high. the ROAS looks great in any dashboard.

a lot of brands either don't run it properly or don't run it at all, which means every time someone searches your brand name, the top result is potentially a competitor who bid on your brand keywords.

yes, that happens. your competitors are bidding on your brand name right now.

if you're not running branded search, you're not just missing organic traffic protection. you're actively gifting warm, pre-qualified, ready-to-buy prospects to whoever bid on your keywords this morning.

**what a properly built branded search campaign looks like:**

- separate campaign from non-branded, with its own budget so non-branded spend can't eat into branded when budgets are tight
- exact match and phrase match on the brand name and all variations: brand name, brand name + product type, brand name + review, brand name + discount
- ad copy that reinforces the brand promise and drives to the most conversion-optimised destination, usually the homepage or a brand landing page rather than a specific PDP
- RLSA layers: people who've visited before get different bid adjustments than cold branded searchers. a previous visitor who searches your brand name in the same week is extremely close to buying. bid aggressively for that person.
- negative keywords to prevent branded campaigns from showing for generic category terms

branded search protection is not optional. it's the floor underneath every other campaign in the account.

## the conquest layer: stealing your competitors' most valuable traffic

conquest campaigns are the mirror image of branded protection.

instead of defending your brand name, you're bidding on your competitors' brand names.

the prospect who searches a competitor's brand name and lands on a well-built comparison page showing them why your product is a better choice is one of the most qualified prospects in your entire account. they have demonstrated purchase intent in the category. they're in evaluation mode. the comparison page does the selling.

**the setup:**

- separate conquest campaign, never mixed with non-branded generic keywords
- exact and phrase match on competitor brand names and their product-specific variants
- destination is a dedicated comparison page: your product vs their product, on features, price, ingredients, delivery, guarantee, social proof
- the comparison page wins by being honest. if a competitor is cheaper on one metric, acknowledge it and show where you win on the metrics that matter more. buyers trust honest comparisons. they distrust pages that show no weaknesses.

the brands i've built conquest campaigns for consistently see ROAS numbers in that campaign tier that look impossible until you understand the intent profile of the traffic hitting it.

## the non-branded search layer: the compounding engine

this is where the Meta-feeds-Google dynamic becomes concrete.

non-branded search campaigns target the queries that sit below brand awareness: problem-oriented searches, ingredient searches, category searches, comparison searches.

"best supplement for deep sleep"

"what causes poor sleep quality"

"magnesium vs melatonin for sleep"

"sleep supplement that actually works"

these are people in the MOF band: they know they have a problem, they're evaluating solution categories, they haven't decided on a brand yet. your Meta ads may have already introduced the brand. your Google search ad is the moment they come back with intent.

the critical piece most brands miss: these campaigns absolutely must not send traffic to a product page.

someone who searched "what causes poor sleep quality" has not decided to buy anything. they're looking for information. if your ad answers that question and leads to an advertorial that educates them on the mechanism, pre-sells the solution category, and then introduces the brand as the logical conclusion - that's a conversion funnel.

if your ad drops them on a product page asking them to spend £45 on a supplement they've never heard of, they bounce. you paid for that click. the sale went to the brand whose ad led to an advertorial.

**the non-branded search structure that works:**

- campaigns segmented by intent tier: problem-awareness keywords separate from category keywords separate from ingredient keywords
- each intent tier points to the landing page type that matches the intent: problem queries to advertorials, category/ingredient queries to comparison pages or listicles
- match types: broad match with a strong negative list, or phrase match with expansions tested carefully
- RLSA modifier: users who have previously visited the site get a bid uplift because they're already familiar with the brand. the combination of prior exposure and active search intent is extremely high converting.

## the Shopping layer: passive revenue that most brands are half-collecting

Shopping is the campaign type ecom brands rely on the most and invest the least in structuring properly.

a well-built Shopping campaign captures buyers who are in full commercial intent mode: they searched a product type, they can see your listing alongside competitors, they're making a purchase decision.

the conversion intent here is higher than almost any other traffic source in the account. which makes the neglect of Shopping infrastructure one of the most expensive mistakes in ecom.

**what separates a Shopping campaign that scales from one that flatlines:**

- product feed with titles built around converting non-branded keywords, not the brand name. google uses the title as the primary matching signal. if it starts with the brand name, you're invisible for discovery queries.
- custom labels that tier products by margin, bestseller status, and seasonal relevance. this lets bid strategies prioritise high-margin SKUs without manual intervention.
- segmented campaigns by product performance: your bestsellers in one campaign with more aggressive bids, new products in a separate campaign in a testing phase, low-margin or slow-moving products in a third with lower bids or excluded.
- shopping feed optimised for CTR as much as relevance: lifestyle images over white backgrounds where the category allows it, promotional prices and "free shipping" badges where applicable, star rating count high enough to display credibility signals

a Shopping campaign with this structure running alongside branded search, conquest, and non-branded search is compounding: every piece of awareness spend on any platform that generates brand or category familiarity eventually flows through one of these channels as high-intent demand.

here's the thing that most brands don't realise until they see it in the data.

your Meta ads are generating google searches.

someone sees your Instagram video. they don't click. they scroll. three days later, they're in a store, someone's using a product like yours, it triggers the memory. they pull out their phone and type your brand name into Google.

or they type "best \[product category\]" because the video reminded them they wanted to solve that problem. that's a non-branded search conversion.

or they type "\[your brand\] review" because the video interested them but they want third-party validation first.

all three of those searches are the direct product of your Meta spend. you paid for the attention that created the intent. if your Google setup captures the intent, you get the sale. if it doesn't, you've funded the market's awareness of a problem your competitors will close.

this is why brands scaling profitably at 8 figures rarely have a single advertising platform. the platforms feed each other. Meta generates the demand. Google captures it.

the inverse is also true: brands with strong organic presence or content distribution - YouTube, podcast sponsorships, influencer placements - see branded search volume grow organically as that distribution compounds. every piece of content that puts the brand in front of someone new eventually flows back through branded search when that person is ready to buy.

**the never-ending retargeting funnel:**

the practical implication of this is that your Google branded and high-intent search campaigns function as a permanent retargeting layer for everything you're running elsewhere.

you don't need to serve a retargeting ad to every person who saw your Meta ad and didn't click. a percentage of them are going to search your brand or category on Google at some point in the next 7-30 days. if you're there when they do, you close them at a fraction of the CPM you'd pay to retarget them on social.

this is why i always tell brands: fix the Google setup before you scale Meta. every pound you spend on Meta without the Google capture layer in place is partially subsidising your competitors' revenue.
