I have said it before, I am a very strong fan of listicles. They're easy to target to hyper specific angles. They're easy to build. Easy to read. Especially for beginners, it's an amazing simple way to get your message across. Listicles are essentially long form copy based product pages. And they don't just work in supplements.

So what is Hollow Socks?

![[hollow-01.jpeg]]

Hollow Socks is an alpaca fiber sock brand — their team is run by @zachmstuck, killer direct response brand. They currently have over 12,000+ ads live and have been scaling like crazy in one of the most boring markets... socks. How? Killer direct response and a lot we can learn from a **new mechanism + old market = $$$**.

![[hollow-02.png]]

They run very UGC-intensive video ads, but incredibly simple direct response copy. TikTok Shop "yapper" style: facebook.com/ads/library/?id=884703321212219 · https://www.facebook.com/ads/library/?id=1497380255314127

They're running hard on the compression angle right now, and their ad scripts tell you everything about their positioning. One opens with: "Most compression sock brands prioritize profit over compliance, not us." Then straight into the pain: stiff, itchy, painful, cheap synthetic materials, tight elastic bands digging into your legs, socks you can't wear for more than a few hours. Then the mechanism pivot: baby alpaca fiber, naturally soft, breathable, 15-20 mmHg graduated compression that actually works. Close: "You can't comply with compression therapy if the socks are unbearable."

![[hollow-03.png]]

That's the whole game right there. They're not selling socks — they're selling comfort. The product is the same (compression). The mechanism is new (alpaca).

So where is most of their traffic going? If I am not wrong, Hollow Socks did (or is close to) 100M annual run rate — and they're sending to simple 10-Reasons-Why-style listicle pages (no need to over complicate it). Here's a full scope of their best performing angles:

Their MAJOR point: we're targeting people who already have compression socks, but theirs suck — synthetic, cheap material. Ours is better because of Alpaca (mechanism).

This headline & table does 3 things at once: positions against the current solution (doctor's synthetic socks); creates a new category (alpaca compression); implies mass adoption ("America is ditching"). The reader doesn't need to be sold on compression — they already believe in it, their doctor told them to wear it. They just need to be convinced they picked the wrong KIND.

Sound familiar? Exact same play as Primal Viking using reindeer organs vs beef organs — you don't convince people organs are good (they already believe that), you convince them they picked the wrong source. Much cheaper to redirect existing belief than create new belief from scratch.

## Landing Page Techniques & Takeaways

**The Comparison Table Up Top.** Before they even start reading, there's a side-by-side comparison: Hollow Alpaca vs Regular Compression across temperature control, comfort, longevity, wearability. This anchors the entire page. You don't have to convince them compression works — you just have to convince them their current socks are the problem. Every reason that follows just reinforces what this table already told you.

![[hollow-07.png]]

**The Listicle Format (10 Reasons).** Each reason follows the same pattern: here's something you hate about your current compression socks → alpaca solves it. Competitive destruction disguised as education.

![[hollow-08.jpeg]]

A few examples of how each reason moves them: "Superior circulation support AND comfort" → you shouldn't have to choose, most compression socks make you pick one, Hollow gives you both. "Stays cool and breathable" → this is the #1 complaint about compression socks, alpaca thermoregulates naturally, goodbye sweaty legs. "Won't roll down or slip" → everyone who wears compression socks knows this pain, you're pulling them up every hour, Hollow's alpaca weave with elastic arch support stays put.

**The Awareness Progression.** Problem-aware → you already wear compression socks, but they're uncomfortable, you don't comply. Mechanism-aware → the issue isn't compression, it's the material — synthetics trap heat, lose their squeeze, feel like a tourniquet. Solution-aware → alpaca fiber fixes every gap synthetics create: soft, breathable, durable, odor-resistant. Product-aware → Hollow is the way to get alpaca compression socks. They never had to sell compression as a concept — they just redirected existing belief toward a new mechanism. Same awareness progression as Primal Viking's reindeer page.

## Urgency & Close

![[hollow-09.jpeg]]

"Buy 2 Get 2 Free." "Stock Alert: Running Low." "87% Sold." "Due to high demand and limited alpaca fiber supply, there is a very high risk of selling out." What's smart about this: the scarcity is tied to the mechanism. Peru has 4 million alpacas — that's it. The next closest country has one-tenth that number. So the urgency isn't "sale ends Sunday" — it's baked into the story itself. Classic scarcity close after the belief has been fully seeded. By this point the reader isn't deciding if they should try alpaca compression — they're deciding if they can afford to keep wearing synthetics.

Look at the testimonials they chose for this page — almost every single one mentions a specific medical condition: DVT, edema, neuropathy, poor circulation, post-surgery recovery.

![[hollow-10.jpeg]]

They're not selling socks — they're selling relief. The reviews do the heavy lifting the copy can't. Real people, real conditions, real outcomes. Selling to this audience is about building trust and giving them proof something will work. The wall of medical-specific reviews does that better than any copy could.

## The Product Page
This is where the listicle lands you. By now the belief is already built — alpaca compression > synthetic compression. So this page doesn't need to re-educate, it just needs to close.

## Why This Matters
Hollow Socks is a masterclass in what I call funnel arbitrage. They brought direct response landing page tactics — listicles, comparison tables, belief seeding, mechanism-first copy — to a market that was still selling socks on Amazon product listings and pharmacy shelves.
